## 1. Script Structure for Spoken Delivery

### 1.1 The 5-Part Video Script Framework

Every effective video script follows this structural arc:

1. **Cold Open / Hook** (first 3-5 seconds)
   - The single most critical moment — determines whether viewers stay or scroll
   - State the promise, reveal a surprising fact, or pose a compelling question
   - No intros, no logos, no "Hey guys" — get to the point immediately

2. **Context / Problem Setup** (next 10-20 seconds)
   - Why should the viewer care? What problem does this solve?
   - Establish stakes: what happens if they don't watch
   - Brief — just enough to set up the main content

3. **Main Content / Body** (bulk of the video)
   - 2-4 key points, each clearly delineated
   - Use the PAS framework (Problem-Agitate-Solution) or AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)
   - Each point follows: claim, evidence/example, takeaway
   - Insert retention hooks between sections (see Section 4)

4. **Climax / Key Revelation** (final teaching moment)
   - The biggest insight, the "aha" moment
   - This is what makes the video worth sharing
   - Position it at 70-80% of the way through to maintain watch time

5. **CTA / Outro** (final 10-15 seconds)
   - One clear call to action — subscribe, comment, click, try something
   - Keep it brief and specific
   - Optional: tease the next video to drive continued viewing

### 1.2 Structure Variations by Format

**Short-Form (15-60 seconds) — Reels, Shorts, TikTok:**
- Hook: 1-2 seconds (immediate pattern interrupt)
- Single point: 10-40 seconds
- CTA: 3-5 seconds
- No intro, no outro padding

**Mid-Form (3-10 minutes) — YouTube, educational:**
- Hook: 5-10 seconds
- Context: 15-30 seconds
- Body: 3-4 key sections with retention hooks between each
- CTA: 10-15 seconds

**Long-Form (10-30+ minutes) — Deep dives, tutorials:**
- Cold open: 15-30 seconds (preview the best moment)
- Intro + agenda: 30-60 seconds
- Body: 5-8 sections with chapter markers
- Mid-roll retention hook at 50% mark
- Conclusion + CTA: 30-60 seconds

### 1.3 Structural Framework Options

**PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution):**
- Problem: Name the pain clearly
- Agitate: Make it worse — show consequences of not fixing it
- Solution: Present your approach as the answer

**AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action):**
- Attention: Bold opening statement or visual
- Interest: Develop the story or idea
- Desire: Show what is possible
- Action: Tell them exactly what to do next

**Hook-Conflict-Resolution-Lesson:**
- Hook: Draw them in
- Conflict: Present the challenge or tension
- Resolution: Show how it was solved
- Lesson: What the viewer should take away

## 2. Pacing Guidelines

### 2.1 Words Per Minute for Spoken Delivery

| Delivery Style | Words/Minute | Use Case |
|----------------|-------------|----------|
| Slow / dramatic | 110-130 WPM | Emotional stories, serious topics |
| Conversational | 140-160 WPM | Standard YouTube, educational |
| Energetic / fast | 160-180 WPM | Shorts, high-energy content |
| Rapid-fire | 180-200+ WPM | Comedy, hype, trend content |

**Standard benchmark:** 150 words per minute of video.

**Length estimates:**
- 1-minute video: ~150 words
- 5-minute video: ~750 words
- 10-minute video: ~1,500 words
- 20-minute video: ~3,000 words

### 2.2 Pacing Rhythm

- **Hook delivery:** Slightly faster than normal pace — energy and momentum from frame one
- **Key points:** Slow down for emphasis when delivering the main insight
- **Transitions:** Speed up slightly to maintain momentum between sections
- **CTA:** Measured pace — clarity over speed

### 2.3 Variety and Pattern Interrupts

Add strategic changes every 15-20 seconds to maintain attention:

- **Visual changes:** Cut to B-roll, graphic, text overlay, or angle change
- **Vocal changes:** Shift in pace, volume, or emphasis
- **Format changes:** Switch from talking head to screen share, demo, or illustration
- **Rhetorical questions:** Pause to let the viewer process before answering
- **Direct address:** "Here's what most people get wrong..." — re-engages passive viewers

### 2.4 Short-Form Pacing (2026 Standards)

- Deliver the complete hook in 2-2.5 seconds
- 15-30 seconds: Ideal for quick tips and hooks
- 25-40 seconds: Mini tutorials
- 30-45 seconds: Story-style content with strong pacing
- Maximum: 60 seconds for most short-form platforms
- Cut all dead air — every second must earn its place

## 3. Visual Cue Notation

### 3.1 Standard Notation System

Use brackets for visual cues throughout the script. This makes it clear what the viewer sees versus what the speaker says.

**Notation format:**
```
[VISUAL CUE TYPE: Description]
```

**Common notation types:**

| Notation | Meaning | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| [B-ROLL: description] | Supplementary footage | [B-ROLL: Typing on laptop, coffee beside keyboard] |
| [GFX: description] | Graphic or animation | [GFX: Animated bar chart showing growth from 10K to 100K] |
| [TEXT: "text"] | On-screen text overlay | [TEXT: "Step 1: Define Your Audience"] |
| [SCREEN: description] | Screen recording or share | [SCREEN: Walk through dashboard settings] |
| [CUT TO: description] | Camera angle change | [CUT TO: Close-up of hands on keyboard] |
| [LOWER THIRD: text] | Name/title graphic | [LOWER THIRD: "[Name] - Founder, [Brand]"] |
| [SFX: description] | Sound effect | [SFX: Notification chime] |
| [MUSIC: description] | Background music change | [MUSIC: Upbeat, energetic — fade in] |
| [PAUSE: Xs] | Intentional silence | [PAUSE: 2s for emphasis] |
| [TRANSITION: type] | Scene transition | [TRANSITION: Quick zoom cut] |

### 3.2 Script Formatting Convention

```
[HOOK — 0:00-0:05]

[B-ROLL: Close-up of frustrated person at computer]
"You've been creating content for months and nothing's working."

[CUT TO: Direct to camera]
"Here's the one thing that changed everything for me."

[TEXT: "The Content Leverage Framework"]
[MUSIC: Subtle beat starts]

---

[SECTION 1 — 0:05-2:00]

[GFX: Animated diagram of the framework]
"Most creators make the mistake of..."
```

### 3.3 Timing Annotations

Include timing estimates for each section:

```
[INTRO — Target: 15 seconds / ~40 words]
[SECTION 1 — Target: 2 minutes / ~300 words]
[SECTION 2 — Target: 3 minutes / ~450 words]
[CTA — Target: 15 seconds / ~40 words]
[TOTAL — Target: 8 minutes / ~1,200 words]
```

## 4. Retention Techniques

### 4.1 The Retention Curve

YouTube analytics consistently show the largest viewer drop-off happens in the first 15-30 seconds. After that:
- Videos maintaining 50-60% average view duration are more likely to be recommended
- Scripted short-form content has 34% better retention rates than improvised content
- The algorithm rewards total watch time, not just completion percentage

### 4.2 Retention Hook Phrases

Insert retention hooks every 60-90 seconds to keep viewers engaged:

**Curiosity hooks:**
- "But here's the trick nobody talks about..."
- "Wait, before you do that step..."
- "This next part is the most important..."
- "Here's where it gets interesting..."

**Open loops:**
- "I'll show you the exact framework in a moment, but first..."
- "There's a reason this works, and it's not what you think..."
- "We'll come back to this — but keep it in mind..."

**Direct engagement:**
- "If you've been doing [X], you need to hear this..."
- "Comment below if you've experienced this..."
- "Most people skip this step, and that's why they fail..."

### 4.3 Anti-Drop-Off Techniques

| Technique | When to Use | How It Works |
|-----------|------------|--------------|
| Cold open preview | First 3-5 seconds | Show the best moment of the video first |
| Pattern interrupt | Every 15-20 seconds | Change visual or vocal pattern |
| Progress indicators | Throughout | "Step 2 of 5..." — gives a sense of progress |
| Payoff promise | After complex sections | "Stick with me — the payoff is worth it" |
| Mid-video hook | 50% mark | Re-engage with a new promise or revelation |
| End screen tease | Final 20 seconds | "In the next video, I'll show you..." |

### 4.4 Writing for Speaking, Not Reading

Scripts must be written for the ear, not the eye:

- Use contractions ("don't" not "do not", "you're" not "you are")
- Write in second person ("you") — speak directly to the viewer
- Short sentences. Fragments are fine. Conversational rhythm.
- Read the script aloud during editing — if it feels unnatural to say, rewrite it
- Avoid jargon unless the audience expects it
- Use concrete examples over abstract concepts
- Replace "utilize" with "use," "facilitate" with "help," "implement" with "do"

### 4.5 The "Talk Track" Test

Before finalizing any script, read it aloud at intended delivery speed:
- Mark any phrases that trip you up — rewrite them
- Note where you naturally want to pause — add [PAUSE] cues
- Flag any section where energy drops — add a retention hook before it
- Time each section to verify pacing targets

## 5. Pre-Script Checklist

Before writing any video script, verify:

- [ ] Target length and platform are defined
- [ ] Core message distilled to one sentence
- [ ] 2-4 key points identified (not more)
- [ ] Hook concept ready (opening line drafted)
- [ ] Visual assets available or planned (B-roll, graphics, demos)
- [ ] CTA defined and specific
- [ ] Brand voice guidelines reviewed
- [ ] Target audience and their current awareness level understood

## 6. Common Script Mistakes

1. **Burying the hook** — Never open with "In this video, I'm going to..." Get to the point.
2. **Too many points** — 2-4 key ideas maximum. More than that and nothing sticks.
3. **Writing for reading** — Scripts should sound natural when spoken aloud.
4. **No visual variety notes** — A talking head for 10 minutes loses viewers. Plan visual changes.
5. **Skipping retention hooks** — Without them, viewers drop off after the first section.
6. **Weak CTA** — "Don't forget to like and subscribe" is generic. Be specific about what you want and why.
7. **Over-scripting** — Leave room for natural delivery. Bullet points for some sections can feel more authentic than word-for-word scripts.
8. **Ignoring pacing** — Monotone delivery at one speed kills retention. Script the energy changes.
