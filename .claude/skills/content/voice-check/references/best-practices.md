# Voice Check — Best Practices Reference

> Compiled from brand voice analysis methodology research (March 2026).
> This file is loaded by the voice-check agent before every voice audit session.

---

## 1. Voice Analysis Methodology

A rigorous voice check requires more than gut feel. Use this structured methodology
to evaluate whether content sounds like the brand it represents.

### The Three-Layer Audit

Voice analysis works best in three sequential layers. Each layer isolates a
different concern — do not mix them in a single pass.

**Layer 1 — Trait Verification**
Does the content embody each stated brand voice trait?

- Read the brand DNA and extract the core voice traits (e.g., "bold, direct, human")
- For each trait, search the content for at least 3 sentences that demonstrate it
- Flag any sentences that actively contradict a stated trait
- Apply the "byline test": if you removed the author/brand name, would a reader
  still identify this as the brand? If not, the voice is generic

**Layer 2 — Lexicon Compliance**
Is the vocabulary on-brand?

- Check all words against the brand's "never use" list
- Verify brand-preferred terms are used consistently (not synonyms or alternatives)
- Measure jargon density — technical terms should be defined or universally understood
  by the target audience
- Flag any competitor-specific phrases that have been inadvertently adopted

**Layer 3 — Tone Calibration**
Is the emotional register appropriate for the context?

- Match the piece's tone against the expected tone for its platform and content type
- Verify that humor (if present) aligns with brand personality boundaries
- Check that formality level matches the style guide's directive for this format
- Ensure tone is consistent from opening to closing — common drift occurs when
  pieces are drafted across multiple sessions

## 2. The Four Dimensions of Tone — NN/g Spectrum Framework

The Nielsen Norman Group's research established that any piece of content can be
mapped along four independent spectrum dimensions. Each dimension runs from one
pole to the opposite, scored on a 0-10 scale.

### The Four Spectrums

| Dimension | Left Pole (0) | Right Pole (10) | What It Measures |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Formality** | Formal | Casual | Structure, word choice, sentence construction |
| **Humor** | Serious | Funny | Presence of jokes, wit, wordplay, levity |
| **Respect** | Respectful | Irreverent | Treatment of the subject matter and audience |
| **Energy** | Matter-of-fact | Enthusiastic | Emotional intensity, excitement, urgency |

### How to Score Each Dimension

For each dimension, read the full piece and assign a score from 0 to 10:

**Formality (0 = Formal, 10 = Casual)**
- 0-2: Third person, no contractions, complex sentence structures, academic or legal register
- 3-4: Professional but accessible, occasional contractions, standard business tone
- 5-6: Conversational professional — contractions used freely, direct address ("you"), moderate sentence variety
- 7-8: Casual and friendly — colloquial expressions, short punchy sentences, first-person perspective
- 9-10: Extremely casual — slang, sentence fragments, text-speak patterns

**Humor (0 = Serious, 10 = Funny)**
- 0-2: No levity whatsoever, clinical or grave tone
- 3-4: Dry wit or occasional light touches, subject treated with appropriate weight
- 5-6: Balanced — informative but with regular humor beats, self-aware without being silly
- 7-8: Humor-forward — jokes, puns, playful metaphors drive the narrative
- 9-10: Comedy-first — entertainment is the primary goal, information is secondary

**Respect (0 = Respectful, 10 = Irreverent)**
- 0-2: Deferential, acknowledges complexity, careful with sensitive topics
- 3-4: Polite and professional, treats subject and reader with consideration
- 5-6: Direct and confident — states opinions without excessive hedging
- 7-8: Provocative — challenges conventions, willing to push boundaries
- 9-10: Deliberately contrarian — mocks norms, uses shock value, takes nothing seriously

**Energy (0 = Matter-of-fact, 10 = Enthusiastic)**
- 0-2: Flat, neutral, purely informational, no emotional language
- 3-4: Measured — occasional emphasis, mostly neutral delivery
- 5-6: Engaged — clear interest in the topic, moderate emotional language
- 7-8: Energetic — exclamation marks, strong adjectives, builds excitement
- 9-10: Hyperbolic — superlatives, urgency language, maximum emotional intensity

### Creating a Brand Tone Profile

A tone profile is the target score for each dimension. Example profiles:

| Brand Archetype | Formality | Humor | Respect | Energy |
|-----------------|-----------|-------|---------|--------|
| **Corporate Authority** | 1-2 | 1-2 | 1-2 | 2-3 |
| **Friendly Expert** | 5-6 | 3-4 | 3-4 | 5-6 |
| **Bold Challenger** | 6-7 | 4-5 | 6-7 | 7-8 |
| **Approachable Guide** | 6-7 | 3-4 | 2-3 | 5-6 |
| **Playful Disruptor** | 8-9 | 7-8 | 7-8 | 8-9 |

To set your brand's profile:
1. Read the brand DNA voice traits
2. Map each trait to its closest position on each spectrum
3. Average across traits to establish the target range (allow +/- 1 point variance)
4. Document the profile as "Formality: X, Humor: X, Respect: X, Energy: X"

### Contextual Tone Adaptation

The target profile should shift based on content type. Use the brand profile as
the baseline and apply these modifiers:

| Context | Formality | Humor | Respect | Energy |
|---------|-----------|-------|---------|--------|
| **Error/apology** | -1 | -2 | -2 | -1 |
| **Celebration/launch** | +1 | +1 | 0 | +2 |
| **Educational content** | -1 | 0 | -1 | 0 |
| **Social media** | +2 | +1 | +1 | +1 |
| **Formal announcement** | -2 | -2 | -1 | 0 |
| **Support/help docs** | 0 | -1 | -2 | 0 |

(Modifiers adjust the baseline score. Clamp results to 0-10 range.)

## 3. Adobe Spectrum Tone Model — Dynamic Tone Positions

Adobe's Spectrum design system uses a five-position tone model that maps well
to contextual adaptation. Rather than a fixed tone, content moves along this
spectrum based on the user's emotional state and needs.

| Tone Position | Attitude | When to Use |
|---------------|----------|-------------|
| **Motivational** | Positive, encouraging | Onboarding, milestones, achievements |
| **Helpful** | Polite, respectful | General guidance, feature explanations |
| **Instructive** | Neutral, direct | Step-by-step processes, documentation |
| **Reassuring** | Professional, reliable | Errors, delays, uncertainty |
| **Supportive** | Concerned, empathetic | Failures, data loss, sensitive situations |

This model complements the NN/g spectrum — use the four-dimension scores for
measurement, and the Adobe positions for selecting the right emotional register
before writing or rewriting.

## 4. Voice Scoring Criteria — Weighted Scorecard

Use this scorecard to generate a composite Brand Voice Consistency Score (BVCS)
for any piece of content. Score each dimension on a 0-100 scale, then apply weights.

### The Five Scoring Dimensions

**Dimension 1: Vocabulary Compliance (Weight: 25%)**

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90-100 | Zero banned words. All preferred terms used. Jargon appropriate for audience. |
| 75-89 | 1-2 minor vocabulary misses. Preferred terms mostly used. |
| 60-74 | Several vocabulary gaps. Some banned words or unapproved synonyms. |
| 40-59 | Frequent vocabulary misses. Jargon inappropriate for audience level. |
| 0-39 | Vocabulary is fundamentally off-brand. Reads like a different company. |

**Dimension 2: Tone Alignment (Weight: 25%)**

Score using the NN/g four-spectrum model (Section 2). Calculate the average
deviation from the brand's target profile across all four spectrums.

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90-100 | All four dimensions within +/- 1 of target. Tone is unmistakably on-brand. |
| 75-89 | Most dimensions within range. One dimension off by 2 points. |
| 60-74 | Two dimensions off by 2+ points. Tone is recognizable but inconsistent. |
| 40-59 | Three or more dimensions off. Tone drifts significantly in parts. |
| 0-39 | Tone is unrecognizable as the brand. Fundamental mismatch. |

**Dimension 3: Structural Patterns (Weight: 15%)**

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90-100 | Sentence length, paragraph density, heading style all match brand norms. |
| 75-89 | Minor structural deviations. Formatting mostly consistent. |
| 60-74 | Noticeable structural drift. Some sections feel like different content. |
| 40-59 | Structure is inconsistent throughout. Mixed formatting conventions. |
| 0-39 | Structure violates brand patterns entirely. |

**Dimension 4: Readability Consistency (Weight: 15%)**

Compare the Flesch-Kincaid score against the target for the content type.

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90-100 | Within 3 FRE points of target. Consistent complexity throughout. |
| 75-89 | Within 5 FRE points. Minor readability variance between sections. |
| 60-74 | Within 10 FRE points. Some sections noticeably harder or easier. |
| 40-59 | Off by 10-15 FRE points. Readability is inconsistent. |
| 0-39 | Off by 15+ FRE points. Content is pitched at the wrong audience level. |

**Dimension 5: Brand Identity (Weight: 20%)**

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90-100 | Passes the byline test. Voice traits are demonstrated 3+ times each. Distinctive perspective present. |
| 75-89 | Mostly passes byline test. Most voice traits present. Perspective occasionally generic. |
| 60-74 | Would not pass byline test. Some traits present but voice is not distinctive. |
| 40-59 | Voice is generic. Could be any brand in the industry. |
| 0-39 | Voice contradicts brand identity. Actively off-brand. |

### Composite Score Calculation

```
BVCS = (Vocabulary x 0.25) + (Tone x 0.25) + (Structure x 0.15)
     + (Readability x 0.15) + (Identity x 0.20)
```

### Action Thresholds

| BVCS Range | Rating | Action |
|------------|--------|--------|
| 85-100 | Excellent | Publish. No voice edits needed. |
| 70-84 | Good | Targeted edit. Fix the lowest-scoring dimension only. |
| 55-69 | Needs Work | Significant revision. Rewrite off-brand sections with guidelines applied. |
| Below 55 | Off-Brand | Regenerate. Start over with brand DNA loaded. |

## 5. Before/After Rewrite Techniques

When a line or passage fails the voice check, use these rewrite patterns. Each
pattern addresses a specific type of voice misalignment.

### Pattern 1: Formality Adjustment

**Problem:** Content is too formal for a brand that targets casual, direct communication.

Before (Formal, score 1/10):
> "We are pleased to announce the availability of our latest product offering,
> which has been designed to address the evolving needs of our valued clientele."

After (Casual, score 7/10):
> "Our new product just dropped — and it's built for exactly what you've been
> asking for."

**Technique:** Remove passive voice, cut corporate filler ("pleased to announce,"
"valued clientele"), use contractions, switch to second person ("you"), shorten
sentences.

---

**Problem:** Content is too casual for a brand that requires professional authority.

Before (Casual, score 8/10):
> "So basically we figured out this crazy hack that totally changes how you
> think about investing. No cap."

After (Professional, score 3/10):
> "Our research team identified an approach that challenges conventional
> investment assumptions. Here's what the data shows."

**Technique:** Remove slang and filler ("basically," "crazy," "totally," "no cap"),
add specificity, cite evidence, use third person or professional first person.

### Pattern 2: Energy Calibration

**Problem:** Content is flat when the brand voice calls for enthusiasm.

Before (Matter-of-fact, score 1/10):
> "The feature is now available. Users can access it from the settings menu."

After (Enthusiastic, score 7/10):
> "It's here! The feature you've been waiting for is live — head to your
> settings to try it out right now."

**Technique:** Add temporal urgency ("right now"), use exclamation marks
sparingly, include emotional language ("waiting for"), address the reader directly.

---

**Problem:** Content is overheated when the brand calls for measured confidence.

Before (Enthusiastic, score 9/10):
> "THIS IS ABSOLUTELY INCREDIBLE!!! We are SO thrilled to share the most
> AMAZING update EVER!!! You won't BELIEVE what's coming!!!"

After (Measured, score 4/10):
> "We're excited to share a meaningful update. This release addresses the
> three most-requested features from our community."

**Technique:** Remove caps, reduce exclamation marks (max one per paragraph),
replace superlatives with specifics, ground excitement in evidence.

### Pattern 3: Humor and Irreverence Adjustment

**Problem:** Content lacks the wit the brand is known for.

Before (Serious, score 0/10):
> "Error 404. The requested page could not be found. Please check the URL
> and try again."

After (Witty, score 6/10):
> "Well, this is awkward. That page doesn't exist — but we do. Let's get
> you back on track."

**Technique:** Add self-awareness, use conversational phrasing, acknowledge
the human behind the screen without undermining the message.

---

**Problem:** Humor is forced or inappropriate for the context.

Before (Forced humor, score 8/10):
> "Oopsie-daisy! Looks like your payment didn't go through! LOL, don't
> worry, we've all been there! Try again, bestie!"

After (Warm but professional, score 3/10):
> "Your payment didn't go through. This can happen for a few reasons —
> check your card details and try again. We're here to help if it persists."

**Technique:** Remove diminutives and internet slang, acknowledge the user's
frustration, provide a clear path forward, keep warmth without trivializing.

### Pattern 4: Consistency Repair

**Problem:** Voice shifts mid-piece (common in multi-author or multi-session drafts).

Before (voice shifts from paragraphs 1 to 2):
> "Let's talk about what's actually going on in the market right now.
> The data tells a story most people aren't seeing."
>
> "It should be noted that macroeconomic indicators suggest a fundamental
> realignment of asset allocation strategies across institutional portfolios."

After (consistent voice throughout):
> "Let's talk about what's actually going on in the market right now.
> The data tells a story most people aren't seeing."
>
> "Here's the headline: big institutions are quietly reshuffling where
> they put their money — and the macro data explains why."

**Technique:** Identify the dominant voice in the piece (usually the opening sets
the tone). Rewrite drifting sections to match. When in doubt, match the brand
profile, not the strongest section.

### Pattern 5: Generic-to-Distinctive Transformation

**Problem:** Content is technically correct but could belong to any brand.

Before (Generic):
> "We help businesses grow through innovative solutions and dedicated support.
> Our team of experts is committed to delivering results."

After (Distinctive — for a bold challenger brand):
> "Most consultants sell you a strategy deck and disappear. We stay until
> the numbers move. That's not a tagline — it's how we've operated for
> 12 years."

**Technique:** Replace abstract claims with specific evidence, inject the brand's
unique perspective, use the brand's characteristic sentence rhythm (short/punchy
vs. flowing/narrative), demonstrate values through example rather than assertion.

## 7. Quick Reference — Voice Check Cheat Sheet

Use this during the audit for fast decision-making.

### Red Flags (Immediate Fix)
- Banned words from the style guide's "never use" list
- Competitor-specific phrases or taglines
- Voice trait contradictions (e.g., brand says "bold" but content hedges constantly)
- Tone whiplash — shifting registers within a single section
- Generic corporate filler that adds no meaning

### Yellow Flags (Investigate)
- Vocabulary that is technically allowed but feels off-brand
- Humor that works in isolation but may not land with the full audience
- Formality level that is appropriate but inconsistent with recent content
- Jargon that the audience probably understands but is not in the style guide

### Green Signals (Reinforce)
- Sentences that perfectly embody a brand voice trait — note them as examples
- Distinctive phrasing that could not belong to a competitor
- Natural tone that matches the target profile without feeling forced
- Consistent voice from opening hook to closing CTA
