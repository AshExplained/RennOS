# Course Design Best Practices Reference

> Compiled reference for the course-design skill. Covers foundational methodologies,
> structural patterns, assessment strategies, and completion optimization techniques.

---

## 1. Backward Design — Understanding by Design (UbD)

Backward design, formalized by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe in *Understanding by Design* (1998),
reverses traditional course planning. Instead of starting with content and activities, you start
with the end — what students should know and be able to do — then work backward.

### The Three Stages

**Stage 1: Identify Desired Results**

- Define the **Big Ideas** — core concepts students should retain long-term
- Write **Essential Questions** — open-ended inquiries that drive critical thinking
  (e.g., "What makes a business strategy sustainable?")
- Craft **SMART Learning Objectives** — Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound
- Prioritize content using a three-layer model:
  - Outer layer: material worth being familiar with
  - Middle layer: important knowledge and skills
  - Inner layer: enduring understandings (the non-negotiables)

**Stage 2: Determine Acceptable Evidence**

- Design assessments **before** designing content
- Balance summative assessments (exams, final projects) with formative assessments (quizzes, reflections)
- Apply the **Six Facets of Understanding** — students demonstrate learning through:
  1. Explaining
  2. Interpreting
  3. Applying
  4. Taking perspective
  5. Empathizing
  6. Self-reflecting
- Ensure assessment formats match the type of understanding being measured

**Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction**

- Design activities that directly support achieving the objectives from Stage 1
- Use the **WHERETO Framework** to structure lessons:
  - **W** — Where are students headed? What is expected?
  - **H** — Hook students early with engaging entry points
  - **E** — Equip students with necessary experiences, tools, knowledge
  - **R** — Provide opportunities to rethink, reflect, revise
  - **E** — Allow students to evaluate their work and self-assess
  - **T** — Tailor instruction to individual needs and learning styles
  - **O** — Organize for optimal learning (sequence matters)
- Replace passive lectures with interactive, active learning experiences

### How to Apply UbD in Course Design

1. Start by writing the transformation promise: "By the end, the student will..."
2. List 3-5 enduring understandings the course must deliver
3. For each understanding, define what evidence would prove mastery
4. Only then design the content, activities, and modules to get there
5. Audit every module: does it directly serve an identified outcome?

## 2. Bloom's Taxonomy — 6 Levels of Learning

Bloom's Taxonomy (revised 2001 by Anderson & Krathwohl) provides a hierarchy of cognitive
processes. Use it to write measurable learning objectives and design assessments at the
appropriate difficulty level.

The levels progress from lower-order thinking (Remember) to higher-order thinking (Create).

### Level 1: Remember

**Definition:** Retrieve relevant knowledge from long-term memory.

**Action Verbs:** define, duplicate, list, memorize, recall, repeat, reproduce, state,
name, recognize, identify, label, match, outline, quote, retrieve, tabulate

**Example Objective:** "List the five stages of the customer journey."

### Level 2: Understand

**Definition:** Construct meaning from instructional messages — explain ideas in your own words.

**Action Verbs:** classify, describe, discuss, explain, identify, locate, recognize, report,
select, translate, summarize, interpret, paraphrase, compare, contrast, exemplify, infer,
predict, restate, generalize, illustrate, extend, extrapolate

**Example Objective:** "Explain the difference between a sales funnel and a flywheel model."

### Level 3: Apply

**Definition:** Use knowledge in a new but related context — shift from theory to practice.

**Action Verbs:** execute, implement, solve, use, demonstrate, interpret, operate, schedule,
sketch, write, carry out, calculate, complete, compute, employ, examine, experiment,
manipulate, modify, organize, predict

**Example Objective:** "Build a basic content calendar using the framework provided."

### Level 4: Analyze

**Definition:** Break information into components and determine relationships between them.

**Action Verbs:** differentiate, organize, relate, compare, contrast, distinguish, examine,
question, test, outline, deconstruct, categorize, classify, connect, detect, diagram,
discriminate, divide, integrate, inventory, separate, structure

**Example Objective:** "Compare two competitor positioning strategies and identify gaps."

### Level 5: Evaluate

**Definition:** Make judgments based on criteria and standards — assess and defend.

**Action Verbs:** appraise, argue, defend, judge, select, support, value, critique, weigh,
assess, justify, prioritize, rate, recommend, conclude, determine, rank

**Example Objective:** "Evaluate three pricing models and recommend the best fit for the case study."

### Level 6: Create

**Definition:** Synthesize elements to form something new and original — the most cognitively
demanding level.

**Action Verbs:** design, assemble, construct, conjecture, develop, formulate, author,
investigate, plan, produce, generate, compose, devise, invent, propose, synthesize

**Example Objective:** "Design a 90-day go-to-market strategy for a new digital product."

### Using Bloom's in Course Design

- **Introductory modules** should target Levels 1-2 (Remember, Understand)
- **Core modules** should target Levels 3-4 (Apply, Analyze)
- **Capstone/final modules** should target Levels 5-6 (Evaluate, Create)
- Every learning objective should start with an action verb from the appropriate level
- Assessments must match the level: a "Create"-level objective requires a creative output,
  not a multiple-choice quiz

## 3. Module Structure Best Practices

### Recommended Module Components

Each module should follow a consistent three-phase structure (Vanderbilt University CDR):

1. **Intake** — Introduce new material through reading, video, or audio
2. **Processing** — Engage with content through discussion, quizzes, application activities, or reflection
3. **Demonstration** — Prove acquisition of knowledge or skills through assessment

### Structural Guidelines

**Content Length and Pacing**
- Keep individual content segments to **10 minutes or less** for maximum attention
- Break modules into short segments, each **no longer than 30 minutes total**
- Video lessons: **3-7 minutes** optimal for retention
- Total module time: **45-90 minutes** of active learning per module

**Module Count and Organization**
- Aim for **3-8 modules** per course (enough depth without overwhelm)
- **2-5 lessons** per module, each with a specific objective
- Every module needs a clear theme tied to one course learning outcome

**Consistency Pattern**
Each module should follow the same predictable structure:
1. Module overview (objectives, what you will learn, estimated time)
2. Core content delivery (videos, readings, demonstrations)
3. Practice activity or exercise
4. Knowledge check or reflection
5. Summary and bridge to next module

**Progressive Complexity**
- Sequence modules from foundational to advanced
- Each module should build on the previous one
- Include prerequisite knowledge checks where appropriate
- Use Bloom's Taxonomy to escalate cognitive demands across modules

### Module Overview Template

Every module should open with:
- Module title and number
- Learning objectives (2-4, using Bloom's action verbs)
- Estimated completion time
- Required materials or prerequisites
- Connection to the overall course transformation promise

## 4. Assessment Design — Formative vs. Summative

### Formative Assessments (During Learning)

**Purpose:** Monitor progress, identify misconceptions, provide feedback during instruction.
These are low-stakes and designed to help learners improve.

**Examples:**
- In-lesson quizzes and knowledge checks
- Reflection prompts and journal entries
- Discussion forum posts
- Peer feedback exercises
- Short practice assignments
- Poll questions during live sessions
- Self-assessment checklists

**Best Practices:**
- Provide clear performance criteria upfront
- Deliver specific, actionable feedback tied to predefined criteria
- Create opportunities for revision and resubmission
- Use results to adjust teaching and content pacing
- Foster self-reflection through peer and self-assessment activities
- Make them frequent — at least one per module

### Summative Assessments (After Learning)

**Purpose:** Evaluate student achievement at the conclusion of a unit, module, or course.
These are higher-stakes and measure whether objectives were met.

**Examples:**
- Final projects and portfolios
- Capstone assignments
- Comprehensive exams or tests
- Final presentations
- Case study analyses
- Certification assessments

**Best Practices:**
- Share rubrics and grading criteria in advance
- Design questions/prompts aligned with course learning objectives
- Require synthesized understanding across multiple modules
- Define clear parameters (length, depth, deadlines, grading standards)
- Use rubric-based evaluation for consistency

### Assessment Alignment Rule

Every assessment should map directly to a learning objective:

| Learning Objective Level | Formative Assessment | Summative Assessment |
|--------------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Remember / Understand | Quizzes, flashcards | Short-answer tests |
| Apply | Practice exercises | Applied projects |
| Analyze | Case discussions | Comparative analyses |
| Evaluate | Peer review activities | Evaluation reports |
| Create | Draft submissions | Final creative projects |

## 5. Engagement Techniques for Online Courses

### Content Engagement

- **Mix media formats** — video, text, audio, infographics, interactive elements
- **Use storytelling** — open with a scenario or real-world problem
- **Provide worked examples** — show the process, not just the answer
- **Include "try it now" moments** — immediate application after every concept
- **Use micro-challenges** — small, achievable tasks that build momentum

### Social Engagement

- **Discussion prompts** — specific, thought-provoking questions (not "what do you think?")
- **Peer accountability** — pair students for check-ins or collaborative exercises
- **Community spaces** — dedicated forums, Slack channels, or cohort groups
- **Live Q&A sessions** — periodic synchronous touchpoints
- **Student showcases** — opportunities to share work and get feedback

### Emotional Engagement

- **Quick wins early** — design Module 1 for a fast, visible result
- **Progress indicators** — show completion percentage, badges, or milestones
- **Celebrate milestones** — acknowledge progress at key checkpoints
- **Personal connection** — share instructor/creator stories and experiences
- **Reduce friction** — make navigation intuitive, minimize technical barriers

### Behavioral Engagement

- **Clear action items** — every lesson ends with "do this next"
- **Deadlines and pacing** — even self-paced courses benefit from suggested timelines
- **Streaks and consistency cues** — encourage regular engagement patterns
- **Email/notification nudges** — re-engage inactive learners at key drop-off points

## 6. Course Completion Optimization

### Completion Rate Benchmarks

- Self-paced courses: **10-20%** average completion
- Cohort-based with live support: **40-70%** completion
- Cohort-based with community + live Q&A: **up to 85-90%** completion
- Higher-priced courses (500+) see **45-60%** completion vs. under-100 courses at **15-20%**

### Strategies to Maximize Completion

**Pre-Course**
- Set clear expectations on what the course covers and who it is for
- Communicate the time commitment required
- Provide a course roadmap and syllabus upfront
- Create an onboarding sequence (welcome email, orientation module)

**During Course**
- Keep lessons short: **3-7 minute videos**, **10-minute content bursts**
- Use multiple content formats (video, text, audio, downloads)
- Include interactive elements (quizzes, mini-assignments) for immediate feedback
- Build community through cohort groups or discussion forums
- Send re-engagement nudges at known drop-off points
- Use progress tracking and visual completion indicators

**Structural**
- Front-load value: make Module 1 deliver a tangible result
- Design for momentum: each module should feel like progress
- Create natural checkpoints and celebrate milestones
- Limit total course length: respect the student's time
- Offer multiple pathways when possible (beginner vs. advanced tracks)

**Community-Driven**
- Online communities boost completion rates by **30-40%**
- Cohort models with peer interaction dramatically outperform solo self-paced
- Build accountability through peer partnerships or small groups
- Include live sessions even in primarily async courses
