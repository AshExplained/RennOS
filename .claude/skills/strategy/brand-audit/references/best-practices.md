# Brand Audit Best Practices & Methodology

Use this reference when conducting brand audits. It provides the scoring rubric, methodology, evidence-gathering playbook, gap patterns, and benchmarks.

---

## 1. Eight-Dimension Scoring Rubric

Each dimension is scored 1-10. Below are anchor descriptions for scores of 1 (critical), 5 (developing), and 10 (exemplary).

### 1.1 Voice Consistency

How uniform the brand's tone, language, and personality are across every touchpoint.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | No discernible voice. Tone varies wildly between platforms — formal on LinkedIn, slang-heavy on Twitter, robotic on the website. Reads like multiple unrelated people are posting. No voice guidelines exist. |
| **5** | Recognizable voice exists but breaks down in certain channels or content types. Core personality comes through on primary platforms but secondary channels (email, comments, bios) feel inconsistent. Voice guidelines exist but are loosely followed. |
| **10** | Unmistakable voice across every touchpoint. Whether reading a tweet, blog post, email subject line, or podcast intro, the audience knows exactly who is speaking. Voice is distinctive, ownable, and reinforced by documented guidelines that are actively followed. |

**Evidence to gather:** Compare 3+ posts from each active platform side by side. Read bios, about pages, email signatures, and comment replies. Flag tone shifts, vocabulary mismatches, and personality drift.

### 1.2 Visual Identity

Consistency and quality of logos, colors, typography, imagery style, and design systems across platforms.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | No visual system. Different profile photos across platforms, no consistent color palette, mismatched fonts, pixelated or stock-heavy imagery. No brand guidelines document exists. |
| **5** | Core elements (logo, primary colors) are consistent, but secondary elements (typography hierarchy, image treatment, graphic style) vary. Some platforms are polished while others look dated or off-brand. Guidelines exist but have gaps. |
| **10** | Cohesive, professional visual system across every touchpoint. Profile photos, banners, post templates, website, and any physical materials share a unified design language. Meets WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility standards (4.5:1 contrast ratio minimum). Visual identity is distinctive and ownable. |

**Evidence to gather:** Screenshot profile photos, banners, and recent posts from every platform. Compare color usage, font choices, and image style. Check logo rendering at different sizes (favicon to banner). Verify accessibility contrast ratios.

### 1.3 Messaging Clarity

How clearly the brand communicates who it is, what it does, who it serves, and why it matters.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | Unclear or absent positioning. Bios are vague ("passionate about stuff"), website lacks a clear value proposition, and a new visitor cannot determine what the brand offers within 10 seconds. Jargon-heavy or generic messaging. |
| **5** | Core message is present but inconsistent. Value proposition exists on the website but bios across platforms tell different stories. Some content clearly communicates expertise while other pieces are unfocused. Audience would need multiple touchpoints to understand the brand. |
| **10** | Crystal-clear positioning everywhere. A first-time visitor immediately understands the brand's unique value, target audience, and key offerings on any platform. Messaging is specific, benefit-oriented, and free of jargon. The "only we" statement is obvious — what this brand does that no one else can claim. |

**Evidence to gather:** Read every bio, about page, and tagline across platforms. Time how long it takes to understand the value proposition from a cold visit. Compare the "elevator pitch" across LinkedIn, Twitter, website, and any other profiles. Check for clarity vs. cleverness.

### 1.4 Audience Alignment

How well the brand's content, tone, and positioning resonate with its intended target audience.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | Content attracts the wrong audience or no audience at all. Topics, language level, and references do not match the intended demographic. No evidence of audience research or persona work. Engagement (when it exists) comes from irrelevant accounts. |
| **5** | General alignment with target audience, but some content misses the mark. Engagement comes from a mix of ideal and non-ideal followers. Content topics are relevant but the depth, format, or delivery does not fully match audience preferences. Some audience research exists. |
| **10** | Deep alignment. Content topics, depth, format, posting cadence, and platform choice are all optimized for the target audience. Engagement consistently comes from ideal audience members. Evidence of ongoing audience feedback loops. Content addresses real pain points and aspirations of the target segment. |

**Evidence to gather:** Review follower/connection demographics where available. Analyze who engages (likes, comments, shares) and whether they match the target persona. Check if content topics map to audience pain points. Look at content format preferences (video vs. text vs. audio) relative to where the audience spends time.

### 1.5 Platform Presence

Strategic selection of platforms, completeness of profiles, and activity level on each.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | Missing from key platforms where the target audience lives. Existing profiles are incomplete (no bio, default banner, missing links). Dormant accounts with no posts in 60+ days. No cross-linking between platforms. |
| **5** | Present on major relevant platforms with complete profiles, but activity is uneven. Some platforms are actively maintained while others are neglected. Cross-linking exists but is incomplete. Platform selection is not strategically justified. |
| **10** | Strategically present on every platform where the target audience is active. All profiles are fully optimized (bio, banner, links, CTAs, pinned content). Consistent posting cadence appropriate to each platform's norms. Intentional cross-linking and traffic routing between platforms. Inactive platforms are either revived or intentionally removed. |

**Evidence to gather:** Inventory every platform where the brand has a profile. Check profile completeness (bio, photo, banner, links, location, pinned posts). Measure posting frequency and recency. Verify cross-links. Assess whether platform selection matches where the target audience spends time.

### 1.6 Content Quality

Depth, originality, production value, and usefulness of content published across platforms.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | Content is shallow, generic, or largely repurposed from others without adding value. Poor production quality (blurry images, typos, broken formatting). No original insights, data, or perspectives. Reads like it could be from anyone — no subject-matter authority is evident. |
| **5** | Mix of quality levels. Some content demonstrates genuine expertise and original thinking, but other pieces are surface-level or follow-the-crowd. Production quality is generally acceptable but inconsistent. Content occasionally provides unique value but does not do so reliably. |
| **10** | Consistently high-quality, original content that demonstrates deep expertise. Every piece provides unique insights, frameworks, data, or perspectives that cannot be found elsewhere. Production values are professional. Content is the kind that gets saved, shared, and referenced. The brand is recognized as a source of truth in its domain. |

**Evidence to gather:** Read the 10 most recent posts per platform. Assess originality (does this say something new?), depth (surface-level or substantive?), and production quality (well-formatted, proofread, visually polished?). Check for evidence of subject-matter expertise. Look at save/bookmark rates where available.

### 1.7 Engagement Quality

The depth, relevance, and reciprocity of interactions with the audience.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | Near-zero engagement, or engagement is entirely superficial (bot-like comments, irrelevant replies). Brand does not respond to comments or messages. No community interaction. Engagement feels purchased or manufactured. |
| **5** | Moderate engagement with some genuine conversations. Brand responds to some comments but not consistently. Engagement is mostly surface-level (likes, basic comments like "great post"). Some community interaction exists but is not cultivated intentionally. |
| **10** | Vibrant, authentic engagement. Comments spark genuine discussions. Brand actively responds and engages in conversations. High ratio of meaningful comments to likes. Audience members engage with each other (community effect). Evidence of relationship building — repeat commenters, DM conversations, collaborations that started from engagement. |

**Evidence to gather:** Review comment sections on recent posts. Measure reply rate (does the brand respond to comments?). Assess comment quality (substantive vs. emoji-only). Look for repeat engagers and community dynamics. Check DM responsiveness where applicable. Calculate engagement rate (engagements / followers) and compare to platform averages.

### 1.8 Differentiation

How clearly the brand stands apart from competitors and alternatives in the market.

| Score | Description |
|:------|:------------|
| **1** | Interchangeable with competitors. No unique angle, methodology, perspective, or positioning. If the brand name were swapped with a competitor's, the audience would not notice. Follows trends rather than setting them. No "only we" factor. |
| **5** | Some differentiation exists but is not consistently communicated. The brand has unique qualities but they are buried or inconsistently highlighted. Positioning overlaps significantly with 2-3 competitors. Differentiation is claimed but not demonstrated through content or actions. |
| **10** | Unmistakably distinct. The brand occupies a clear, ownable position that competitors cannot easily replicate. Unique methodology, perspective, story, or approach is woven into every piece of content. Audience can articulate why this brand is different without prompting. The brand defines or leads its category rather than following it. |

**Evidence to gather:** Identify 3-5 direct competitors or alternatives. Compare positioning statements, content themes, visual identity, and voice side by side. Ask: if all logos were removed, could you tell this brand apart? Look for a proprietary framework, unique origin story, contrarian viewpoint, or distinctive methodology. Check audience perception — do comments and testimonials reference what makes this brand unique?

## 2. Brand Audit Methodology

### 2.1 Keller's Brand Equity Model (Adapted for Personal Brands)

The Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) model provides a four-level pyramid for assessing brand strength:

1. **Identity (Who are you?)** — Brand awareness and recognition. Can the target audience identify the brand? Measured through brand recall (unprompted) and recognition (prompted).

2. **Meaning (What are you?)** — Brand associations and positioning. What does the audience associate with the brand? Split into performance (functional attributes, expertise, reliability) and imagery (personality, values, style).

3. **Response (What about you?)** — Audience judgments (quality, credibility, consideration) and feelings (warmth, excitement, security, social approval). Measured through sentiment analysis and NPS.

4. **Resonance (What about you and me?)** — Deep psychological bond. Loyalty, attachment, community, and active engagement. The peak — where audiences become advocates.

**Application to personal brands:** Score each pyramid level. A brand cannot have strong resonance without first establishing identity and meaning. Gaps at lower levels undermine everything above.

### 2.2 Personal Brand Equity (PBE) Framework

Research validates six critical attributes of personal brand equity:

1. **Visibility** — How frequently and prominently the brand appears in relevant spaces
2. **Credibility** — Trust signals: credentials, track record, social proof, endorsements
3. **Differentiation** — Unique positioning relative to peers and competitors
4. **Online Presence** — Digital footprint quality and completeness
5. **Professional Network** — Strength and relevance of connections and collaborations
6. **Reputation** — Aggregate perception across audience, peers, and industry

These six attributes map cleanly onto the eight scoring dimensions, providing theoretical grounding for the rubric.

### 2.3 Perception Mapping

Perceptual mapping plots the brand on a two-axis grid relative to competitors on dimensions that matter to the target audience.

**How to build a perception map for a personal brand:**

1. Select two attributes the target audience values most (e.g., "Technical Depth vs. Accessibility" or "Established Authority vs. Fresh Perspective")
2. Identify 3-5 competitors or reference brands in the same space
3. Plot each brand's position based on content analysis, audience feedback, and platform signals
4. Identify white space — positions that are underserved and strategically attractive
5. Compare current position to intended position from Brand DNA

The gap between current position and intended position is the perception gap — the central finding of any brand audit.

### 2.4 Brand-Market Fit (BMF) Signal

A quantitative complement to qualitative scoring:

**BMF = (Branded Search Volume / Total Category Search Volume) x Sentiment Score**

- High BMF: Audience actively seeks out the brand by name — strong pull
- Low BMF: Brand is one of many options — commodity positioning, vulnerable to competition

For personal brands, proxies include: direct profile visits, name-based searches, inbound inquiry volume, and referral traffic.

## 3. Common Gap Patterns

These are the most frequent gaps discovered in personal brand audits. When conducting an audit, actively look for each one.

### 3.1 The Expertise Gap
**Pattern:** The brand possesses deep expertise but content does not demonstrate it. Posts are surface-level, sharing news or generic advice rather than original insights.
**Signal:** Audience respects the person in private conversations but public content does not reflect that level. Low save/bookmark rates.
**Fix:** Shift content mix toward original frameworks, case studies, behind-the-scenes breakdowns, and data-driven insights.

### 3.2 The Consistency Gap
**Pattern:** Brand quality and activity fluctuate dramatically. Periods of high-quality posting followed by long silences. Visual and verbal identity drift over time.
**Signal:** Follower growth stalls despite occasional viral posts. Audience cannot predict what to expect. Materials contain outdated messaging (studies show 40-60% of brand materials contain outdated messaging).
**Fix:** Establish a sustainable content cadence. Reduce volume if needed to maintain consistency. Audit and update all profiles quarterly.

### 3.3 The Platform Gap
**Pattern:** Brand is absent from platforms where the target audience is most active, or is spread too thin across too many platforms with low-quality presence everywhere.
**Signal:** Target audience members say "I didn't know you were on [platform]" or engagement is high on one platform but near-zero on others.
**Fix:** Audit where the target audience actually spends time. Consolidate to 2-3 primary platforms. Kill dormant profiles that create a negative impression.

### 3.4 The Perception Gap
**Pattern:** How the brand sees itself differs from how the audience sees it. The brand claims "innovative thought leader" but the audience sees "reliable explainer."
**Signal:** Comment themes and testimonial language do not match the brand's self-description. Audience uses different words to describe the brand than the brand uses for itself.
**Fix:** Either align messaging to actual perception (lean into what the audience already values) or deliberately shift content and positioning to close the gap (slower, harder).

### 3.5 The Differentiation Gap
**Pattern:** Brand positioning overlaps heavily with competitors. Content, visual style, and messaging are interchangeable with 3+ others in the space.
**Signal:** Audience follows the brand AND all direct competitors — no loyalty signal. Content could be attributed to anyone in the niche.
**Fix:** Develop a proprietary framework, contrarian perspective, or unique format. Identify and relentlessly reinforce the "only I" factor.

### 3.6 The Engagement Gap
**Pattern:** Content gets impressions and likes but no meaningful interaction. Brand broadcasts but does not converse.
**Signal:** High follower count but low comment-to-like ratio. Brand rarely replies to comments. No repeat engagers or community dynamics.
**Fix:** Shift from broadcast to conversation. End posts with genuine questions. Reply to every comment for 30 days. Feature audience contributions.

### 3.7 The Alignment Gap
**Pattern:** Brand DNA says one thing but actual content and behavior say another. Stated values are not reflected in what the brand publishes or how it engages.
**Signal:** Gap between Brand DNA document and content audit findings. Audience trust issues. ESG or authenticity concerns.
**Fix:** Re-evaluate whether the Brand DNA needs updating or whether execution needs to catch up to aspiration. Close the say-do gap.

### 3.8 The Visibility Gap
**Pattern:** Brand is strong but unknown. High content quality and clear positioning, but insufficient distribution and discoverability.
**Signal:** Low follower counts relative to content quality. Minimal inbound inquiries. Brand is not surfaced in AI/LLM responses or search results for relevant queries.
**Fix:** Invest in distribution (collaborations, guest appearances, cross-posting, SEO). Test AI discoverability by prompting LLMs about the brand's domain and checking if the brand is mentioned.

## 4. Evidence-Gathering Playbook

### 4.1 Per-Platform Evidence Checklist

**LinkedIn:**
- Profile completeness (headline, about, experience, featured, skills)
- Post frequency and format mix (text, carousel, video, article)
- Engagement rate (reactions + comments / followers) — benchmark: 2-5% is good, 5%+ is strong
- Comment quality and reply rate
- Recommendation and endorsement signals
- Creator mode and newsletter usage

**Twitter/X:**
- Bio clarity and link presence
- Tweet frequency and format mix
- Engagement rate — benchmark: 0.5-1% is average, 2%+ is strong
- Thread and long-form content quality
- Reply and quote-tweet behavior
- List memberships and community participation

**Personal Website/Blog:**
- Load speed (target: under 3 seconds)
- Mobile responsiveness
- Clear value proposition above the fold
- SEO fundamentals (meta descriptions, headings, alt text)
- Content depth and publication frequency
- Conversion paths (newsletter, contact, CTA)
- Schema.org markup (Person, ProfessionalService)

**YouTube/Video:**
- Thumbnail consistency and quality
- Title and description optimization
- Average view duration vs. video length
- Subscriber-to-view ratio
- Comment engagement and response rate

**Newsletter/Email:**
- Open rate — benchmark: 30-40% is good, 40%+ is strong for personal brands
- Click-through rate — benchmark: 2-5% is good
- Subscriber growth trend
- Content quality and consistency
- Unsubscribe rate — benchmark: under 0.5% per send

**Podcast (if applicable):**
- Episode consistency and cadence
- Download trends
- Guest quality and relevance
- Cross-promotion effectiveness
- Review ratings and volume

### 4.2 AI Discoverability Check

A 2026-critical dimension. Test how AI systems represent the brand:

1. Prompt 3+ LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) with questions about the brand's domain
2. Check if the brand is mentioned, and whether the description is accurate
3. Verify entity consistency (name, role, affiliations) across AI responses
4. Search for the brand name directly and assess what the AI reports
5. Flag any hallucinations or outdated information

## 5. Industry Benchmarks for Personal Brands

These benchmarks provide context for scoring. They represent "good" performance for established personal brands in the knowledge/expertise space (consultants, creators, thought leaders).

### 5.1 Platform Engagement Benchmarks

| Platform | Metric | Average | Good | Excellent |
|:---------|:-------|:--------|:-----|:----------|
| LinkedIn | Engagement rate | 1-2% | 2-5% | 5%+ |
| Twitter/X | Engagement rate | 0.3-0.5% | 0.5-2% | 2%+ |
| Instagram | Engagement rate | 1-3% | 3-6% | 6%+ |
| YouTube | Avg. view duration | 30-40% | 40-60% | 60%+ |
| Newsletter | Open rate | 20-30% | 30-40% | 40%+ |
| Newsletter | Click rate | 1-2% | 2-5% | 5%+ |
| Website | Bounce rate | 60-70% | 40-60% | Under 40% |
| Website | Avg. session duration | 1-2 min | 2-4 min | 4+ min |

### 5.2 Personal Brand Health Indicators

| Indicator | Healthy Signal | Warning Signal |
|:----------|:---------------|:---------------|
| Follower growth | Steady 2-5% monthly | Flat or declining |
| Inbound inquiries | Regular, relevant | Rare or off-target |
| Content saves/bookmarks | 1-3% of impressions | Under 0.5% |
| Repeat engagers | 10-20% of commenters | Under 5% |
| Referral/word-of-mouth | Frequent mentions by others | Brand only self-promotes |
| AI discoverability | Accurately mentioned in LLM outputs | Absent or misrepresented |
| Audience sentiment | 80%+ positive | Under 60% positive |
| Profile completeness | 100% on primary platforms | Missing key fields |
| Cross-platform consistency | Unified identity | Fragmented presentation |

### 5.3 Career and Business Impact Benchmarks

Research-backed data points for context:

- Buyers increasingly research individuals rather than corporate pages — personal brand drives B2B trust
- High Personal Brand Equity (PBE) scores correlate significantly with job satisfaction, salary progression, and advancement opportunities
- 81% of consumers buy from brands they trust — trust is the primary currency of personal branding
- Strong personal brands command pricing premiums of 10-25% over comparable competitors
- Employee advocates (strong personal brands within organizations) generate 8x more engagement than corporate brand accounts

### 5.4 Overall Brand Health Score Interpretation

| Score Range | Rating | Interpretation |
|:------------|:-------|:---------------|
| 72-80 | Exceptional | Market leader. Brand is a category-defining force. Focus on maintaining and expanding. |
| 56-71 | Strong | Well-positioned brand with clear identity. Minor gaps to address. |
| 40-55 | Developing | Solid foundation but significant gaps in 2-3 dimensions. Prioritize and focus. |
| 24-39 | Emerging | Brand has potential but lacks consistency, clarity, or presence. Needs structured buildout. |
| 8-23 | Critical | Major gaps across most dimensions. Requires foundational brand strategy work before tactical fixes. |
