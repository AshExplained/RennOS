# Brand DNA — Best Practices Reference

Use this document as domain methodology when building or updating a brand DNA. Cross-reference these frameworks to produce a complete, differentiated, and actionable brand identity.

---

## 1. Brand DNA Core Components

A brand DNA distills an entire identity into a single-page reference. The standard components (per Peter Wilken's Brand Centered Management framework and ThinkFWD):

| Component | Definition |
|:---|:---|
| **Role** | Core reason for existing beyond profit. The job the brand does in people's lives. |
| **Promise** | The overarching commitment to stakeholders — what the brand will always deliver. |
| **Benefit** | The value customers receive when the brand fully expresses its DNA. |
| **Culture / Spirit** | The values and behaviors that shape daily operations and internal decision-making. |
| **Icons & Attributes** | Signature visual and sensory elements — logos, colors, sounds, textures. |

### Quality Test: RCDC Framework

Evaluate every Brand DNA draft against four measures:

- **Relevant** — Does it matter to the target audience?
- **Compelling** — Does it engage and motivate?
- **Differentiating** — Does it set the brand apart from competitors?
- **Credible** — Can the brand actually deliver on it?

If any of the four fail, revise before finalizing.

## 2. Brand Archetype Framework (12 Jungian Archetypes)

Archetypes tap into universal psychological patterns identified by Carl Jung. A brand should align ~70% with one primary archetype and ~30% with a secondary "influencer" archetype for differentiation.

### The 12 Archetypes

| # | Archetype | Core Desire | Brand Voice | Example Brands |
|:--|:----------|:------------|:------------|:---------------|
| 1 | **The Innocent** | Safety | Optimistic, honest, pure | Dove, Coca-Cola, Nintendo |
| 2 | **The Everyman** | Belonging | Friendly, approachable, down-to-earth | IKEA, Home Depot, eBay |
| 3 | **The Hero** | Mastery | Motivational, empowering, determined | Nike, FedEx, Under Armour |
| 4 | **The Outlaw** | Liberation | Rebellious, anti-establishment, raw | Harley-Davidson, Diesel, Jack Daniels |
| 5 | **The Explorer** | Freedom | Adventurous, daring, independent | The North Face, Patagonia, Subaru |
| 6 | **The Creator** | Innovation | Imaginative, expressive, visionary | LEGO, Adobe, Crayola |
| 7 | **The Ruler** | Control | Authoritative, commanding, sophisticated | Mercedes-Benz, Rolex, Microsoft |
| 8 | **The Magician** | Transformation | Mysterious, enchanting, visionary | Apple, Disney, Tesla |
| 9 | **The Lover** | Intimacy | Passionate, sensual, warm | Chanel, Victoria's Secret, Godiva |
| 10 | **The Caregiver** | Service | Compassionate, nurturing, protective | Johnson & Johnson, UNICEF, Volvo |
| 11 | **The Jester** | Enjoyment | Playful, humorous, irreverent | Dollar Shave Club, Old Spice, M&Ms |
| 12 | **The Sage** | Understanding | Analytical, thoughtful, authoritative | Google, Harvard, The Wall Street Journal |

### How to Apply Archetypes

1. **Identify the primary archetype** — Which one aligns with the brand's mission and audience expectations?
2. **Select a secondary archetype** — Which one adds differentiation without creating confusion?
3. **Map voice traits** — Use the archetype's voice descriptors as a starting point for the voice section.
4. **Validate against competitors** — If the primary archetype matches your top competitor, lean harder into the secondary or pick a different primary.
5. **Use archetypes as guardrails** — When creating content, ask: "Would [archetype] say this?"

### Archetype Groupings by Human Motivation

| Motivation | Archetypes |
|:-----------|:-----------|
| **Stability & Control** | Caregiver, Ruler, Creator |
| **Independence & Fulfillment** | Innocent, Sage, Explorer |
| **Risk & Mastery** | Hero, Magician, Outlaw |
| **Belonging & Enjoyment** | Everyman, Jester, Lover |

## 3. Positioning Frameworks

### 3.1 Classic Positioning Statement

The standard formula:

> For **[target audience]**, **[brand]** is the **[category]** that **[key differentiator]** because **[proof/reason to believe]**.

Rules:
- One sentence. No comma-separated lists of benefits.
- The differentiator must be something only you can claim.
- The proof must be verifiable.

### 3.2 Perceptual Mapping

A 2D visual tool that plots brands on two axes that matter most to the category. Common axis pairs:

| Axis X | Axis Y |
|:-------|:-------|
| Affordable <-> Premium | Traditional <-> Innovative |
| Mainstream <-> Niche | Functional <-> Emotional |
| Simple <-> Complex | Serious <-> Playful |

**How to use it:**
1. Choose the two dimensions that best define the competitive landscape.
2. Plot 5-8 competitor brands on the map.
3. Identify the "white space" — positions no one occupies.
4. Evaluate whether the white space is genuinely valuable to customers or just empty for a reason.
5. Position the brand in the most defensible and desirable open space.

### 3.3 Category Design

From "Play Bigger" methodology — instead of competing in an existing category, define a new one:

1. **Name the category** — Create a label for a problem space no one has named.
2. **Frame the problem** — Articulate the problem in a way that makes your solution the obvious answer.
3. **Educate the market** — Invest in explaining the category, not just your product.
4. **Become the category king** — First-mover advantage is massive in category design.

Best for: Brands building something genuinely new rather than competing for market share.

### 3.4 Blue Ocean Strategy

Create uncontested market space using the **Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create (ERRC) Grid**:

| Action | Question |
|:-------|:---------|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors does the industry take for granted that should be removed entirely? |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should be scaled back well below the industry standard? |
| **Raise** | Which factors should be elevated far above what the industry offers? |
| **Create** | Which factors should be introduced that the industry has never offered? |

This grid forces strategic trade-offs instead of "be better at everything" thinking.

## 4. Voice Spectrum Methodology

Voice is constant (who you are). Tone varies by context (how you say it in a given situation). Both must be defined.

### 4.1 The Four Dimensions of Tone (Nielsen Norman Group)

Every piece of content can be plotted on four spectrums:

```
Formal <------------------------------> Casual
Serious <------------------------------> Funny
Respectful <------------------------------> Irreverent
Matter-of-fact <------------------------------> Enthusiastic
```

**How to use:**
1. Place a marker on each spectrum for the brand's default position.
2. Define acceptable range — e.g., "We sit at 7/10 casual by default, but shift to 4/10 in legal or crisis contexts."
3. Identify which dimension shifts by context and which stays fixed.

### 4.2 Extended Six-Axis Framework

For more granularity, add two more axes:

```
Objective <------------------------------> Editorial
Simple <------------------------------> Technical
```

### 4.3 Voice Characteristic Matrix

Define the voice using a structured matrix:

| Voice Trait | Description | This Means We... | This Means We Never... |
|:------------|:------------|:-----------------|:----------------------|
| e.g., Direct | We get to the point | Use short sentences, lead with the action | Bury the point in qualifications or jargon |
| e.g., Warm | We treat the reader like a peer | Use "you" and "we," show empathy | Talk down, lecture, or sound robotic |

Aim for 4-5 voice traits maximum — more than that becomes unactionable.

### 4.4 Tone Variation Table

Show how the same voice adjusts across contexts:

| Context | Tone Shift | Example |
|:--------|:-----------|:--------|
| Social media | More casual, more enthusiastic | "We just shipped something wild." |
| Email newsletter | Warm, conversational | "Here's what we've been working on." |
| Error message | Empathetic, direct | "Something went wrong. Here's how to fix it." |
| Crisis comms | Formal, respectful, matter-of-fact | "We take this seriously. Here's what happened." |

## 5. Messaging Pillar Development

### What Messaging Pillars Are

3-5 core themes that form the "load-bearing walls" of brand communication. Every piece of content should map back to at least one pillar.

### How to Identify Pillars

1. **Start with the value proposition** — What is the single most important thing you offer?
2. **List every benefit** — Brain-dump every reason someone should care about the brand.
3. **Cluster benefits into themes** — Group related benefits. Each cluster becomes a candidate pillar.
4. **Stress-test each pillar:**
   - Is it true? (Credibility)
   - Is it unique to us? (Differentiation)
   - Does the audience care? (Relevance)
   - Can we create 20+ pieces of content under this theme? (Depth)
5. **Narrow to 3-5** — If you have more, you have sub-themes, not pillars.

### Pillar Hierarchy (Messaging Pyramid)

```
         +---------------+
         |   Tagline     |  <-- One line that captures the brand
         +---------------+
         |  Value Prop   |  <-- 1-2 sentences on the core promise
         +---------------+
         |   Pillars     |  <-- 3-5 themes that support the value prop
         |  (3-5 max)    |
         +---------------+
         | Proof Points  |  <-- Facts, stats, stories that back each pillar
         +---------------+
```

### Pillar Format

Each pillar should include:
- **Pillar name** — 2-4 word label (e.g., "Radical Transparency")
- **Pillar statement** — One sentence explaining the theme
- **Proof points** — 3-5 specific facts, features, or stories that support it
- **Content angles** — Types of content that could be created under this pillar

## 6. How to Write Anti-Examples

Anti-examples define the brand by exclusion — what it does NOT sound like. They prevent voice drift and give content creators clear boundaries.

### The "This But Never That" Formula

For each voice trait, define the boundary it must not cross:

| We Are... | But Never... |
|:----------|:-------------|
| Confident | Arrogant |
| Direct | Blunt or rude |
| Witty | Sarcastic or mocking |
| Warm | Saccharine or fake |
| Expert | Condescending or jargon-heavy |
| Bold | Reckless or offensive |
| Casual | Sloppy or unprofessional |
| Passionate | Preachy or self-righteous |

### Writing Full Anti-Examples

Provide side-by-side comparisons of on-brand vs. off-brand phrasing:

| Scenario | On-Brand (Do) | Off-Brand (Don't) |
|:---------|:--------------|:-------------------|
| Announcing a feature | "We built this because you asked for it." | "Introducing our revolutionary, industry-first innovation." |
| Handling criticism | "Fair point. Here's what we're doing about it." | "We respectfully disagree with this characterization." |
| Teaching a concept | "Here's the simplest way to think about this." | "As any expert would know..." |

### Anti-Example Categories

Cover at least these categories:
1. **Tone anti-examples** — What emotion we never project (e.g., "never condescending")
2. **Vocabulary anti-examples** — Words and phrases we never use (e.g., "never say 'synergy' or 'disrupt'")
3. **Structure anti-examples** — Formats we avoid (e.g., "never use walls of text without headers")
4. **Competitor voice anti-examples** — Specific competitors whose voice we must not mirror

## 7. Visual Identity in Brand DNA

While brand DNA is primarily verbal, a summary of visual identity anchors the document:

| Element | What to Define |
|:--------|:---------------|
| **Color palette** | Primary, secondary, accent colors with hex codes |
| **Typography** | Heading and body fonts, size hierarchy |
| **Photography style** | Subject matter, lighting, composition preferences |
| **Iconography** | Style (line, filled, duotone), corner radius |
| **Layout principles** | Whitespace philosophy, grid approach, density |
