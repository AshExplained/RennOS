# Brand DNA — DevMentor (Fictional Example)

> Last updated: 2026-03-24

---

## 1. Core Identity

### Who
Marcus Chen is a former senior engineer at Stripe and Google who now teaches software developers how to level up from mid-level to staff engineer. He runs DevMentor — a one-person education brand focused on system design, career strategy, and technical leadership.

### What
DevMentor produces technical courses, a weekly newsletter ("The Staff Engineer"), a YouTube channel with system design breakdowns, and a paid community for engineers targeting staff-level promotions.

### Why
The tech industry produces thousands of competent mid-level engineers but gives them almost no guidance on the leap to staff. Promotion committees are opaque, system design interviews are poorly understood, and most "career advice" content is generic hustle culture. DevMentor exists to give ambitious engineers a clear, honest roadmap from mid to staff — because the industry wastes enormous talent by keeping the path invisible.

---

## 2. Brand Archetype

- **Primary Archetype:** The Sage — DevMentor exists to seek, organize, and share hard-won engineering knowledge. The brand's authority comes from depth, not hype.
- **Secondary Archetype:** The Hero — The audience is on a career quest, and DevMentor equips them to overcome specific obstacles (promo committees, system design interviews, influence without authority).

## 4. Voice

### Voice Characteristics

| Trait | Description | This Means We... | This Means We Never... |
|:------|:------------|:-----------------|:----------------------|
| **Precise** | We say exactly what we mean with zero filler | Use concrete numbers, name specific technologies, give step-by-step breakdowns | Use vague phrases like "it depends" without following up with the actual variables |
| **Candid** | We tell the truth about how tech careers actually work | Share uncomfortable realities (politics exist, pedigree matters, not everyone gets promoted) | Sugarcoat, dodge hard questions, or pretend meritocracy is the whole story |
| **Structured** | We organize complex topics into clear mental models | Use numbered lists, decision trees, and named frameworks | Write stream-of-consciousness posts or ramble without a clear takeaway |
| **Peer-level** | We talk to engineers as equals, not students | Use "you" and "we," reference shared frustrations, assume competence | Lecture, talk down, or explain things the audience already knows |

### Voice Spectrum

```
Formal <-----------*-------> Casual        Default: 6/10 (conversational but not sloppy)
Serious <------*------------> Funny         Default: 4/10 (dry humor only, never jokey)
Respectful <---*---------------> Irreverent   Default: 3/10 (deeply respectful of the reader's time)
Matter-of-fact <-----------*-------> Enthusiastic Default: 7/10 (genuine enthusiasm for engineering craft)
```

### On-Brand Examples

| Scenario | On-Brand Phrasing |
|:---------|:------------------|
| Announcing a new course | "The system design course is live. 12 modules, 47 real case studies, zero whiteboard-and-marker hand-waving." |
| Responding to a compliment | "Glad it landed. That section on load balancing took three rewrites." |
| Teaching a concept | "Here's how to think about database sharding in three steps — the same framework I used at Stripe when we hit the single-node ceiling." |
| Correcting a misconception | "This is a common take, but it breaks down once you factor in write amplification. Here's why." |

### Anti-Examples (What We Do NOT Sound Like)

| We Are... | But Never... |
|:----------|:-------------|
| Confident | Arrogant or dismissive of other engineers' experience |
| Direct | Blunt to the point of cruelty — we never mock someone for not knowing something |
| Opinionated | Dogmatic — we acknowledge trade-offs and say "in this context" |
| Technical | Gatekeeping — we never flex jargon to exclude people |

### Words & Phrases

| Use | Avoid |
|:----|:------|
| "Here's the framework" | "Let me break it down for you" (condescending) |
| "Trade-off" | "Best practice" (implies one right answer) |
| "In my experience at [company]" | "Trust me" (unsubstantiated authority) |
| "The uncomfortable truth is..." | "Hot take:" (performative contrarianism) |
| "Staff-level thinking" | "10x engineer" (cringe, elitist) |

## 5. Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1: The Unwritten Rules

- **Statement:** The path from mid to staff is governed by unwritten rules that no one teaches you — DevMentor makes them explicit.
- **Proof Points:**
  - Marcus served on promotion committees at Google and Stripe
  - The "Promotion Packet Teardown" series has 2M+ views
  - Community members report 3x higher promo success rate vs. industry average
- **Content Angles:** Promo committee breakdowns, "what your manager won't tell you" posts, career decision frameworks

### Pillar 2: System Design, Not System Memorization

- **Statement:** We teach system design as a thinking skill, not a set of answers to memorize before an interview.
- **Proof Points:**
  - 47 case studies drawn from real production systems (Stripe, Google, Netflix)
  - Students design systems from first principles, not templates
  - Interview pass rates 40% higher than prep-course industry average
- **Content Angles:** "How X actually works" deep dives, design review walkthroughs, trade-off analysis posts

### Pillar 3: Engineering Leadership Without the Title

- **Statement:** Staff-level impact starts before the title — you can lead without authority if you know how.
- **Proof Points:**
  - "Influence Without Authority" workshop rated 4.9/5 by 800+ engineers
  - RFC writing guide adopted internally by 12 companies
  - Weekly newsletter covers real leadership scenarios, not theory
- **Content Angles:** Stakeholder management, writing effective RFCs, cross-team project leadership, technical strategy docs

### Pillar 4: Radical Honesty About Tech Careers

- **Statement:** We tell the truth about compensation, politics, pedigree, and the gap between what companies say and what they do.
- **Proof Points:**
  - Compensation transparency posts with real numbers
  - "The Meritocracy Myth" article shared 50K+ times
  - No sponsored content — revenue comes from courses and community only
- **Content Angles:** Comp negotiation, when to leave vs. stay, navigating reorgs, the real cost of startup equity

## 6. Audience Summary

### Primary Audience

- **Who:** Software engineers with 3-7 years of experience, currently at mid or senior level, working at mid-to-large tech companies. Ages 26-35. Ambitious but frustrated by opaque promotion processes.
- **Pain Points:** "I'm doing staff-level work but not getting the title." Don't know what the promo committee actually looks for. System design interviews feel like a black box. Career advice online is either too generic or too hustle-culture.
- **Goals:** Get promoted to staff engineer within 12-18 months. Build a reputation for technical leadership. Understand system design deeply, not just interview-level.
- **Where They Are:** Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Hacker News, YouTube, Substack, internal Slack communities.
- **How They Talk:** Technical but not academic. Value precision. Allergic to buzzwords and hustle culture. Respect specificity — "show me the code" mentality.

### Secondary Audience

- **Who:** Engineering managers who were recently promoted from IC roles and want to support their reports' growth to staff.
- **Pain Points:** Don't have a framework for coaching engineers toward staff. Promo packets are hard to write well.
- **Goals:** Help their best engineers get promoted. Build a team known for developing senior talent.

## 8. Tone Variation Guide

| Context | Tone Shift | Example |
|:--------|:-----------|:--------|
| Twitter/X | More casual, punchier, dry wit allowed | "Your system design interview answer doesn't need a load balancer. It needs a napkin sketch and five good questions." |
| Newsletter | Warm, peer-to-peer, slightly more personal | "I rewrote this section three times because the first version was the kind of hand-wavy advice I hate reading." |
| YouTube | Enthusiastic about the craft, teaching mode | "Let's walk through how Netflix actually handles this — and why the textbook answer is wrong." |
| Course content | Structured, precise, patient | "Step 3: Calculate the write throughput. Here's the formula and here's why each variable matters." |
| Community / DMs | Most casual, supportive, direct | "Good question. Short answer: yes, but only if your org uses a calibration committee. Here's why..." |
| Crisis / Sensitive | Formal, empathetic, no humor | "We got this wrong. Here's what happened, what we've changed, and how to get a refund if you want one." |
