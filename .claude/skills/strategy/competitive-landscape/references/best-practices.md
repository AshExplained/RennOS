# Competitive Analysis — Best Practices Reference

A consolidated reference covering four core frameworks for competitive landscape analysis:
Porter's Five Forces, Perceptual Mapping, SWOT Analysis, and Strategic Group Mapping.
Use these frameworks together to build a thorough, multi-dimensional competitive picture.

---

## 1. Porter's Five Forces

### Purpose

Developed by Michael Porter (Harvard Business School, 1979), this framework identifies the
five competitive forces that shape every industry and determine its long-term profitability.
It answers: "How attractive is this industry, and where does competitive pressure come from?"

### The Five Forces

#### 1.1 Threat of New Entrants

New competitors entering an industry introduce additional capacity and pricing pressure,
capping profit potential.

**Factors to evaluate:**
- Economies of scale requirements
- Brand awareness and loyalty costs
- Capital requirements for entry
- Access to distribution channels
- Government regulations and patents
- Network effects protecting incumbents
- Switching costs for customers

#### 1.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Powerful suppliers can squeeze industry profitability by raising prices or reducing the
quality of inputs.

**Factors to evaluate:**
- Number and concentration of available suppliers
- Availability of substitute inputs
- Switching costs between suppliers
- How critical the supplier's input is to the final product
- Whether the supplier can forward-integrate into the industry

#### 1.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers

Powerful customers force prices down or demand more service at existing prices, capturing
more value for themselves.

**Factors to evaluate:**
- Buyer concentration relative to the industry
- Volume of purchases by individual buyers
- Product differentiation level (commodity vs. unique)
- Switching costs for buyers
- Price sensitivity of the buyer segment
- Whether buyers can backward-integrate

#### 1.4 Threat of Substitute Products or Services

Alternatives from other industries that meet the same underlying need limit pricing power
and industry profitability.

**Factors to evaluate:**
- Price-performance trade-off of substitutes
- Buyer switching costs to the substitute
- Buyer propensity to switch
- Whether the substitute offers a fundamentally different value proposition

#### 1.5 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

Intense competition drives down prices or dissipates profits by raising the cost of
competing (marketing wars, R&D races, service escalation).

**Conditions that intensify rivalry:**
- Numerous or similarly-sized competitors
- Slow industry growth
- High fixed costs forcing volume competition
- Low product differentiation
- High exit barriers (specialized assets, emotional commitment)
- Diverse competitor goals and strategies

### How to Apply Porter's Five Forces

1. **Assess each force** on a low / medium / high threat scale
2. **Identify the 1-2 dominant forces** most impacting industry profitability
3. **Analyze barriers and drivers** behind each force
4. **Ask diagnostic questions** specific to the competitive landscape
5. **Map interconnections** between forces (e.g., high buyer power + low switching costs)
6. **Develop positioning** — choose cost leadership, differentiation, or niche focus
7. **Reassess periodically** — industry structure changes over time

### Responding to the Five Forces: Porter's Generic Strategies

- **Cost Leadership:** Reduce operational costs to maintain margins and grow share
- **Differentiation:** Create unique, hard-to-replicate offerings that command premiums
- **Focus/Niche:** Serve a narrow segment more effectively than broad-market competitors

### Limitations

- Provides a snapshot, not a dynamic view — must be updated regularly
- Does not account for internal company capabilities directly
- May undervalue the role of disruptive innovation
- Works best when complemented with other frameworks (SWOT, perceptual mapping)

## 2. Perceptual Mapping

### Purpose

Perceptual mapping is a visual technique that plots competitors on a two-dimensional matrix
based on how they are perceived by customers. It reveals positioning gaps, direct
competitors, and white-space opportunities.

### Core Methodology

#### Step 1: Identify Competitors

List 5-10 key competitors or peers in the space. Include direct competitors, adjacent
players, and potential substitutes.

#### Step 2: Choose Two Axes (Dimensions)

This is the most critical decision. Each axis represents a key attribute that customers
value when making decisions.

**Guidelines for choosing axes:**
- Select dimensions that genuinely differentiate competitors from each other
- Use attributes that matter to buyers (not just internal metrics)
- Ensure the two axes are independent (low correlation with each other)
- Avoid redundant pairs (e.g., "price" and "cost" measure the same thing)
- Test multiple axis pairs — different pairs reveal different insights

**Common axis pairs by industry type:**

| Industry | Possible X-Axis | Possible Y-Axis |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Personal brands | Content depth | Audience size |
| SaaS | Ease of use | Feature richness |
| Retail | Price | Quality / Premium feel |
| Media | Niche expertise | Mainstream reach |
| Consulting | Specialization | Firm size |
| Food & Beverage | Health-focus | Convenience |
| Education | Credentialing power | Accessibility |

**For personal brands specifically, strong axis options include:**
- Content depth vs. content breadth
- Thought leadership vs. entertainment value
- Niche authority vs. mainstream appeal
- Production quality vs. posting frequency
- Community engagement vs. audience scale

#### Step 3: Plot Each Competitor

Position each competitor (and your own brand) on the map based on available data:
- Customer surveys and sentiment analysis
- Social media analytics (engagement, reach, follower demographics)
- Content analysis (topics, formats, frequency)
- Market research reports
- Qualitative assessment from sales/marketing teams

#### Step 4: Identify Clusters and White Space

- **Clusters:** Groups of competitors positioned near each other are direct rivals
  fighting for the same positioning territory
- **White space:** Empty areas on the map represent unclaimed positioning —
  potential opportunities if there is genuine demand there
- **Crowded zones:** Heavily populated areas indicate intense competition and
  potential commoditization

#### Step 5: Interpret and Act

- Where does your brand sit relative to competitors?
- Are you in a crowded zone or occupying unique space?
- Is there attractive white space you could move toward?
- Which competitors are closest to you (your direct rivals)?
- Does your perceived position match your intended position?

### Best Practices

- **Create multiple maps** with different axis pairs for richer analysis
- **Update quarterly** as competitive positions shift
- **Validate with data** — don't rely solely on internal assumptions
- **Include emerging competitors** that may not yet be well-known
- **Use as a communication tool** — maps are excellent for aligning team understanding

### Limitations

- Only captures two dimensions per map
- Positioning is somewhat subjective and perception-based
- Provides a snapshot, not a trend view
- Must be refreshed regularly to remain useful

## 3. SWOT Analysis

### Purpose

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) is a structured framework for
evaluating both internal capabilities and external market conditions. It connects
what you are good at with what the market makes possible.

### The Four Quadrants

#### Strengths (Internal, Positive)

What does the brand/company do well? What unique resources or advantages does it have?

**Questions to ask:**
- What are the brand's core competencies?
- What do customers consistently praise?
- What assets, skills, or relationships are hard to replicate?
- Where does the brand outperform competitors?

#### Weaknesses (Internal, Negative)

Where does the brand fall short? What limitations constrain growth?

**Questions to ask:**
- What do customers complain about?
- Where are resource gaps (time, money, skills, tools)?
- What do competitors do better?
- What internal processes are inefficient?

#### Opportunities (External, Positive)

What external conditions or trends could benefit the brand?

**Questions to ask:**
- What market trends favor the brand's strengths?
- Are there underserved customer segments?
- What technological or platform changes create openings?
- Are competitors leaving gaps the brand could fill?

#### Threats (External, Negative)

What external conditions or trends could harm the brand?

**Questions to ask:**
- What are competitors doing that could steal market share?
- Are there regulatory, economic, or platform changes that could hurt?
- Is the market becoming commoditized?
- Are customer preferences shifting away from the brand's strengths?

### Best Practices (9 Tips for Mastering SWOT)

1. **Perform SWOT semi-frequently** — at least every two months to stay current with the
   competitive environment
2. **Use for competitive positioning** — SWOT is most effective when you understand how
   you are positioned against competitors
3. **Analyze competitors too** — don't limit SWOT to your own brand; conduct parallel
   analyses on primary competitors for a comprehensive landscape view
4. **Use as a communication tool** — the SWOT framework is universally recognized and
   helps translate findings for stakeholders
5. **Extend into enablement assets** — convert findings into practical materials like
   battlecards, talking points, and positioning guides
6. **Involve cross-functional teams** — engage people from different areas to avoid
   blind spots from single-perspective analysis
7. **Ensure actionability** — track how thoroughly recommendations are implemented;
   the analysis is only valuable if it drives action
8. **Try TOWS analysis** — invert the acronym to start with external factors (Threats
   and Opportunities) first, which can yield fresh strategic insights
9. **Apply broadly** — SWOT adapts to products, personal development, projects, and
   individual content pillars, not just overall business strategy

### The TOWS Matrix Extension

After completing SWOT, build a TOWS matrix to generate strategies:

| | **Opportunities** | **Threats** |
|---|---|---|
| **Strengths** | SO: Use strengths to capture opportunities | ST: Use strengths to mitigate threats |
| **Weaknesses** | WO: Overcome weaknesses to capture opportunities | WT: Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats |

### Making SWOT Actionable

- **Prioritize factors** against your strategic vision and current OKRs
- **Examine relationships** between factors — a strength may directly counter a threat
- **Pressure-test** your resulting strategy through scenario planning or business wargaming
- **Assign owners** to each action item with clear timelines
- **Complement with PEST analysis** (Political, Economic, Social, Technological) for a
  fuller external picture

### Limitations

- Can become a list-making exercise without actionable follow-through
- Subject to cognitive bias if done by a single person
- Does not inherently prioritize factors — all items appear equal
- Static unless actively updated

## 4. Strategic Group Mapping

### Purpose

Strategic Group Mapping visualizes how firms in the same industry cluster based on the
strategic choices they make. It shows "who competes with whom and how," revealing
distinct groups that follow similar approaches and face similar competitive dynamics.

### When to Use

- Identifying your direct competitors vs. indirect ones
- Understanding mobility barriers between competitive positions
- Spotting white space where few or no competitors operate
- Informing repositioning, pricing, or market entry decisions
- Understanding where profits tend to concentrate in the industry

### Step-by-Step Methodology

#### Step 1: Define the Industry and Identify Competitors

- List all significant players in the industry (aim for at least 5-10)
- Include direct rivals, adjacent players, and emerging entrants
- Research each competitor's products/services, market impact, objectives,
  strengths, and weaknesses

#### Step 2: Identify Differentiating Strategic Variables

Choose variables that meaningfully distinguish how companies compete.

**Common strategic variables:**
- Price level / pricing strategy
- Product quality and features
- Product/service diversity (narrow vs. broad line)
- Geographic coverage (local, regional, national, global)
- Market segments served (mass market vs. niche)
- Distribution channels (direct, retail, online, hybrid)
- Vertical integration degree
- Technology / innovation level
- Brand image and positioning
- Customer service intensity
- Marketing spend / approach

#### Step 3: Select Two Variables for the Map Axes

**Critical rules for axis selection:**
- Choose two variables with **low correlation** to each other — if they move together,
  the map won't reveal meaningful groups
- Select variables that **actually separate companies** into distinct clusters
- Prefer variables that represent **genuine strategic choices**, not just outcomes
- Avoid pairs that are essentially measuring the same thing

**Recommended axis pairs:**
- Price vs. product range breadth
- Geographic scope vs. specialization depth
- Innovation level vs. market segment (mass vs. niche)
- Quality/premium positioning vs. distribution reach
- Content depth vs. audience breadth (for personal brands)

#### Step 4: Plot Companies on the Map

- Create a 2D grid with the two selected variables as axes
- Position each company based on its standing on both variables
- Use circle size to represent relative market share or audience size
- Label each company clearly

#### Step 5: Identify Groups and Interpret

- **Clusters** of companies positioned near each other form strategic groups
- **Distance between groups** indicates how different their strategies are
- **Mobility barriers** are the obstacles preventing companies from moving
  between groups (capital requirements, brand reputation, capabilities)
- **Within-group rivalry** is typically more intense than between-group rivalry

#### Step 6: Extract Strategic Insights

Analyze the completed map to determine:
- Which group does your brand belong to?
- Who are your closest competitors within your group?
- Are there attractive groups with less competition?
- What barriers would you need to overcome to reposition?
- Where are the gaps that represent genuine opportunities?

#### Step 7: Update Regularly

Strategic positions shift as companies evolve. Refresh the map at least quarterly
or when significant competitive moves occur.

### Industry Examples

| Industry | Axis 1 | Axis 2 | Typical Groups |
|----------|--------|--------|----------------|
| Automobiles | Price | Vehicle type | Economy, mid-range, luxury, electric-focused |
| Smartphones | Price | Tech innovation | Budget, mid-tier, flagship, ultra-premium |
| Airlines | Price | Route coverage | Low-cost domestic, full-service intl, regional |
| Restaurants | Price | Dining experience | Fast food, fast-casual, casual, fine dining |
| Personal brands | Content depth | Audience size | Micro-niche experts, broad educators, entertainers |

## 5. How to Choose Matrix Axes (Cross-Framework Guidance)

Both perceptual mapping and strategic group mapping depend on choosing the right axes.
This section provides consolidated guidance applicable to both.

### Principles for Axis Selection

1. **Independence:** The two axes should measure different things. If they are highly
   correlated (e.g., price and quality often move together), the map collapses into a
   diagonal line and reveals little.

2. **Differentiation power:** Good axes separate competitors into visibly distinct
   positions. If everyone lands in the same spot, try different dimensions.

3. **Strategic relevance:** Axes should represent factors that genuinely drive competitive
   advantage or customer decision-making in this specific industry.

4. **Measurability:** Prefer axes where you can reasonably assess each competitor's
   position, even if qualitatively.

5. **Actionability:** Choose dimensions where repositioning is possible — axes that
   represent strategic choices you can actually make.

### Process for Selecting Axes

1. **Brainstorm 8-12 candidate dimensions** relevant to the industry
2. **Eliminate redundant pairs** that measure the same underlying factor
3. **Score each dimension** on differentiation power (does it spread competitors out?)
4. **Check correlation** between your top candidates — choose pairs with low correlation
5. **Create 2-3 maps** with different axis pairs to capture multiple perspectives
6. **Validate with data** — adjust if the resulting map doesn't match market reality

### Common Pitfalls

- **Using correlated axes** — price and quality, audience size and revenue
- **Choosing internal metrics** instead of customer-facing attributes
- **Being too abstract** — axes should be concrete enough to assess
- **Using only one map** — a single map with two axes cannot capture the full picture;
  create multiple maps
- **Never updating** — axes that mattered last year may not matter this year

## 6. Combining the Frameworks

For comprehensive competitive landscape analysis, use the four frameworks in sequence:

| Step | Framework | What It Reveals |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | Porter's Five Forces | Industry-level competitive dynamics and attractiveness |
| 2 | Strategic Group Mapping | Who competes with whom and how strategies cluster |
| 3 | Perceptual Mapping | How customers perceive competitors and where gaps exist |
| 4 | SWOT Analysis | Internal strengths/weaknesses vs. external opportunities/threats |

This progression moves from macro (industry structure) to micro (your specific position),
ensuring nothing is overlooked.
