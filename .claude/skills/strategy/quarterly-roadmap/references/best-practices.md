## 1. OKR Methodology (Objectives and Key Results)

### What Are OKRs?

OKRs are a goal-setting framework where an **Objective** defines *what* you want
to achieve (qualitative, aspirational, directional), and **Key Results** define
*how* you will measure progress toward that objective (quantitative, specific,
time-bound).

### How to Write Good Objectives

- **Aspirational yet achievable** — stretch goals that inspire action, not
  paralyze with impossibility
- **Qualitative and directional** — describe a desired state, not a metric
  (metrics belong in Key Results)
- **Active voice, positive framing** — "Build a thriving community" not "Don't
  lose community members"
- **Short but complete** — one sentence that captures the intent
- **Tied to strategy** — every objective should trace back to a larger strategic
  priority
- **Limit to 3-5 per team per quarter** — more leads to dilution and lost focus

**Good objective examples:**
- "Become the go-to voice for [niche topic] on LinkedIn"
- "Build a sustainable content engine that runs without daily intervention"
- "Create a product launch experience that converts curious visitors into buyers"

**Bad objective examples:**
- "Increase followers by 20%" (this is a Key Result, not an Objective)
- "Do more content" (vague, no direction)
- "Be better at social media" (unmeasurable, unfocused)

### How to Write Good Key Results

- **Outcome-focused, not task-focused** — "Increase retention by 10%" not "Send
  follow-up emails"
- **Quantifiable with a specific metric** — every KR needs a number
- **2-4 Key Results per Objective** — enough to triangulate success, few enough
  to stay focused
- **Include a baseline and target** — "Grow email list from 2,000 to 5,000"
- **Mix leading and lagging indicators** — leading (actions taken) predict
  lagging (outcomes achieved)

**Key Result formula:**
```
[Verb] [metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [date]
```

**Examples:**
- "Increase average post engagement rate from 3.2% to 5.0% by end of Q2"
- "Publish 12 long-form articles (up from 4 last quarter)"
- "Generate 500 qualified leads from content (baseline: 180)"

### Two Types of OKRs

| Type | Ambition | Expected Completion | Use When |
|------|----------|-------------------|----------|
| **Committed** | Realistic stretch | 100% expected | Core business goals, must-haves |
| **Aspirational** (Moonshots) | Aggressive stretch | 70% = success | Innovation, growth experiments |

### OKR Review Cadence

- **Weekly or bi-weekly** check-ins to surface blockers early
- **Monthly** progress reviews with scoring (0.0-1.0 scale)
- **End-of-quarter** retrospective: what worked, what didn't, what to carry
  forward
- Treat OKRs as a **learning system**, not a grading system — the goal is
  insight, not punishment

### Common OKR Pitfalls

1. Setting too many OKRs (loss of focus)
2. Writing Key Results that are really tasks/activities
3. No alignment between individual, team, and company OKRs
4. Setting and forgetting — no regular check-ins
5. Making OKRs either too safe (sandbagging) or impossibly ambitious
6. Operating in silos without cross-functional visibility
7. Confusing OKRs with KPIs (KPIs track health; OKRs drive change)

## 2. Theme-Based Roadmapping

### What Is a Theme-Based Roadmap?

A theme-based roadmap organizes work around **strategic themes** rather than
individual tasks or features. Each theme represents a high-level area of focus
that multiple initiatives, campaigns, or projects contribute to.

### Why Themes Over Task Lists

- **Reduces cognitive overhead** — you think in 3-4 buckets, not 47 tasks
- **Enables flexibility** — the specific work under a theme can shift without
  changing the strategic direction
- **Improves communication** — stakeholders understand "why" without needing
  granular details
- **Supports prioritization** — when everything maps to a theme, orphaned tasks
  become visible

### How to Define Quarterly Themes

1. **Review strategic priorities** — what matters most this quarter?
2. **Look at constraints** — time, energy, budget, seasonal factors
3. **Group related work** — which initiatives naturally cluster together?
4. **Name the theme clearly** — it should be self-explanatory in 3-5 words
5. **Limit to 3-4 themes per quarter** — the discipline of choosing is the point

**Theme examples for a personal brand:**
- "Authority Building" — thought leadership content, speaking, guest posts
- "Community Activation" — engagement, events, partnerships
- "Revenue Engine" — product launch, sales funnels, monetization
- "Foundation & Systems" — tools, automations, processes

### Monthly Focus Within Themes

Each month within the quarter gets a primary theme emphasis:
- **Month 1:** Foundation/setup for the quarter's big bets
- **Month 2:** Execution peak — most campaigns and content ship here
- **Month 3:** Optimization, measurement, and transition planning for next quarter

This creates a natural rhythm: build, execute, reflect.

## 3. ADHD-Friendly Planning Methodology

### Core Principle: Work WITH the Brain, Not Against It

ADHD brains thrive on novelty, visible progress, and meaningful work. They
struggle with vague long-term goals, invisible progress, and arbitrary tasks.
Every planning decision should account for this.

### Smaller Milestones, Visible Progress

- **Break quarterly goals into 2-week milestones** — a 90-day goal feels
  abstract; a 14-day target feels actionable
- **Make progress visible** — use dashboards, checklists, or progress bars that
  show movement
- **Celebrate completions** — small wins build momentum; skipping celebration
  kills motivation
- **One or two big things per week** plus a handful of small tasks — this is the
  sustainable sweet spot

### Reducing Cognitive Overhead

- **Thought dumps before planning** — jot down everything swirling in your head,
  then organize. Externalizing clears working memory.
- **Decision fatigue prevention** — pre-decide recurring choices (content format,
  posting schedule, review day) so they don't consume daily energy
- **Template everything** — don't start from blank; use templates for content,
  planning, and reviews
- **Single source of truth** — one roadmap, one calendar, one dashboard. Multiple
  systems fragment attention.

### Capacity-Based Planning

- **Plan to 70% capacity** — leave 30% buffer for unexpected tasks, energy dips,
  and hyperfocus opportunities
- **Track energy, not just time** — some days are high-output; some are
  maintenance-only. Build the plan to accommodate both.
- **Internal deadlines before real ones** — set your own deadline 2-3 days early
  to create buffer without the crisis
- **Body doubling / accountability** — having someone (or something) that expects
  your output helps bridge the intention-action gap

### The "Good Enough" Principle

- Perfectionism is the enemy of ADHD productivity
- Define "done" criteria BEFORE starting — when the criteria are met, ship it
- 80% quality shipped beats 100% quality stuck in drafts
- Review and iterate next quarter rather than perfecting this quarter

### Sustainable Rhythm

- **Consistent daily routine** reduces decision fatigue
- **Peak hours for deep work** — identify when focus is strongest and protect
  those blocks
- **Strategic breaks** — Pomodoro (25 min work / 5 min break) or similar
  structured intervals
- **Weekly review ritual** — 30 minutes to assess what shipped, what stalled,
  and what to adjust

## 4. Cross-Department Dependency Mapping

### What Is a Dependency?

A dependency exists when one team's work cannot start, proceed, or finish without
input, output, or approval from another team.

### Four Dependency Types

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Finish-to-Start (FS)** | B can't start until A finishes | Content can't be designed until copy is written |
| **Start-to-Start (SS)** | B can start when A starts | Social promotion starts when content publishing starts |
| **Finish-to-Finish (FF)** | B can't finish until A finishes | Launch can't complete until all assets are ready |
| **Start-to-Finish (SF)** | B can finish when A starts | Old system retires when new system goes live |

### How to Map Dependencies

1. **List all initiatives** for the quarter across departments
2. **For each initiative, ask:** "What do I need from other teams? What do other
   teams need from me?"
3. **Classify each dependency** by type (FS, SS, FF, SF) and strength
   (hard blocker vs. nice-to-have)
4. **Visualize** using a dependency diagram, network chart, or simple table
5. **Identify critical path** — which dependency chains determine the overall
   timeline?
6. **Assign owners** — every dependency needs a person responsible for
   coordinating handoffs

### Dependency Risk Indicators

- **Red:** Hard blocker, no workaround, on the critical path
- **Yellow:** Blocker with a workaround, or not on the critical path
- **Green:** Soft dependency, can proceed independently if needed

### Best Practices

- Review dependencies at the start of each month, not just at planning time
- Build 1-week buffers between dependent handoffs
- Communicate dependency status in weekly check-ins
- When a dependency is at risk, escalate immediately — don't wait for the
  deadline

## 5. Risk Assessment and Mitigation

### Simple Risk Framework (Probability x Impact)

For each identified risk, score on two dimensions:

| | Low Impact | Medium Impact | High Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High Probability** | Monitor | Mitigate | Critical — plan now |
| **Medium Probability** | Accept | Mitigate | Mitigate |
| **Low Probability** | Accept | Monitor | Monitor |

### Common Quarterly Roadmap Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Overcommitment / scope creep | High | High | Plan to 70% capacity; say no to new themes mid-quarter |
| Key person unavailable | Medium | High | Document processes; cross-train on critical tasks |
| Content quality drops under volume | Medium | Medium | Define minimum quality bar; use templates |
| External event disrupts plan | Low | High | Build flexible themes that can pivot; keep 1 "swap" slot |
| Burnout / energy crash | Medium | High | Build recovery weeks; track energy trends |
| Dependency delays from other teams | Medium | Medium | Buffer handoff timelines; identify workarounds early |

### Mitigation Strategies

- **Avoid** — eliminate the risk by removing the activity
- **Mitigate** — reduce probability or impact through proactive planning
- **Transfer** — shift the risk to someone better positioned to handle it
- **Accept** — acknowledge and monitor, with a trigger point for action

## 6. Capacity Planning — Avoiding Overcommitment

### The Overcommitment Trap

The most common failure mode for quarterly roadmaps is trying to do too much.
Ambition without capacity awareness leads to abandoned initiatives, burnout, and
eroded trust in the planning process itself.

### Capacity Planning Principles

1. **Audit available hours realistically** — subtract meetings, admin, energy
   dips, and life from total hours. For a solo operator or small team, real
   deep-work hours are often 50-60% of "available" time.

2. **Size initiatives before committing** — estimate effort for each initiative
   in rough buckets:
   - **Small:** < 4 hours total
   - **Medium:** 4-16 hours total
   - **Large:** 16-40 hours total
   - **XL:** 40+ hours — consider breaking down or deferring

3. **Apply the 70% rule** — fill only 70% of estimated capacity. The remaining
   30% absorbs surprises, allows for quality, and prevents the "always behind"
   feeling.

4. **Prioritize ruthlessly** — use a simple framework:
   - **Must do** — contractual, deadline-driven, or strategically critical
   - **Should do** — important but flexible on timing
   - **Could do** — valuable if capacity allows
   - **Won't do this quarter** — explicitly deferred (this list is as important
     as the "do" list)

5. **One major initiative per month** — plus supporting work. Trying to run
   multiple large initiatives simultaneously is a recipe for all of them
   suffering.

6. **Build in recovery** — at least one lighter week per month for catch-up,
   reflection, and energy management.

### Quarterly Capacity Check Formula

```
Available weeks: 13 (minus holidays/vacation = X working weeks)
Deep work hours per week: ~20 (realistic for most people)
Total deep work hours: X * 20

Subtract:
- Recurring commitments (meetings, admin): ~30%
- Buffer for surprises: ~15%
- Recovery/lighter weeks: ~10%

Net available for new initiatives: ~45% of total = X * 9 hours

If your roadmap requires more hours than this, cut scope.
```

## 7. Putting It All Together — The Planning Sequence

1. **Set 3-5 OKRs** for the quarter (aspirational objectives, measurable key
   results)
2. **Define 3-4 themes** that group the work needed to achieve those OKRs
3. **Map themes to months** with a primary focus per month
4. **Break each theme into 2-week milestones** (ADHD-friendly chunks)
5. **Map dependencies** between departments/teams for each milestone
6. **Run a capacity check** — does the plan fit within 70% of available capacity?
7. **Identify and score risks** using the probability x impact matrix
8. **Define mitigation strategies** for medium and high risks
9. **Set the review cadence** — weekly check-ins, monthly reviews, end-of-quarter
   retro
10. **Write the "Won't Do" list** — explicitly state what you are choosing NOT to
    do this quarter
