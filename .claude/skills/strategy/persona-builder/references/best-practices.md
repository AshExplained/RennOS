# Persona Builder — Best Practices Reference

This reference consolidates proven frameworks for building audience personas. Read this before starting any persona work.

---

## 1. Jobs-to-be-Done Framework (JTBD)

JTBD reframes audience understanding from "who is the customer?" to "what job is the customer hiring a product/content/service to do?" People don't buy products — they hire them to make progress in their lives.

### The Three Job Dimensions

Every job has three layers. All three must be captured in a persona:

| Dimension | Definition | Example (Creator Audience) |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Functional** | The practical task they need accomplished | "Learn how to grow a newsletter from 0 to 10K subscribers" |
| **Emotional** | How they want to feel during and after | "Feel confident that I'm not wasting time on the wrong tactics" |
| **Social** | How they want to be perceived by others | "Be seen as someone who built something real, not just a hobbyist" |

### Writing Job Statements

Use this formula:

```
When I [situation/context], I want to [motivation/action], so I can [expected outcome].
```

Examples:
- "When I'm planning my content calendar, I want to know what topics actually resonate, so I can stop guessing and start growing."
- "When I'm considering a career pivot, I want to see proof that creators like me can make it work, so I can justify the risk to myself and my family."

### The Job Map

Break each job into stages to understand where the persona struggles:

1. **Define** — How does the persona define what they need?
2. **Locate** — Where do they search for solutions?
3. **Prepare** — What do they need before they can start?
4. **Confirm** — How do they validate they chose the right solution?
5. **Execute** — What does the actual process look like?
6. **Monitor** — How do they track progress?
7. **Modify** — What adjustments do they make along the way?
8. **Conclude** — How do they know the job is done?

### JTBD Interview Questions

When gathering data for personas, use these prompts:
- "Walk me through the last time you tried to solve this problem."
- "What did you try before you found [current solution]?"
- "What was the trigger that made you start looking?"
- "What almost stopped you from taking action?"
- "What would have to be true for you to switch to something different?"

### Real-World JTBD Examples

- **Airbnb**: The job isn't "book accommodation" — it's "experience a place like a local and feel a sense of belonging."
- **Spotify**: The job isn't "play music" — it's "have the right soundtrack for every moment without effort."
- **Substack**: The job isn't "send emails" — it's "build a direct relationship with my audience that I own."

## 2. Empathy Mapping Methodology

Empathy maps add emotional depth to personas by forcing you to consider four dimensions of a person's experience. They bridge the gap between data points and human understanding.

### The Four Quadrants

| Quadrant | What It Captures | How to Fill It |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Says** | Direct quotes — what they literally say in interviews, comments, reviews | Pull verbatim quotes from research. "I just want something that works." |
| **Thinks** | Internal thoughts — what they believe but may not say out loud | Infer from behavior, hesitations, contradictions. "Am I too late to start?" |
| **Does** | Observable actions — what they actually do (not what they say they do) | Track behavior: bookmarks 20 articles but reads 2, signs up but never logs in |
| **Feels** | Emotional states — adjective + context | "Overwhelmed: too many options, no clear path forward" |

### Additional Dimensions: Pains and Gains

Below the four quadrants, capture:
- **Pains**: Frustrations, obstacles, fears, risks they face
- **Gains**: Desired outcomes, measures of success, what "winning" looks like

### Six-Step Empathy Mapping Process

1. **Define scope** — Which persona or audience segment are you mapping?
2. **Gather research** — Pull from interviews, surveys, analytics, social listening, support tickets
3. **Individual brainstorm** — Each team member populates quadrants independently (avoids groupthink)
4. **Converge and cluster** — Group similar notes, identify themes and contradictions
5. **Identify gaps** — Where do you lack data? Flag for follow-up research
6. **Synthesize** — Distill into the persona profile

### When to Use Empathy Maps

- At the start of persona creation to organize raw research
- To pressure-test an existing persona — "Do we actually know what they think and feel, or are we guessing?"
- When a persona feels flat or generic — the empathy map forces specificity

## 3. Extracting Personas: Data vs. Interviews

There are two valid approaches. Use both when possible.

### Data-Driven Personas

Built from quantitative signals:
- **Analytics**: Platform demographics, engagement patterns, content performance
- **CRM data**: Purchase history, lifecycle stage, churn patterns
- **Social listening**: Topics they discuss, hashtags they use, accounts they follow
- **Survey data**: Self-reported preferences, pain points, goals

**Strengths**: Scalable, objective, reveals actual behavior (not stated behavior)
**Weaknesses**: Tells you what people do, not why. Lacks emotional context.

### Interview-Driven Personas

Built from qualitative conversations:
- **1-on-1 interviews**: Deep understanding of motivations, triggers, and context
- **Focus groups**: Surface social dynamics and shared language
- **Customer support transcripts**: Real problems in their own words
- **Community observation**: How they talk when they don't know you're listening

**Strengths**: Rich context, emotional depth, reveals the "why"
**Weaknesses**: Small sample sizes, self-reporting bias, time-intensive

### The Hybrid Approach (Recommended)

1. Start with data to identify behavioral clusters (who are the segments?)
2. Conduct interviews within each cluster to understand motivations (why do they do what they do?)
3. Validate interview insights against data patterns (does the data support what they told us?)
4. Build the persona combining both — data for the "what," interviews for the "why"

## 4. Common Persona Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Too Generic
**Symptom**: "Sarah, 28, lives in a city, uses Instagram, wants to grow her career."
**Fix**: Add behavioral specificity. What does she do at 11pm when she can't sleep? What podcast episode changed her mind about something? What's the last thing she screenshot on her phone?

### Mistake 2: Too Many Personas
**Symptom**: 8-12 personas that nobody can remember or use.
**Fix**: Limit to 3-5 personas maximum. 1-3 primary, 1-2 secondary. Each persona must imply a distinct content strategy or value proposition. If two personas would receive the same content — merge them.

### Mistake 3: Fictional Demographics Over Real Behavior
**Symptom**: The persona has a specific age (34), salary ($78K), and car (Honda Civic) but no insight into decision-making patterns.
**Fix**: Demographics are the least important part. Lead with behavior, motivation, and context. Use age ranges, not exact ages. Focus on what they do, not who they theoretically are.

### Mistake 4: Set-and-Forget
**Symptom**: Personas created once, never updated, now 18 months old and irrelevant.
**Fix**: Review personas quarterly. Feed in new data from analytics, feedback, and market shifts. Personas are living documents.

### Mistake 5: No Validation
**Symptom**: The team agrees the persona "feels right" but has never tested it.
**Fix**: Use validation techniques (see Section 6 below). A persona that hasn't been validated is just a hypothesis.

### Mistake 6: Created in Isolation
**Symptom**: Marketing builds personas without input from sales, support, or product.
**Fix**: Cross-functional input is essential. Sales knows objections. Support knows frustrations. Product knows usage patterns. Each adds a dimension that research alone can miss.

### Mistake 7: Persona Without a Job
**Symptom**: You know who they are but not what they're trying to accomplish.
**Fix**: Every persona must have at least one clear JTBD. Without it, the persona is a portrait with no purpose.

## 5. Behavioral Segmentation Principles

Behavioral segmentation groups audiences by what they do rather than who they are. It produces more actionable personas than demographic segmentation alone.

### Six Behavioral Dimensions

| Dimension | What It Captures | Example |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Cognitive Style** | How they process information | Analytical (wants data, proof) vs. Intuitive (wants stories, vision) |
| **Decision Triggers** | What shifts them from passive to active | Urgency, peer adoption, pain threshold, opportunity cost |
| **Motivational Anchors** | Core drivers beneath surface goals | Achievement, autonomy, affiliation, purpose, security |
| **Risk Orientation** | How they handle uncertainty | Risk-seeking (early adopter) vs. risk-averse (needs social proof) |
| **Environmental Pressure** | External forces shaping their choices | Financial constraints, career pressure, family obligations, industry trends |
| **Role Dynamics** | Their position in the decision process | Initiator, influencer, decision-maker, buyer, end-user |

### Behavioral Data Sources

- Website/app analytics (pages visited, time on page, drop-off points)
- Email engagement (open rates, click patterns, reply behavior)
- Content consumption (topics, formats, depth, frequency)
- Purchase behavior (what they buy, when, how often, average order value)
- Community behavior (lurker vs. commenter vs. contributor)
- Social media behavior (share vs. like vs. save patterns)

### Segmentation Process

1. **Collect behavioral data** from all available touchpoints
2. **Identify clusters** — groups that behave similarly across multiple dimensions
3. **Name and characterize** each cluster based on dominant behaviors
4. **Layer in qualitative data** — interviews, quotes, motivations
5. **Map clusters to JTBD** — what job is each cluster hiring you for?
6. **Validate with outcomes** — do these segments predict real behavior (purchases, engagement, churn)?

## 6. Persona Validation Techniques

A persona is a hypothesis until validated. Use these methods:

### Technique 1: Data Back-Testing
Take your persona's predicted behavior and check it against actual analytics. If "The Aspiring Creator" supposedly prefers long-form content, does your long-form content actually outperform short-form with that segment?

### Technique 2: Blind Matching
Show real customer data (anonymized) to team members and ask them to match each customer to a persona. If matching is inconsistent, the personas lack distinctive clarity.

### Technique 3: Predictive Testing
Use personas to predict how a segment will respond to new content, offers, or features. Run the experiment. Compare prediction to outcome. Refine.

### Technique 4: Customer Interviews
Present the persona (without the name) to real customers in that segment. Ask: "Does this sound like you?" Note where they agree, push back, or add nuance.

### Technique 5: A/B Testing
Create content or messaging tailored to a specific persona. Test it against generic content with that segment. If persona-targeted content doesn't outperform, the persona needs work.

### Technique 6: Stakeholder Review
Share personas with sales, support, and community managers — the people who talk to customers daily. They will immediately flag what rings true and what feels off.

### Validation Cadence

- **Initial validation**: Within 2 weeks of creation
- **Ongoing validation**: Quarterly review against fresh data
- **Trigger-based review**: After major market shifts, product launches, or audience growth milestones
