# FTC Disclosure Best Practices Reference

> Last updated: 2026-03-24
> For use by the disclosure-check agent when auditing content for compliance.

---

## 1. FTC Disclosure Rules — Core Principles

### 1.1 Material Connection

A "material connection" is any relationship between an endorser and a brand that could affect the credibility of the endorsement. The FTC requires disclosure of ALL material connections, including:

- **Financial compensation** — Cash payment for a post, video, or mention
- **Free or discounted products** — Gifted items, PR packages, review units
- **Affiliate relationships** — Commission-based links or codes
- **Employment or ambassador status** — Brand ambassador, employee, consultant
- **Ownership or equity** — Ownership stake, equity, stock options in the company
- **Family or personal relationships** — Family member works at or owns the brand
- **Special access or perks** — Early access, VIP experiences, event invitations
- **Barter arrangements** — Services exchanged for products or exposure

**Key rule:** If ANY form of value was exchanged — whether or not a formal contract exists — disclosure is required.

### 1.2 Clear and Conspicuous Standard

The FTC's "clear and conspicuous" standard requires that a reasonable consumer must be able to notice AND understand the disclosure. This means:

| Requirement | What It Means |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Prominent placement** | Before the fold, above "more" / "see more" buttons, not buried in hashtags |
| **Plain language** | Normal words a consumer understands, not abbreviations or jargon |
| **Same medium** | Visual disclosure for visual content, spoken disclosure for audio content |
| **Sufficient duration** | On screen long enough to read; spoken clearly enough to hear |
| **Adequate contrast** | Readable font size, contrasting colors, not obscured by other elements |
| **Proximity** | Near the endorsement claim, not on a separate page or buried in comments |
| **Unavoidable** | The average person cannot miss it — it must be encountered before engaging with content |

**The test:** Would someone scrolling at normal speed, watching at normal playback, or listening casually clearly understand that this is sponsored/paid/affiliate content?

### 1.3 Who Is Liable

Both the **influencer/creator** AND the **brand/advertiser** are liable for non-compliance. Brands must:

- Include explicit disclosure requirements in creator briefs
- Provide examples of compliant disclosures
- Monitor published content for compliance
- Include audit rights and withhold-payment clauses in contracts

## 2. Platform-Specific Placement Requirements

### 2.1 Instagram (Posts, Stories, Reels)

**Posts:**
- Use Instagram's built-in "Paid Partnership" label AND include disclosure in caption text
- Disclosure must appear in the **first line** of the caption — before the "more" cutoff
- Platform label alone is NOT sufficient per FTC guidance
- Acceptable caption formats:
  - `#ad | Partnered with [Brand] on this post`
  - `Sponsored by [Brand]. [Rest of caption]`

**Stories:**
- Place disclosure as a text overlay on the **first frame**
- Use a readable font size and contrasting color
- Superimpose text so it stays visible for the full duration of the story frame
- Do NOT rely on small text in a corner

**Reels:**
- Disclosure must appear **in the video itself** (on-screen text), not just in the caption
- State it verbally if there is spoken audio
- On-screen text overlay in the opening 2-3 seconds
- Also include disclosure in the caption as a secondary layer

**Live:**
- State disclosure verbally at the beginning
- Repeat periodically for viewers joining late
- Pin a comment with the disclosure if the platform allows

### 2.2 YouTube (Videos, Shorts, Live)

**Long-form videos:**
- Enable YouTube's "Includes paid promotion" checkbox (adds banner in first 10 seconds)
- **Verbal disclosure within the first 30 seconds** of the video — do not wait until mid-roll
- Include written disclosure in the video description **above the fold** (before "Show more")
- For affiliate content, pin a comment with the affiliate disclosure

**YouTube Shorts:**
- On-screen text overlay in the first 2-3 seconds
- Verbal mention if audio is present
- Include disclosure in the description

**YouTube Live:**
- Verbal disclosure at the start
- Repeat every 15-20 minutes for new viewers joining
- Include in the stream description
- Use on-screen graphics/banners periodically

### 2.3 TikTok (Videos, Lives)

**Videos:**
- Use TikTok's built-in **"Commercial Content Disclosure" toggle** (adds "Paid partnership" label)
- Add on-screen text overlay in the **opening 2-3 seconds**
- Include #ad or #sponsored in the caption in the first two lines
- Verbal disclosure if there is spoken audio
- Platform toggle alone is NOT sufficient

**TikTok LIVE:**
- Verbal disclosure at the start
- Repeat periodically as new viewers join
- Use both visual and verbal elements since some viewers watch without sound

### 2.4 Twitter/X (Posts, Threads, Spaces)

**Tweets:**
- Include disclosure **in the tweet text itself** — not just via links
- Due to character limits, acceptable short-form terms include:
  - `#ad`
  - `[Brand] Partner`
  - `[Brand] Ambassador`
  - `Sponsored`
- Place at the beginning of the tweet, not the end

**Threads:**
- Include disclosure in the **first tweet** of the thread
- Repeat in subsequent tweets if they contain separate endorsements

**X Spaces (audio):**
- Verbal disclosure at the beginning
- Repeat periodically for late joiners

### 2.5 Blog Posts & Articles

- Place disclosure **at the top of the article**, before any product mentions or affiliate links
- Use a clearly formatted disclosure box or bold/highlighted text
- Do NOT bury it in a sidebar, footer, or "About" page
- Each individual blog post needs its own disclosure — a site-wide disclosure page is NOT sufficient
- For affiliate content, disclose near each affiliate link or in a prominent header statement
- Example placement:
  ```
  DISCLOSURE: This post is sponsored by [Brand]. I received compensation for this review.
  All opinions are my own.
  ```

### 2.6 Podcasts & Audio Content

- **Spoken disclosure at the beginning** of the episode, before sponsored content
- Repeat at the **end of the episode** for listeners who skip ahead
- For longer episodes (30+ minutes), repeat disclosure before each sponsored segment
- Include written disclosure in the **show notes / episode description**
- The spoken disclosure must clearly state the nature of the relationship:
  - "This episode is sponsored by [Brand]"
  - "I received [product] from [Brand] to review"
  - "[Brand] is paying me to talk about this product"
- Pre-roll ad reads must be distinguishable from editorial content

## 3. Acceptable vs. Unacceptable Disclosure Language

### 3.1 Acceptable Language

These terms and phrases meet FTC requirements when placed prominently:

| Term / Phrase | Context |
|---------------|---------|
| `#ad` | Social media posts — must be at the START, not buried in hashtags |
| `#sponsored` | Social media posts — prominent placement required |
| `Ad` or `Advertisement` | Any format |
| `Sponsored by [Brand]` | Any format |
| `Paid partnership with [Brand]` | Any format |
| `[Brand] paid me to create this` | Any format — very clear |
| `I received this product for free from [Brand]` | Gifted product reviews |
| `Thanks to [Brand] for sponsoring this [post/video/episode]` | Any format — natural and clear |
| `This post contains affiliate links — I earn a commission if you purchase` | Affiliate content |
| `Paid Brand Ambassador for [Brand]` | Ambassador relationships |
| `I own stock in / have an equity stake in [Brand]` | Ownership relationships |
| `[Brand] is a client of mine` | Service-provider relationships |

### 3.2 Unacceptable Language

These terms are too vague, ambiguous, or misleading and DO NOT meet FTC requirements:

| Term / Phrase | Why It Fails |
|---------------|-------------|
| `#sp` | Abbreviation — consumers don't understand it |
| `#spon` | Abbreviation — not clear enough |
| `#collab` | Ambiguous — could mean unpaid collaboration |
| `#partner` (alone) | Ambiguous — does not clarify financial relationship |
| `#ambassador` (alone) | Ambiguous — must specify "Paid Brand Ambassador" |
| `Thanks [Brand]!` (alone) | Does not explain the nature of the relationship |
| `#blessed` | Does not disclose anything |
| `Made possible by [Brand]` | Too vague about the financial arrangement |
| Disclosure only in bio or "About" page | Not proximate to the endorsement |
| Disclosure only in comments | Easily missed, not prominent |
| Disclosure after 15+ lines of caption | Buried — not above the fold |
| Disclosure only via platform label | FTC says platform tools alone are insufficient |
| Mixing disclosure into a cluster of hashtags | Buried and easily missed |

### 3.3 Gray Area — Use with Caution

| Term / Phrase | Guidance |
|---------------|----------|
| `[Brand] Partner` | Acceptable on character-limited platforms (Twitter/X) but add context when possible |
| `[Brand] Ambassador` | Must include "Paid" qualifier — "Paid Brand Ambassador" |
| `Gifted` or `#gifted` | Acceptable only if it is truly a no-strings gift; if any obligation exists, use "Sponsored" |
| Platform "Paid Partnership" labels | Use as a supplement, NEVER as the sole disclosure |

## 4. Common Violations and Penalties

### 4.1 Common Violations

1. **Buried disclosures** — Placing #ad at the end of a long caption or in a cluster of hashtags
2. **Relying solely on platform tools** — Using only Instagram's "Paid Partnership" label without caption disclosure
3. **Vague language** — Using #collab, #partner, or "thanks" without clarifying the financial relationship
4. **Missing disclosures entirely** — No disclosure at all on sponsored content
5. **Disclosure in wrong medium** — Text-only disclosure on a video where viewers may not read captions
6. **Insufficient duration** — On-screen text that flashes too quickly to read
7. **Unsubstantiated claims** — Making health, performance, or results claims without evidence
8. **Endorsing unused products** — Promoting products the endorser has never actually used
9. **Undisclosed affiliate links** — Sharing affiliate links or discount codes without revealing the commission relationship
10. **AI-generated endorsements** — Using AI-generated content or virtual influencers without disclosing both the sponsorship AND the AI involvement

### 4.2 Penalty Structure

| Severity | Consequence |
|----------|-------------|
| **First offense / minor** | FTC warning letter requiring corrective action |
| **Repeated violations** | Civil penalties of **up to $51,744 per violation** (adjusted annually for inflation) |
| **Deceptive health/safety claims** | Higher penalties, potential injunctive relief, mandatory customer refunds |
| **Systematic deception** | Consent orders, ongoing FTC monitoring, public disclosure of violations |

### 4.3 Notable Enforcement Cases

**Google & iHeartMedia (2022)** — $9.4 million settlement for broadcasting nearly 29,000 deceptive endorsements about the Pixel 4 phone, where influencers had not actually used the product.

**Teami LLC (2020)** — $930,000 in customer refunds ordered after influencers made unsubstantiated health claims about detox teas on Instagram with inadequate disclosures.

**CSGO Lotto (2017)** — FTC settlement after two influencers promoted an online gambling platform they owned without disclosing their ownership interest.

**Sunday Riley (2019)** — Skincare company settled FTC charges for posting fake reviews and directing employees to write positive reviews on Sephora's website.

**Fashion Nova (2022)** — $4.2 million settlement for suppressing negative reviews, misleading consumers about product quality.

**UrthBox (2019)** — Company encouraged users to post positive reviews in exchange for free snack boxes without requiring disclosure of the material connection.

### 4.4 Enforcement Trends (2024-2026)

- **Increased scrutiny of health, finance, and crypto content** — Higher enforcement priority
- **Short-form video focus** — FTC updated guidance specifically for TikTok and Reels disclosures
- **AI-generated content** — Virtual influencers and AI content now require dual disclosure (sponsorship + AI)
- **Warning letter volume** — Over 150 warning letters issued to influencers in 2025 alone
- **Child-directed content** — Heightened enforcement for toy and family creators on YouTube
- **Negative influencing** — "De-influencing" content that disparages competitors on behalf of a paying brand also requires disclosure

## 5. Quick-Reference Compliance Checklist

Use this checklist when auditing any piece of content:

- [ ] **Material connection identified** — What is the relationship? (paid, gifted, affiliate, ownership, etc.)
- [ ] **Disclosure present** — Is there an explicit disclosure?
- [ ] **Clear language used** — Does it use approved terms? (ad, sponsored, paid partnership)
- [ ] **Prominent placement** — Is it before the fold / above "more" / in the first frame?
- [ ] **Correct medium** — Spoken for audio/video, written for text, both for video
- [ ] **Sufficient duration** — On screen long enough to read / spoken clearly
- [ ] **Platform tool used** — Paid Partnership label, commercial content toggle, paid promotion checkbox
- [ ] **Platform tool supplemented** — Additional manual disclosure beyond just the platform tool
- [ ] **No unsubstantiated claims** — Are product claims truthful and backed by evidence?
- [ ] **Results claims qualified** — Are any results/performance claims accompanied by appropriate disclaimers?
- [ ] **AI disclosure if applicable** — Is AI-generated content disclosed alongside sponsorship?
