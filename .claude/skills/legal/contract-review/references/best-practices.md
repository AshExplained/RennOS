# Contract Review Best Practices

> Reference document for the contract-review skill.
> Last updated: 2026-03-24

---

## 1. Common Contract Red Flags by Deal Type

### Sponsorship Agreements
- **Vague deliverables** — "promotional content in formats to be reasonably determined by the Sponsor" leaves scope open-ended
- **Unlimited content usage rights** — brand can repurpose your content forever, on any channel, including paid ads
- **Post-term exclusivity** — non-compete extends months or years after the deal ends
- **One-sided termination** — brand can terminate at will; creator cannot
- **No performance metrics** — success criteria undefined, giving the brand subjective veto over deliverables
- **Missing FTC disclosure requirements** — no mention of mandatory #ad or #sponsored tags
- **Payment contingent on brand approval** — "Sponsor reserves the right to withhold payment if content does not meet brand standards" with no objective criteria
- **Auto-renewal without opt-out** — contract silently renews without renegotiation

### Partnership Agreements
- **Unequal revenue splits without justification** — disproportionate share with no clear rationale
- **IP assignment without compensation** — transferring IP rights beyond the scope of the partnership
- **Unlimited non-compete scope** — prevents working in entire industries, not just with direct competitors
- **No exit mechanism** — no buyout clause or dissolution procedure
- **Joint liability without joint control** — liable for partner's actions but no oversight rights
- **Missing governance structure** — no decision-making framework for disputes between partners

### Freelance / Service Agreements
- **Overly broad IP assignment** — "all work product, including pre-existing IP, becomes client property"
- **Unlimited liability** — freelancer liable for consequential damages far exceeding contract value
- **Scope creep provisions** — no change order process; client can add work without adjusting compensation
- **Payment delays** — net-60 or net-90 terms, or payment only upon "final acceptance" with no deadline
- **Non-compete clauses** — preventing work with other clients in the same industry for years
- **Kill fee absence** — no compensation if project is cancelled mid-execution
- **Work-for-hire without premium** — all rights transferred at base rate with no usage licensing

### Licensing Agreements
- **Perpetual, irrevocable license** — licensor loses all control over use permanently
- **Sublicensing rights without consent** — licensee can grant rights to third parties
- **No quality control provisions** — licensee can use IP in ways that damage brand reputation
- **Audit rights missing** — no ability to verify royalty calculations
- **Territory creep** — "worldwide" when deal was discussed as regional
- **No minimum guarantees** — licensee pays nothing if they never use the license

### SaaS / Technology Agreements
- **Broad data usage rights** — "Customer grants Company a worldwide, royalty-free license to use Customer data to improve services"
- **No data portability** — no export mechanism or API access to your own data upon termination
- **Unilateral price increases** — vendor can raise prices 5-10% annually with no cap
- **Auto-renewal with narrow exit windows** — 60-120 day notice required; missing by one day locks you in
- **Liability cap below potential damages** — vendor liability capped at one month's fees
- **No SLA commitments** — no uptime guarantees or remedies for service failures
- **Vendor can change terms unilaterally** — "Company may modify these terms at any time with 30 days notice"
- **No data deletion upon termination** — vendor retains your data indefinitely

## 2. Missing Clauses Checklist

Every contract should be checked for these clauses. A missing clause is itself a red flag.

### Termination
- [ ] Mutual termination rights (both parties can exit)
- [ ] Termination for cause (material breach, insolvency, force majeure)
- [ ] Termination for convenience (with notice period)
- [ ] Notice period specified (typically 30-60 days)
- [ ] Wind-down obligations (transition, handover, final deliverables)
- [ ] Survival clauses (which obligations persist after termination)

### Intellectual Property
- [ ] Ownership clearly assigned (who owns what)
- [ ] License scope defined (exclusive vs non-exclusive, territory, duration)
- [ ] Pre-existing IP carved out (creator retains rights to prior work)
- [ ] Derivative works addressed
- [ ] Usage rights limitations (platforms, formats, modifications)
- [ ] Work-for-hire designation if applicable

### Liability
- [ ] Liability cap specified (typically 1x-2x annual contract value)
- [ ] Mutual limitation of liability (not one-sided)
- [ ] Exclusion of consequential/indirect damages
- [ ] Carve-outs for willful misconduct, gross negligence, IP infringement
- [ ] Insurance requirements

### Exclusivity
- [ ] Scope narrowly defined (specific competitors, not entire industries)
- [ ] Duration limited (during contract term only, or minimal post-term)
- [ ] Territory specified
- [ ] Compensation proportional to exclusivity scope
- [ ] Competitor list defined or definable

### Payment
- [ ] Amount and currency specified
- [ ] Payment schedule with dates (not vague milestones)
- [ ] Late payment penalties / interest
- [ ] Invoice procedure defined
- [ ] Kill fee / cancellation compensation
- [ ] Expense reimbursement terms
- [ ] Payment method specified

### Dispute Resolution
- [ ] Governing law specified
- [ ] Jurisdiction / venue defined
- [ ] Escalation procedure (negotiation before litigation)
- [ ] Mediation or arbitration clause
- [ ] Prevailing party attorney's fees
- [ ] Statute of limitations

### Force Majeure
- [ ] Definition of qualifying events
- [ ] Notice requirements
- [ ] Performance suspension vs termination rights
- [ ] Duration limits (how long before either party can exit)

### Confidentiality
- [ ] Definition of confidential information
- [ ] Exclusions (publicly known, independently developed)
- [ ] Duration of obligations (typically 2-5 years, or indefinite for trade secrets)
- [ ] Permitted disclosures (legal requirements, advisors)
- [ ] Return/destruction of materials upon termination

### Indemnification
- [ ] Mutual vs one-sided (should be mutual or balanced)
- [ ] Scope defined (IP infringement, third-party claims, breach)
- [ ] Cap on indemnification obligations
- [ ] Notice and defense procedures
- [ ] Relationship to liability cap

## 3. Unfavorable Terms — Bad vs Acceptable Language

### Payment Terms

**Bad:**
> "Payment shall be made within 60 business days of Sponsor's final approval of all deliverables."

**Why it is bad:** 60 business days (~12 weeks) is excessive, and "final approval" is subjective with no deadline.

**Acceptable:**
> "Payment of $[amount] shall be made within 30 calendar days of invoice date. Late payments accrue interest at 1.5% per month."

---

### Exclusivity

**Bad:**
> "Creator shall not engage in any collaboration with any company in health, wellness, fitness, or lifestyle sectors for 12 months following expiration of this Agreement."

**Why it is bad:** Overly broad categories, extends well beyond contract term, blocks unrelated partnerships.

**Acceptable:**
> "During the Term, Creator shall not promote [specific competitor names] or their products. No post-term exclusivity applies."

---

### Content Ownership

**Bad:**
> "All content created under this Agreement shall become the sole property of the Sponsor, with a perpetual, worldwide, irrevocable license to modify, adapt, publish, and distribute."

**Why it is bad:** Creator loses all rights to their own work permanently; brand can modify and use forever.

**Acceptable:**
> "Creator retains ownership of all content. Sponsor is granted a non-exclusive license to use content on [specified platforms] for [12 months] from publication date. Modifications require Creator's prior written consent."

---

### Termination

**Bad:**
> "Early termination by the Influencer is not permitted. Sponsor may terminate at any time without penalty or notice."

**Why it is bad:** Completely one-sided; creator is locked in while brand can walk away freely.

**Acceptable:**
> "Either party may terminate this Agreement with 30 days' written notice. If Sponsor terminates without cause, Creator shall be compensated for all completed and in-progress deliverables."

---

### Liability

**Bad:**
> "Contractor shall indemnify and hold harmless Client from any and all claims, damages, and expenses, without limitation."

**Why it is bad:** Unlimited liability exposure; a single issue could result in damages far exceeding the contract value.

**Acceptable:**
> "Each party's total aggregate liability shall not exceed the total fees paid or payable under this Agreement. Neither party shall be liable for indirect, incidental, or consequential damages."

---

### Scope of Work

**Bad:**
> "Contractor shall perform such services as may be reasonably requested by Client from time to time."

**Why it is bad:** No defined scope; unlimited work for fixed pay. Classic scope creep enabler.

**Acceptable:**
> "Contractor shall deliver the specific deliverables listed in Exhibit A. Additional work requires a written change order signed by both parties, with adjusted compensation."

---

### Data Usage (SaaS)

**Bad:**
> "Customer grants Company a worldwide, royalty-free, perpetual license to use, analyze, and distribute Customer Data for any purpose including improving services and developing new products."

**Why it is bad:** Vendor can use your data to build competing products or sell insights to third parties.

**Acceptable:**
> "Company may use Customer Data solely to provide the contracted services. Aggregated, anonymized data may be used for service improvement only. Customer Data remains Customer's property."

---

### Non-Compete (Freelance)

**Bad:**
> "Contractor agrees not to provide similar services to any other client in the technology industry for a period of 24 months following termination."

**Why it is bad:** Effectively prevents the freelancer from working in their field for two years.

**Acceptable:**
> "During the Term, Contractor shall not provide services to [named direct competitors]. This restriction expires upon termination of the Agreement."

---

### Auto-Renewal

**Bad:**
> "This Agreement shall automatically renew for successive one-year periods unless either party provides written notice of non-renewal at least 90 days prior to the expiration date."

**Why it is bad:** Easy to miss the narrow opt-out window; silent renewal at potentially outdated terms.

**Acceptable:**
> "This Agreement expires on [date]. Renewal requires written agreement by both parties with renegotiated terms. Either party will provide 30 days' notice of intent to renew or not renew."

## 4. Risk Rating Methodology

### HIGH Risk — Walk Away or Major Renegotiation Required

The contract scores HIGH if **any** of the following are true:

- Unlimited or uncapped liability exposure
- Full IP assignment with no compensation or reversion rights
- Post-term non-compete exceeding 6 months or spanning broad industries
- One-sided termination (other party can exit freely; you cannot)
- No payment terms, or payment contingent on subjective approval with no timeline
- Missing 3+ clauses from the Missing Clauses Checklist (Section 2)
- Regulatory non-compliance (missing FTC disclosure, GDPR/CCPA violations)
- Indemnification is one-sided and uncapped
- Auto-renewal with no practical exit mechanism
- Perpetual, irrevocable content/IP license to the other party
- Contract value is significantly below market rate for the scope demanded
- Multiple red flags present simultaneously

**Verdict guidance:** Walk away unless the other party agrees to substantial renegotiation. Never sign a HIGH-risk contract without attorney review.

### MEDIUM Risk — Negotiate Before Signing

The contract scores MEDIUM if **any** of the following are true:

- Liability cap exists but is disproportionately low relative to risk
- Exclusivity is present but somewhat broader than necessary
- Payment terms are slow (net-45+) but otherwise clear
- 1-2 clauses from the Missing Clauses Checklist are absent
- Ambiguous language in key sections (scope, deliverables, acceptance criteria)
- Termination provisions exist but favor one party
- IP license scope is broader than discussed but not perpetual/irrevocable
- Auto-renewal with a reasonable (30-day) opt-out window
- Minor regulatory gaps (e.g., disclosure language present but incomplete)
- Terms are below market rate but within 20% of fair value

**Verdict guidance:** Negotiate the flagged items. Most MEDIUM-risk contracts can become acceptable with targeted amendments. Obtain a redline/markup.

### LOW Risk — Acceptable to Sign (with minor notes)

The contract scores LOW when:

- All critical clauses are present and balanced
- Liability is mutually capped at a reasonable level (1x-2x contract value)
- IP terms are clear and fair to both parties
- Payment terms are net-30 or better with clear schedule
- Exclusivity is narrowly scoped or absent
- Termination rights are mutual with reasonable notice periods
- Regulatory compliance addressed (FTC, GDPR/CCPA as applicable)
- No more than 2 minor issues (cosmetic, easily fixable)
- Terms align with market rates and industry standards

**Verdict guidance:** Sign as-is or with minor clarifications. Flag any remaining notes for the record.

## 5. Industry-Standard Terms by Contract Type

### Sponsorship / Brand Deals (Creator Side)
- **Payment:** 50% upfront, 50% on delivery (or net-15 to net-30)
- **Exclusivity:** Limited to named direct competitors, during contract term only
- **Content rights:** Creator retains ownership; brand gets limited license (specific platforms, 6-12 months)
- **Usage in paid ads (whitelisting):** Separate fee, typically 20-50% of base rate, with defined duration
- **Revisions:** 1-2 rounds included; additional rounds at additional cost
- **Cancellation:** Kill fee of 25-50% of total if brand cancels after briefing
- **FTC compliance:** Both parties responsible for disclosure; contract specifies disclosure format
- **Approval process:** Brand has 48-72 hours to approve content; silence = approval

### Partnership / Joint Venture
- **Revenue split:** Proportional to investment/contribution, documented in writing
- **Decision-making:** Defined governance (majority vote, unanimous for major decisions)
- **Exit mechanism:** Buyout formula, right of first refusal, dissolution procedure
- **Non-compete:** During term + 6-12 months, limited to direct competition
- **Audit rights:** Both parties can audit books annually
- **Capital calls:** Process and limits defined

### Freelance / Service Agreements
- **Payment:** Milestone-based or net-30; deposit of 25-50% upfront for new clients
- **Scope:** Detailed SOW with change order process
- **IP:** Creator retains rights until final payment; then transfers as specified
- **Liability:** Capped at total contract value
- **Kill fee:** 25-50% of remaining contract value
- **Revisions:** 2-3 rounds included; hourly rate for additional

### Licensing
- **Royalty rates:** Industry-dependent; typically 5-15% of net revenue
- **Minimum guarantees:** Annual minimums protect licensor
- **Quality control:** Licensor approval rights on all uses
- **Territory and channel:** Explicitly defined, not "worldwide, all channels"
- **Audit rights:** Licensor can audit licensee's books annually
- **Term:** 1-3 years with renewal options, not perpetual

### SaaS / Technology
- **Uptime SLA:** 99.9% minimum with service credits for downtime
- **Data ownership:** Customer owns all data; vendor is processor only
- **Data portability:** Export in standard formats at any time
- **Price locks:** Fixed pricing for initial term; capped annual increases (3-5%)
- **Termination:** 30-day notice; pro-rata refund of prepaid amounts
- **Data deletion:** Within 30 days of termination, with certification
- **Security:** SOC 2 Type II compliance minimum; breach notification within 72 hours

## 6. Creator / Influencer-Specific Contract Pitfalls

### Content Rights Traps
1. **Perpetual license disguised as "partnership"** — Brand frames the deal as collaborative but takes permanent content rights
2. **Whitelisting without compensation** — Brand runs paid ads using your content/likeness without additional payment
3. **Modification rights** — Brand can edit your content, change your message, or use it out of context
4. **Portfolio restrictions** — You cannot show the work in your own portfolio or case studies

### Compensation Pitfalls
5. **"Exposure" as payment** — No monetary compensation; just "brand visibility"
6. **Pay-per-performance only** — Entire compensation tied to metrics you cannot fully control (algorithm changes, etc.)
7. **Rate erosion** — Multi-post package priced below per-post market rate without volume justification
8. **Hidden costs** — Creator expected to cover production, props, location, travel at own expense
9. **No usage fee for extensions** — Brand extends campaign using your content without additional compensation

### Scope and Creative Control
10. **Unlimited revisions** — No cap on revision rounds; brand can demand endless changes
11. **Mandatory messaging** — Scripted talking points that sound inauthentic to your audience
12. **Approval on unrelated content** — Brand must approve your organic (non-sponsored) posts during the term
13. **Platform expansion** — Contract says "Instagram" but brand expects TikTok, YouTube, blog posts, etc.

### Compliance and Liability
14. **Creator bears all FTC liability** — Contract makes you solely responsible for disclosure compliance
15. **Product liability transfer** — You become liable for product claims or harm caused by the product you promoted
16. **Morality clause without mutuality** — Brand can terminate you for "reputational damage" but you cannot terminate if the brand has a scandal

### Platform-Specific Issues (2025-2026)
17. **Instagram:** Feed post reach is 3-5% organic in 2026; contracts should not guarantee reach metrics
18. **TikTok:** Algorithm volatility means view counts are unpredictable; avoid performance-based-only pay
19. **YouTube:** Longer shelf life; license duration matters more — demand expiry dates on content usage
20. **Cross-platform repurposing:** Content created for one platform being redistributed to others without consent or compensation

### FTC Enforcement Trends (2025-2026)
- FTC has increased enforcement against both creators and brands
- Sephora: $1.5M settlement for influencer disclosure failures
- Amazon influencers: $5,000+ fines per undisclosed video
- TikTok creators: personal penalties for undisclosed sponsorships
- 72% of brand-influencer disputes stem from unclear contract terms (Influencer Marketing Hub, 2026)
- Contracts MUST include explicit disclosure requirements specifying format and placement
