## 1. Contract Section Templates

Use these sections as the structural backbone for any contract. Not every contract needs
every section — select what applies, but always include Parties, Scope, Compensation,
IP Rights, Termination, and Dispute Resolution at minimum.

### 1.1 Preamble

The opening paragraph that establishes the agreement's identity.

**Must include:**
- Full title of the agreement (e.g., "Content Sponsorship Agreement")
- Effective date
- Full legal names of all parties
- Entity type and state/country of organization (if business entities)
- Short-form references for each party used throughout (e.g., "Creator," "Brand")

**Template:**

```
This [Agreement Type] ("Agreement") is entered into as of [Date] ("Effective Date")
by and between:

[Full Legal Name], a [entity type] organized under the laws of [jurisdiction],
with its principal place of business at [address] ("Party A Short Name");

and

[Full Legal Name], an individual / a [entity type] organized under the laws of
[jurisdiction], with its principal place of business at [address] ("Party B Short Name").
```

**Tips:**
- Use descriptive role-based labels ("Creator" and "Brand") rather than generic ("Party A")
- Include "doing business as" (d/b/a) if either party operates under a trade name
- Spell out the full date — avoid ambiguous formats like 03/04/26

### 1.2 Recitals (Background / "Whereas" Clauses)

Context-setting paragraphs that explain why the parties are entering the agreement.
Not strictly binding but provide interpretive context if disputes arise.

**Must include:**
- What each party does (brief description)
- The purpose of the agreement
- Any prior relationship or agreements being replaced

**Template:**

```
WHEREAS, [Party A] is a [description of business/role]; and

WHEREAS, [Party B] is a [description of business/role]; and

WHEREAS, the parties wish to [purpose of the agreement];

NOW, THEREFORE, in consideration of the mutual covenants and agreements set forth
herein, and for other good and valuable consideration, the receipt and sufficiency
of which are hereby acknowledged, the parties agree as follows:
```

**Tips:**
- Keep recitals factual and brief
- Do not include operative terms (obligations, deadlines) in recitals
- Modern contracts may drop "WHEREAS" in favor of plain language headers

### 1.3 Definitions

A dedicated section defining key terms used throughout the contract. Critical for
eliminating ambiguity.

**Must include:**
- Any term with a specific or technical meaning in this agreement
- Terms that appear in multiple sections
- Terms where everyday meaning differs from intended contractual meaning

**Template:**

```
1. Definitions

For purposes of this Agreement, the following terms have the meanings set forth below:

"Content" means [describe — e.g., all written, visual, audio, or video materials
created by Creator under this Agreement].

"Deliverables" means [describe — e.g., the specific items Creator is obligated
to produce, as detailed in Exhibit A].

"Territory" means [describe — e.g., worldwide, or specific geographic regions].

"Term" means the period from the Effective Date through [end date or event],
unless earlier terminated in accordance with Section [X].
```

**Tips:**
- Alphabetize definitions for easy reference
- Capitalize defined terms consistently throughout the contract
- Define terms on first use if the contract is short; use a definitions section if >5 terms
- Avoid circular definitions (defining a term using itself)

### 1.4 Scope of Work / Services

The core of what is being agreed to — what each party will do.

**Must include:**
- Specific deliverables or services
- Quality standards or specifications
- Timeline and milestones
- Approval process for deliverables
- What is explicitly excluded from scope

**Template:**

```
2. Scope of Work

2.1 Services. [Party A] shall provide the following services to [Party B]:
   (a) [Service/deliverable 1 with specifics]
   (b) [Service/deliverable 2 with specifics]
   (c) [Service/deliverable 3 with specifics]

2.2 Deliverable Schedule.
   | Deliverable | Due Date | Format |
   |-------------|----------|--------|
   | [Item]      | [Date]   | [Spec] |

2.3 Approval Process. [Party B] shall review each deliverable within [X] business
days of receipt. If revisions are needed, [Party B] shall provide written feedback.
[Party A] shall have [X] rounds of revisions included in the scope.

2.4 Exclusions. This Agreement does not include [list exclusions].
```

**Tips:**
- Be as specific as possible — vague scope is the #1 source of contract disputes
- Attach detailed specifications as an exhibit if needed
- Specify the number of revision rounds included
- Define what "completion" or "acceptance" means

### 1.5 Compensation and Payment

How, when, and how much one party pays the other.

**Must include:**
- Total compensation amount or rate structure
- Payment schedule (milestone-based, monthly, upon completion)
- Payment method (wire, ACH, check, platform)
- Currency
- Late payment penalties or interest
- Expense reimbursement policy (if applicable)
- Tax obligations (who handles withholding, 1099s, etc.)

**Template:**

```
3. Compensation

3.1 Fees. [Party B] shall pay [Party A] a total fee of $[amount] for the
Services described in Section 2, structured as follows:
   (a) $[amount] upon execution of this Agreement
   (b) $[amount] upon delivery of [milestone]
   (c) $[amount] upon completion and acceptance of all Deliverables

3.2 Payment Terms. All payments are due within [30] days of invoice date.
Invoices shall be sent to [email/address].

3.3 Late Payment. Overdue payments shall accrue interest at a rate of [1.5]%
per month, or the maximum rate permitted by law, whichever is lower.

3.4 Expenses. [Party B] shall reimburse [Party A] for pre-approved,
documented expenses up to $[cap] per [period].

3.5 Taxes. Each party is responsible for its own tax obligations arising
from this Agreement. [Party A] acknowledges they are an independent contractor
and not an employee of [Party B].
```

**Tips:**
- Always specify currency (USD, GBP, EUR)
- Include a kill fee if the project can be cancelled mid-stream
- For recurring work, specify rate review periods
- Include a cap on expenses if reimbursement is offered

### 1.6 Intellectual Property Rights

Who owns what — before, during, and after the agreement.

**Must include:**
- Ownership of pre-existing IP (each party retains theirs)
- Ownership of newly created IP (work product)
- License grants (scope, duration, territory, exclusivity)
- Usage rights (platforms, formats, modifications allowed)
- Attribution requirements
- Rights upon termination

**Template:**

```
4. Intellectual Property

4.1 Pre-Existing IP. Each party retains all rights to its pre-existing
intellectual property. Nothing in this Agreement transfers ownership of
pre-existing IP.

4.2 Work Product Ownership. [Choose one:]

   Option A (Creator retains): Creator retains all rights, title, and interest
   in the Deliverables. Creator grants [Party B] a [non-exclusive / exclusive],
   [revocable / irrevocable] license to use the Deliverables for [specific
   purposes] in [Territory] for [duration].

   Option B (Client owns): Upon full payment, all Deliverables and work product
   shall be considered "work made for hire" and shall be the exclusive property
   of [Party B]. To the extent any Deliverable does not qualify as work made
   for hire, Creator hereby assigns all rights to [Party B].

4.3 Usage Rights. [Party B] may use the Deliverables:
   (a) On the following platforms: [list]
   (b) In the following formats: [list]
   (c) For the following duration: [specify — e.g., 12 months from delivery]
   (d) Modifications: [Party B] [may / may not] modify the Deliverables
       without Creator's prior written consent.

4.4 Attribution. [Party B] [shall / shall not] provide attribution to Creator
in the following form: [specify].

4.5 Post-Termination. Upon termination, [Party B]'s license to use the
Deliverables [continues / terminates within X days]. [Party B] shall
[remove / may continue using] previously published Content.
```

**Tips:**
- Never leave IP ownership ambiguous — this is the most litigated area for creators
- Specify platform-by-platform usage rights (social media, website, paid ads, print)
- Distinguish between organic use and paid/boosted use (different value)
- Time-limit usage rights — perpetual licenses should cost significantly more
- Address AI training rights explicitly in 2026 contracts

### 1.7 Confidentiality

Protecting sensitive information shared between parties.

**Must include:**
- Definition of confidential information
- Obligations of the receiving party
- Exclusions (public info, independently developed, legally required disclosures)
- Duration of confidentiality obligations
- Return or destruction of confidential materials

**Template:**

```
5. Confidentiality

5.1 Definition. "Confidential Information" means any non-public information
disclosed by one party ("Discloser") to the other ("Recipient"), whether
orally, in writing, or electronically, including but not limited to business
plans, financial data, customer lists, marketing strategies, product roadmaps,
and the terms of this Agreement.

5.2 Obligations. The Recipient shall:
   (a) Use Confidential Information solely for purposes of this Agreement
   (b) Not disclose it to third parties without Discloser's prior written consent
   (c) Protect it with at least the same care used for its own confidential
       information, but no less than reasonable care

5.3 Exclusions. Confidentiality obligations do not apply to information that:
   (a) Is or becomes publicly available through no fault of Recipient
   (b) Was known to Recipient before disclosure
   (c) Is independently developed by Recipient without use of Confidential Information
   (d) Is disclosed pursuant to a legal requirement, provided Recipient gives
       prompt notice to Discloser

5.4 Duration. Confidentiality obligations survive termination for [2] years.

5.5 Return of Materials. Upon termination or request, Recipient shall
promptly return or destroy all Confidential Information and certify destruction
in writing.
```

**Tips:**
- Consider whether the existence of the deal itself is confidential
- For creators: clarify whether campaign details can be shared in case studies/portfolios
- Keep the duration reasonable — 2-3 years is standard; perpetual may be excessive

### 1.8 Termination

How the agreement ends — planned or unplanned.

**Must include:**
- Term / duration of the agreement
- Renewal provisions (auto-renew vs. manual)
- Termination for convenience (either party can exit with notice)
- Termination for cause (breach, insolvency, etc.)
- Cure period (time to fix a breach before termination)
- Effects of termination (payment for work done, return of materials, survival clauses)

**Template:**

```
6. Term and Termination

6.1 Term. This Agreement begins on the Effective Date and continues until
[end date] unless earlier terminated ("Term").

6.2 Renewal. [This Agreement will / will not] automatically renew for
successive [period] terms unless either party provides written notice of
non-renewal at least [30] days before the end of the current Term.

6.3 Termination for Convenience. Either party may terminate this Agreement
at any time upon [30] days' prior written notice to the other party.

6.4 Termination for Cause. Either party may terminate this Agreement
immediately upon written notice if:
   (a) The other party materially breaches this Agreement and fails to cure
       such breach within [15] days of receiving written notice; or
   (b) The other party becomes insolvent, files for bankruptcy, or ceases
       normal business operations.

6.5 Effects of Termination.
   (a) [Party B] shall pay [Party A] for all Services completed and accepted
       through the termination date.
   (b) [Kill fee: If terminated for convenience by Party B, Party B shall pay
       [X]% of remaining fees as a cancellation fee.]
   (c) Each party shall return the other's Confidential Information.
   (d) Sections [Confidentiality, IP, Limitation of Liability, Dispute
       Resolution] shall survive termination.
```

**Tips:**
- Always include a kill fee for creator contracts — protect against last-minute cancellations
- Specify what happens to partially completed work
- List which sections survive termination (these remain binding after the contract ends)
- For ongoing relationships, address transition/handoff obligations

### 1.9 Dispute Resolution

How disagreements are handled — before and instead of court.

**Must include:**
- Escalation process (informal negotiation first)
- Mediation step (optional but recommended)
- Arbitration or litigation (binding resolution)
- Governing law and jurisdiction
- Fee allocation (who pays for dispute resolution)

**Template:**

```
7. Dispute Resolution

7.1 Informal Resolution. The parties shall first attempt to resolve any
dispute arising out of this Agreement through good-faith negotiation between
their respective representatives for a period of [30] days.

7.2 Mediation. If negotiation fails, the parties shall submit the dispute
to mediation administered by [mediation body, e.g., AAA, JAMS] in
[location], with costs shared equally.

7.3 Arbitration. If mediation fails, the dispute shall be resolved by
binding arbitration administered by [arbitration body] in [location]
under its [Commercial / Consumer] Arbitration Rules. The arbitrator's
decision shall be final and enforceable in any court of competent
jurisdiction.

   [Alternative — Litigation:]
7.3 Litigation. If mediation fails, either party may pursue the dispute
in the state or federal courts of [jurisdiction]. Each party consents
to personal jurisdiction in such courts.

7.4 Governing Law. This Agreement shall be governed by and construed in
accordance with the laws of [State/Country], without regard to its
conflict-of-laws principles.

7.5 Fees. The prevailing party in any dispute shall be entitled to
recover its reasonable attorneys' fees and costs from the other party.
```

**Tips:**
- Multi-tiered clauses (negotiate → mediate → arbitrate) reduce costs and preserve relationships
- Arbitration is faster and private but limits appeal rights
- Specify the seat of arbitration and applicable rules
- For international deals, consider ICC or WIPO arbitration
- Small-claims carve-outs let either party go directly to small claims court for low-value disputes

### 1.10 General Provisions (Boilerplate)

Standard clauses that appear in virtually every contract. Do not skip these —
they handle edge cases that matter when things go wrong.

**Must include:**
- Force majeure
- Entire agreement / integration clause
- Amendment process
- Severability
- Waiver
- Assignment
- Notices
- Independent contractor status (if applicable)
- Counterparts and electronic signatures

**Template:**

```
8. General Provisions

8.1 Force Majeure. Neither party shall be liable for failure to perform
due to causes beyond its reasonable control, including natural disasters,
war, pandemic, government action, or internet/infrastructure failure.
The affected party must provide prompt notice and use reasonable efforts
to mitigate.

8.2 Entire Agreement. This Agreement, together with all exhibits,
constitutes the entire agreement between the parties and supersedes
all prior negotiations, representations, and agreements.

8.3 Amendments. This Agreement may be amended only by a written
instrument signed by both parties.

8.4 Severability. If any provision is found unenforceable, the remaining
provisions continue in full force and effect.

8.5 Waiver. Failure to enforce any provision does not waive the right
to enforce it later.

8.6 Assignment. Neither party may assign this Agreement without the
other party's prior written consent, except in connection with a
merger, acquisition, or sale of substantially all assets.

8.7 Notices. All notices must be in writing and delivered to the
addresses listed in the preamble by email (with read receipt) or
certified mail.

8.8 Independent Contractor. [Party A] is an independent contractor,
not an employee, partner, or agent of [Party B].

8.9 Counterparts. This Agreement may be executed in counterparts,
including electronic signatures, each of which shall be deemed an original.
```

**Tips:**
- Force majeure: list specific events relevant to your industry (platform outages for digital creators)
- Always include the entire agreement clause to prevent "but you said on the phone..." disputes
- Electronic signature validity is standard in 2026 but state it explicitly for clarity

## 2. Plain Language Principles

Contracts should be understandable by the people who sign them, not just the lawyers
who draft them. Plain language improves compliance, reduces disputes, and builds trust.

### 2.1 Core Principles

| Principle | Do This | Not This |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| **Use everyday words** | "begin" | "commence" |
| | "end" | "terminate" (use only as defined term) |
| | "pay" | "remunerate" |
| | "tell" / "notify" | "furnish notice to" |
| | "about" | "with respect to" / "in connection with" |
| | "if" | "in the event that" |
| | "under" | "pursuant to" |
| | "before" | "prior to" |
| | "enough" | "sufficient" |
| **Use active voice** | "The Client shall pay..." | "Payment shall be made by..." |
| **Use "must" for obligations** | "Creator must deliver..." | "Creator shall deliver..." |
| **One idea per sentence** | Split compound obligations | Long sentences with multiple "and"s |
| **Use positive statements** | "Keep records for 3 years" | "Do not fail to maintain records for a period of no less than 3 years" |

### 2.2 Sentence Structure Rules

1. **Keep sentences under 25 words** when possible
2. **One obligation per sentence** — if a sentence has more than one "and," split it
3. **Lead with the actor** — "Creator must..." not "It is required that Creator..."
4. **Use lists for multiple items** — bullets and numbered lists are easier to parse than inline lists
5. **Front-load key information** — put the most important terms (price, deadline, rights) early

### 2.3 Document Structure Rules

1. **Use descriptive headings** — "How Payment Works" over "Section 4.2(b)(iii)"
2. **Number every section and subsection** — enables precise cross-referencing
3. **Use consistent formatting** — same font, spacing, and hierarchy throughout
4. **Include a table of contents** for contracts over 5 pages
5. **Put detailed specs in exhibits** — keep the main body readable
6. **Use tables for structured data** — payment schedules, deliverable lists, timelines

### 2.4 Words and Phrases to Eliminate

| Instead of... | Use... |
|---------------|--------|
| "herein," "hereof," "hereto," "hereby" | "in this Agreement," "of this Agreement" |
| "whereas" | "Background:" or just state the facts |
| "notwithstanding the foregoing" | "Despite [specific section]" or "Even if..." |
| "shall" | "must" (for obligations) or "will" (for future facts) |
| "in lieu of" | "instead of" |
| "null and void" | "void" (one word does the job) |
| "indemnify and hold harmless" | "indemnify" (legally equivalent on its own) |
| "in the event that" | "if" |
| "for the purpose of" | "to" |
| "at such time as" | "when" |
| "the sum of $5,000" | "$5,000" |
| "during the period of" | "during" or "for" |

### 2.5 Readability Checks

Before finalizing any contract:
- **Flesch Reading Ease score** should be above 40 (ideally 50-60 for non-technical sections)
- **Read it aloud** — if you stumble, simplify
- **Give it to a non-lawyer** — if they cannot explain the key terms back to you, rewrite
- **Check defined terms** — every capitalized term should appear in the Definitions section
- **Cross-reference audit** — verify every "Section X" reference points to the correct section

## 3. Contract Types for Creators

Creators need different contracts for different working relationships. Each type has
unique considerations beyond standard business contracts.

### 3.1 Sponsorship / Brand Deal Agreement

**When to use:** A brand pays a creator to produce content featuring or promoting their
product, service, or brand.

**Key sections beyond standard:**
- **Deliverables specification:** Number of posts, platforms, formats (Reel, Story, YouTube video, etc.)
- **Content approval process:** Brand review timeline, number of revision rounds, approval deadlines
- **FTC/advertising disclosure:** Mandatory #ad or #sponsored tags, compliance with platform and regulatory rules
- **Usage rights / whitelisting:** Whether the brand can boost/promote creator content as paid ads (and for how long)
- **Exclusivity:** Whether the creator is barred from working with competitors, and for how long
- **Performance metrics:** Whether payment is tied to views, engagement, or conversions
- **Content ownership vs. license:** Creator typically retains ownership and grants a time-limited license
- **Organic vs. paid distinction:** Separate pricing and terms for organic posts vs. paid ad usage

**Creator-specific risks to address:**
- Platform algorithm changes affecting deliverable performance
- Brand requesting excessive revisions beyond agreed rounds
- Usage rights creep (brand using content beyond agreed scope/duration)
- AI training exclusions (brand may not use creator content to train AI models)

### 3.2 Freelance / Client Services Agreement

**When to use:** A creator provides services (writing, design, video production,
consulting) to a client for a fee.

**Key sections beyond standard:**
- **Scope boundaries:** Precisely what is and is not included
- **Revision policy:** Number of revisions included, hourly rate for additional revisions
- **Project timeline:** Milestones with specific dates, not vague timeframes
- **Client responsibilities:** What the client must provide (assets, access, feedback) and by when
- **Late payment terms:** Interest rate, right to pause work, right to withhold deliverables
- **Portfolio rights:** Creator's right to showcase work in their portfolio
- **Subcontracting:** Whether the creator can delegate parts of the work

**Creator-specific risks to address:**
- Scope creep (client expanding requirements without additional compensation)
- Client ghosting (non-responsive client delaying the project)
- Spec work (doing work before contract is signed)
- Kill fee protection if client cancels mid-project

### 3.3 Content Licensing Agreement

**When to use:** A party wants to use existing content that a creator has already
produced, or a creator licenses content for specific uses.

**Key sections beyond standard:**
- **Licensed content description:** Specific identification of what is being licensed
- **License type:** Exclusive vs. non-exclusive
- **License scope:** Platforms, territories, formats, duration
- **Modification rights:** Whether licensee can edit, crop, alter, or create derivatives
- **Sub-licensing:** Whether the licensee can grant rights to third parties
- **Attribution requirements:** How and where the creator must be credited
- **Takedown rights:** Creator's right to require removal under certain conditions
- **Renewal terms:** Automatic renewal, renegotiation window, rate adjustments

**Creator-specific risks to address:**
- Perpetual license requests (avoid unless compensated accordingly — 3-5x standard rate)
- AI training usage (explicitly exclude unless separately negotiated)
- Platform ToS conflicts (some platforms claim broad content licenses)
- Content being used in contexts that damage creator's brand

**Common license durations (2026 industry standards):**
- Social media organic: 30-90 days
- Social media paid/boosted: 30-60 days
- Website usage: 6-12 months
- Print/out-of-home: per campaign
- Perpetual: premium pricing, one-time buyout

### 3.4 Collaboration Agreement (Creator-to-Creator)

**When to use:** Two or more creators work together on a joint project — podcast,
video series, course, event, product, or content collaboration.

**Key sections beyond standard:**
- **Roles and responsibilities:** Who does what (production, editing, promotion, etc.)
- **Revenue split:** How income is divided (50/50, proportional to contribution, etc.)
- **Decision-making:** How creative and business decisions are made (consensus, lead creator decides)
- **Brand and credit:** How the collaboration is branded, how each creator is credited
- **Audience ownership:** Who owns the subscriber/follower list if a new channel is created
- **Content ownership:** Joint ownership vs. each creator owns their contribution
- **Exit strategy:** What happens if one creator wants to leave the collaboration
- **Non-compete during collaboration:** Whether creators can do similar projects with others

**Creator-specific risks to address:**
- Unequal effort (one creator doing more work than agreed)
- Audience overlap disputes
- One creator's controversy affecting the collaboration
- Monetization disagreements as the project grows
- Content rights after the collaboration ends

### 3.5 Consulting / Advisory Agreement

**When to use:** A creator provides expert advice, coaching, strategy, or mentorship
to a client or company — not producing content, but sharing expertise.

**Key sections beyond standard:**
- **Advisory scope:** Topics, formats (1:1 calls, group sessions, async review), frequency
- **Time commitment:** Hours per week/month, scheduling process, cancellation policy
- **Equity or compensation structure:** Retainer, hourly, project-based, or equity in client's company
- **Non-compete / non-solicit:** Restrictions on working with client's competitors or poaching their team
- **Use of name/likeness:** Whether the client can use the creator's name as an advisor/endorser
- **Results disclaimer:** Advisory services do not guarantee specific outcomes

**Creator-specific risks to address:**
- Client expecting content creation under a consulting agreement
- Overcommitment of time without clear boundaries
- Equity vesting schedules that penalize early departure
- Client publicly associating creator with outcomes creator did not control
