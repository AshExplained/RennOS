# Wellness Check — Best Practices Reference

Use this document as domain methodology when conducting a wellness check-in. Cross-reference these frameworks to produce a structured, evidence-based, and actionable assessment.

---

## 1. Foundational Wellness Frameworks

### 1.1 SAMHSA Eight Dimensions of Wellness

The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) defines wellness through eight interconnected dimensions, developed by Peggy Swarbrick. Each dimension affects overall quality of life, and neglecting any one can cascade into others.

| Dimension | Definition | Key Indicators |
|:----------|:-----------|:---------------|
| **Emotional** | Ability to cope with life's challenges and navigate emotions | Stress management, emotional regulation, self-awareness |
| **Physical** | Recognizing the need for physical activity, nutrition, and sleep | Exercise habits, diet quality, sleep patterns, energy levels |
| **Social** | Developing a sense of connection and belonging | Quality relationships, social support network, community involvement |
| **Intellectual** | Engaging in creative and stimulating mental activities | Curiosity, learning, problem-solving engagement |
| **Spiritual** | Expanding sense of purpose and meaning in life | Values alignment, sense of meaning, mindfulness practice |
| **Occupational** | Enrichment and satisfaction through work | Work-life balance, career fulfillment, professional growth |
| **Financial** | Satisfaction with current and future financial situations | Financial security, planning, reduced money-related stress |
| **Environmental** | Living in pleasant, stimulating environments that support wellbeing | Safe housing, access to nature, organized living space |

**How to apply:** While all eight dimensions matter, the RennOS wellness check focuses on the six most assessable through self-report: emotional (stress, mood), physical (sleep, energy, nutrition, activity), social (connection), intellectual (purpose/motivation), spiritual (meaning alignment), and environmental (living conditions). Financial and occupational are tracked by other RennOS departments.

### 1.2 National Wellness Institute Six Dimensions

The Wellness Alliance (formerly National Wellness Institute) defines wellness as "functioning optimally within your current environment" and identifies six core dimensions: Emotional, Physical, Intellectual, Occupational, Spiritual, and Social. Their framework emphasizes:

- Wellness is an evolving process, not a destination
- It is holistic and multidimensional
- It encompasses lifestyle, spiritual, and environmental domains
- Each dimension is interdependent with the others

### 1.3 The Indivisible Self Model (IS-Wel)

Developed by Myers and Sweeney, this evidence-based model identifies five second-order factors of wellness:

1. **Creative Self** — thinking, emotions, control, work, positive humor
2. **Coping Self** — leisure, stress management, self-worth, realistic beliefs
3. **Social Self** — friendship, love
4. **Essential Self** — spirituality, gender identity, cultural identity, self-care
5. **Physical Self** — exercise, nutrition

This model underpins the Wellness Evaluation of Lifestyle (WEL) and its successor, the Five-Factor Wellness Inventory (5F-Wel), considered among the most psychometrically validated wellness instruments.

## 2. The Seven Assessment Dimensions

For the RennOS wellness check, assess these seven dimensions. Each includes evidence-based measurement guidance.

### 2.1 Stress Level

**What to measure:** Perceived stress — how unpredictable, uncontrollable, and overloaded life feels.

**Evidence-based approach — Perceived Stress Scale (PSS-10):**
The PSS-10 is the most widely used psychological instrument for measuring stress perception. It has strong psychometric properties: Cronbach's alpha consistently above 0.70 across all validated studies, with test-retest reliability also above 0.70.

**Simplified self-assessment (1-10 scale):**

| Score | Level | Interpretation |
|:------|:------|:---------------|
| 1-3 | Low | Manageable stress, feeling in control |
| 4-5 | Moderate-low | Some pressure but coping well |
| 6-7 | Moderate-high | Stress is noticeable and affecting daily life |
| 8-10 | High | Overwhelmed, difficulty coping, potential burnout |

**Key questions to ask:**
- How unpredictable has your life felt recently?
- How often do you feel unable to control the important things?
- What are the top 1-3 sources of stress right now?
- How confident do you feel in your ability to handle current problems?

**Red flags (flag for professional support):**
- Persistent score of 8+ over multiple check-ins
- Physical symptoms: chest tightness, headaches, jaw clenching, digestive issues
- Inability to identify any coping strategies that help
- Stress accompanied by feelings of hopelessness

### 2.2 Sleep Quality

**What to measure:** Both quantity and quality of sleep, including subjective satisfaction.

**Evidence-based approach — Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI):**
The PSQI is the most widely adopted subjective sleep quality measure (Cronbach's alpha = 0.83, test-retest reliability r = 0.85). It assesses seven components:

1. Subjective sleep quality
2. Sleep latency (time to fall asleep)
3. Sleep duration
4. Habitual sleep efficiency (time asleep vs. time in bed)
5. Sleep disturbances
6. Use of sleep medication
7. Daytime dysfunction

Global PSQI scores range 0-21; a score above 5 indicates significant sleep difficulties.

**Simplified self-assessment (1-10 scale):**

| Score | Level | Interpretation |
|:------|:------|:---------------|
| 1-3 | Poor | Significant sleep issues affecting daily function |
| 4-5 | Below average | Inconsistent or insufficient sleep |
| 6-7 | Good | Generally restful, minor issues |
| 8-10 | Excellent | Consistent, restorative sleep |

**Key questions to ask:**
- How many hours of sleep are you averaging?
- How long does it typically take you to fall asleep?
- Do you wake up feeling rested?
- Any disruptions: waking during the night, early morning waking, vivid dreams?
- Screen time before bed?
- Consistent sleep and wake times?

**Red flags:**
- Consistently under 6 hours per night
- Taking more than 30 minutes to fall asleep regularly
- Daytime dysfunction: inability to concentrate, microsleeps, irritability
- Using alcohol or substances to fall asleep
- Sleep quality score declining over consecutive check-ins

### 2.3 Energy Levels

**What to measure:** Both physical and mental energy, including variability throughout the day.

**Evidence-based context:**
Research using Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) methodology rates energy on Likert scales, typically 0-8 or 1-10, from "extremely tired" to "extremely energetic." Energy is strongly correlated with sleep quality, stress, nutrition, and physical activity — making it an integrative signal.

**Simplified self-assessment (1-10 scale):**

| Score | Level | Interpretation |
|:------|:------|:---------------|
| 1-3 | Low | Persistent fatigue, difficulty starting or completing tasks |
| 4-5 | Below average | Getting through the day but dragging |
| 6-7 | Good | Functional energy with some dips |
| 8-10 | High | Sustained energy, feeling vital and capable |

**Key questions to ask:**
- Physical energy: do you feel capable of physical tasks without excessive fatigue?
- Mental energy: can you concentrate and think clearly?
- When during the day is energy highest? Lowest?
- Are there specific drains: poor sleep, skipped meals, overwork, dehydration?
- Has your energy pattern changed recently?

**Red flags:**
- Persistent low energy (3 or below) across multiple check-ins
- Energy levels not improving with adequate sleep
- Sudden, unexplained energy crashes
- Low energy combined with low mood (possible depression indicator)

### 2.4 Mood

**What to measure:** Overall emotional state, emotional range, and emotional stability.

**Evidence-based approach — WHO-5 Well-Being Index:**
The WHO-5 is a brief, validated instrument (5 items, Likert scale) measuring subjective wellbeing over the past two weeks. Scoring:

- Raw score: 0-25 (multiply by 4 for percentage score 0-100)
- Score below 50% (raw 13): indicates poor wellbeing, warrants further screening
- Score below 28%: possible clinical depression, recommend professional assessment

The five WHO-5 items assess: cheerfulness, calmness/relaxation, feeling active/vigorous, waking fresh/rested, and daily life filled with interesting things.

**Simplified self-assessment — Mood Description:**
Rather than a single number, mood is best captured through description:

| Category | Indicators |
|:---------|:-----------|
| **Positive** | Content, optimistic, motivated, calm, grateful, curious |
| **Neutral** | Flat, "fine," going through the motions, neither good nor bad |
| **Negative** | Anxious, sad, irritable, frustrated, overwhelmed, apathetic |
| **Mixed** | Emotional volatility, rapid shifts, good moments punctuated by lows |

**Key questions to ask:**
- In one or two words, how would you describe your overall mood this week?
- Has your mood been mostly stable or shifting?
- What has brought you joy or satisfaction recently?
- What has been weighing on you emotionally?
- Are you experiencing any emotions that feel disproportionate to the situation?

**Red flags:**
- Persistent low mood lasting more than two weeks
- Loss of interest in activities previously enjoyed (anhedonia)
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
- Mood that does not improve with positive experiences
- Any mention of self-harm or suicidal ideation — immediate professional referral

### 2.5 Social Connection

**What to measure:** Quality of relationships, sense of belonging, and frequency of meaningful interaction.

**Evidence-based context:**
The Social Connectedness Scale (SCS) is the most established instrument, with Cronbach's alpha consistently in the high 0.80s to low 0.90s. Research shows low social connectedness is associated with increased vulnerability to depression and anxiety. A systematic review of 24 social connectedness measures found the UCLA-3 (loneliness) and MSPSS (social support) have the strongest psychometric evidence.

A brief three-item screener found that assessing three domains provides comprehensive information:
1. Risk of loneliness/social isolation
2. Adequacy of social/emotional support
3. Frequency of meaningful social contacts

**Simplified self-assessment (1-10 scale):**

| Score | Level | Interpretation |
|:------|:------|:---------------|
| 1-3 | Isolated | Few or no meaningful connections, feeling lonely |
| 4-5 | Disconnected | Some connections but lacking depth or frequency |
| 6-7 | Connected | Regular meaningful interactions, adequate support |
| 8-10 | Thriving | Strong support network, deep connections, sense of belonging |

**Key questions to ask:**
- Do you have people you can talk to openly about what matters to you?
- When was the last meaningful (not transactional) interaction with someone?
- Do you feel more connected or more isolated compared to last check-in?
- Are there relationships that are draining rather than energizing?
- Do you feel part of a community (online or offline)?

**Red flags:**
- Persistent isolation scores (3 or below) across multiple check-ins
- Active withdrawal from previously valued relationships
- No one to call in an emergency or share good news with
- Isolation combined with low mood — high-risk combination for depression

### 2.6 Physical Activity

**What to measure:** Movement patterns, exercise frequency, and sedentary behavior.

**Evidence-based context:**
WHO guidelines recommend at least 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, or 75-150 minutes of vigorous activity. Research consistently shows physical activity improves mood, reduces anxiety and depression, enhances sleep quality, and boosts energy — making it a keystone habit that affects multiple wellness dimensions.

**Simplified self-assessment (1-10 scale):**

| Score | Level | Interpretation |
|:------|:------|:---------------|
| 1-3 | Sedentary | Little to no intentional movement |
| 4-5 | Low active | Some movement but below recommended levels |
| 6-7 | Active | Meeting general activity guidelines |
| 8-10 | Very active | Consistent exercise routine, varied movement |

**Key questions to ask:**
- How many times did you exercise this week? What type?
- How much of your day is spent sitting?
- Do you feel your body is getting the movement it needs?
- Any physical discomfort or pain that limits activity?
- Are you moving because you enjoy it, or does it feel like a chore?

**Red flags:**
- Complete sedentary lifestyle with no intention to change
- Excessive exercise (potential over-training or exercise compulsion)
- Physical pain that is being ignored rather than addressed
- Activity levels dropping alongside declining mood

### 2.7 Nutrition

**What to measure:** Eating patterns, hydration, and relationship with food.

**Evidence-based context:**
Nutritional psychiatry research demonstrates strong links between diet quality and mental health. The Mediterranean diet pattern has the strongest evidence base for mood and cognitive function. Key nutritional factors for wellness include: adequate hydration, regular meal timing, sufficient micronutrient intake (particularly B vitamins, vitamin D, omega-3 fatty acids, magnesium), and balanced macronutrient consumption.

**Simplified self-assessment (1-10 scale):**

| Score | Level | Interpretation |
|:------|:------|:---------------|
| 1-3 | Poor | Skipping meals, relying on processed food, dehydrated |
| 4-5 | Below average | Inconsistent eating, some nutritional gaps |
| 6-7 | Good | Mostly balanced, regular meals, adequate hydration |
| 8-10 | Excellent | Consistent whole-food nutrition, mindful eating |

**Key questions to ask:**
- How regular are your meals? Any skipping?
- How much water are you drinking daily?
- Are you eating mostly whole foods or relying on convenience food?
- Any emotional eating or loss of appetite?
- How does your energy relate to your eating patterns?

**Red flags:**
- Persistent skipped meals or extreme caloric restriction
- Emotional eating as a primary coping mechanism
- Significant weight changes (gain or loss) without intention
- Disordered eating patterns — refer to professional support
- Chronic dehydration

## 3. Assessment Methodology

### 3.1 Check-In Structure

Follow this evidence-informed structure for each wellness check:

**Phase 1 — Open-ended entry (2-3 minutes)**
Start with a broad, non-directive question: "How are you feeling overall?" This captures the most salient concern before structured assessment potentially anchors responses.

**Phase 2 — Structured dimension scan (5-7 minutes)**
Walk through each of the seven dimensions with a 1-10 rating plus brief context. Use the key questions from Section 2 as prompts — do not ask all of them, select 1-2 per dimension based on what seems most relevant.

**Phase 3 — Pattern recognition (2-3 minutes)**
Compare current scores to previous check-ins (if available). Identify:
- Dimensions that have improved or declined
- Correlations between dimensions (e.g., poor sleep driving low energy and irritable mood)
- Persistent patterns that may need intervention

**Phase 4 — Action planning (2-3 minutes)**
Based on findings, suggest 1-3 specific, actionable steps. Prioritize the dimension with the highest leverage — often a keystone habit like sleep or exercise that improves multiple other dimensions.

### 3.2 Tone and Approach

The wellness check must be:

- **Warm and supportive** — The person should feel safe being honest
- **Non-judgmental** — No scores are "bad," they are information
- **Curious, not clinical** — Ask follow-up questions rather than diagnosing
- **Empowering** — Focus on what is within the person's control
- **Proportionate** — Match urgency to severity; do not catastrophize low scores

Avoid:
- Lecturing or moralizing ("you should exercise more")
- Minimizing ("everyone feels stressed")
- Diagnosing ("that sounds like depression")
- Overwhelming with too many action items

### 3.3 Frequency

- **Standard cadence:** Weekly check-ins provide the best balance of tracking resolution and sustainability
- **High-stress periods:** Increase to 2-3 times per week
- **Stable periods:** Biweekly is sufficient
- **Minimum viable:** Monthly, though this misses important short-term patterns

## 4. Scoring and Tracking

### 4.1 Dimension Scoring

Each dimension uses a 1-10 scale. Aggregate scoring:

| Metric | Calculation |
|:-------|:-----------|
| **Dimension score** | Single 1-10 self-report per dimension |
| **Composite wellness score** | Average of all seven dimension scores |
| **Trend indicator** | Change from previous check-in (improving, stable, declining) |

### 4.2 Composite Score Interpretation

| Composite Score | Overall Wellness Level | Response |
|:----------------|:----------------------|:---------|
| 8.0-10.0 | Thriving | Maintain current habits, note what is working |
| 6.0-7.9 | Good | Minor adjustments, reinforce strong areas |
| 4.0-5.9 | Mixed | Identify and address 1-2 weakest dimensions |
| 2.0-3.9 | Struggling | Prioritize immediate relief, consider professional support |
| 1.0-1.9 | Crisis | Professional support recommended urgently |

### 4.3 Tracking Format

Each wellness check should be recorded with:

```markdown
## Wellness Check — [Date]

### Scores
| Dimension | Score | Trend | Notes |
|:----------|:------|:------|:------|
| Stress | X/10 | up/down/stable | brief context |
| Sleep | X/10 | up/down/stable | brief context |
| Energy | X/10 | up/down/stable | brief context |
| Mood | X/10 | up/down/stable | brief context |
| Social | X/10 | up/down/stable | brief context |
| Activity | X/10 | up/down/stable | brief context |
| Nutrition | X/10 | up/down/stable | brief context |

**Composite:** X.X/10 (trend)

### Key Observations
- [Pattern or correlation noted]
- [Change from previous check-in]

### Action Items
1. [Specific, achievable action]
2. [Specific, achievable action]

### Flags
- [Any red flags noted, or "None"]
```

### 4.4 Longitudinal Analysis

After 4+ check-ins, analyze:

- **Baseline establishment:** What are typical scores for each dimension?
- **Volatility:** Which dimensions fluctuate most? (These may need more attention)
- **Correlations:** Which dimensions move together? (Identifies root causes vs. symptoms)
- **Seasonal patterns:** Any time-of-year effects on wellness?
- **Intervention effectiveness:** Did suggested actions lead to measurable improvement?

## 5. When to Flag Concerns

### 5.1 Immediate Professional Referral

Flag immediately and recommend professional support if any of the following are present:

- **Suicidal ideation or self-harm:** Any mention, however casual, of wanting to hurt oneself or not wanting to be alive
- **Persistent hopelessness:** Feeling that nothing will improve, lasting more than two weeks
- **Inability to function:** Cannot perform basic daily tasks (hygiene, eating, work)
- **Substance dependence:** Using alcohol, drugs, or medication to cope with daily life
- **Disordered eating:** Restricting, binging, purging, or obsessive food behaviors
- **Panic attacks:** Recurring episodes of intense physical anxiety symptoms
- **Psychotic symptoms:** Hallucinations, delusions, severe paranoia

**How to flag:** Gently, without alarm. Frame professional support as a strength, not a failure:
> "What you're describing sounds really difficult. A therapist or counselor could give you tools and support that go beyond what a wellness check can offer. That's not a sign of weakness — it's the smart move."

### 5.2 Elevated Monitoring

Increase check-in frequency and track closely if:

- Any single dimension scores 3 or below for two consecutive check-ins
- Composite score drops below 4.0
- Three or more dimensions decline simultaneously
- The person reports feeling "stuck" or unable to improve despite effort
- Major life events: job loss, relationship changes, bereavement, relocation, health diagnosis

### 5.3 Positive Reinforcement Triggers

Equally important — recognize and reinforce when things are going well:

- Any dimension improves by 2+ points
- Composite score reaches or exceeds 7.0
- A previously flagged dimension returns to healthy range
- The person implements a suggested action and reports benefit
- New healthy habits are established (3+ consecutive weeks)

## 6. Intervention Toolkit

### 6.1 Immediate Relief (High Stress / Crisis Moments)

| Technique | Time | Evidence Base |
|:----------|:-----|:-------------|
| Box breathing (4-4-4-4) | 2 min | Activates parasympathetic nervous system |
| 5-4-3-2-1 grounding | 3 min | Interrupts anxiety spirals via sensory focus |
| Cold water on wrists/face | 1 min | Triggers dive reflex, reduces heart rate |
| Body scan meditation | 5 min | Reduces muscle tension and somatic stress |
| Writing out stressors | 5 min | Externalizes worry, reduces cognitive load |

### 6.2 Lifestyle Adjustments by Dimension

**Sleep hygiene:**
- Consistent sleep/wake times (even on weekends)
- No screens 30-60 minutes before bed
- Cool, dark, quiet sleep environment
- No caffeine after 2 PM
- Wind-down routine: reading, stretching, journaling

**Stress management:**
- Time-blocking and single-tasking
- Setting boundaries (saying no)
- Regular breaks using Pomodoro or similar
- Nature exposure (even 20 minutes has measurable effects)
- Journaling: expressive writing reduces cortisol

**Energy optimization:**
- Hydration: aim for 2-3 liters per day
- Meal timing: avoid long gaps, include protein and complex carbs
- Movement breaks: 5 minutes every hour of sedentary work
- Sunlight exposure in the first hour after waking
- Strategic caffeine timing (not within 8 hours of bedtime)

**Mood support:**
- Gratitude practice (3 things daily, shown to improve wellbeing in RCTs)
- Social contact: even brief positive interactions elevate mood
- Achievement logging: tracking small wins combats helplessness
- Behavioral activation: doing valued activities even when unmotivated
- Limiting doomscrolling and negative news consumption

**Social connection:**
- Schedule regular check-ins with important people
- Join communities aligned with interests
- Practice vulnerability: share honestly rather than performing "fine"
- Reduce time with energy-draining relationships
- Volunteer or contribute to community

## 7. Validated Instruments Reference

For deeper assessment when a dimension needs closer examination, these validated instruments can be used or referenced:

| Instrument | Measures | Items | Time | Access | Reliability |
|:-----------|:---------|:------|:-----|:-------|:------------|
| **PSS-10** | Perceived stress | 10 | 5 min | Free | alpha > .70 |
| **PSQI** | Sleep quality | 19 | 10 min | Free | alpha = .83 |
| **WHO-5** | Wellbeing/mood | 5 | 2 min | Free | Validated in 35+ countries |
| **PHQ-9** | Depression screening | 9 | 5 min | Free | alpha = .89 |
| **GAD-7** | Anxiety screening | 7 | 5 min | Free | alpha = .92 |
| **UCLA-3** | Loneliness | 3 | 1 min | Free | Validated in large populations |
| **PWS** | Perceived wellness (6 dimensions) | 36 | 15 min | Free | Strong construct validity |

**Important:** These are screening tools, not diagnostic instruments. Elevated scores indicate areas for further exploration, not clinical diagnoses.
