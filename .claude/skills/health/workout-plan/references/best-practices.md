## 1. Core Training Principles

Every effective training program is built on these foundational principles:

### Specificity (SAID Principle)

Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands — the body adapts specifically to the
type of stress placed upon it.

- Training for strength? Use heavy loads and low reps.
- Training for endurance? Use lighter loads and higher reps.
- Training for a sport? Mimic the movement patterns and energy demands of that sport.

**Application:** Always align exercise selection, rep ranges, and rest periods
with the stated goal.

### Overload

The body must be subjected to a stimulus greater than what it is currently
accustomed to in order to adapt and improve.

- If the stimulus stays the same, adaptation plateaus.
- Overload can be achieved through increased weight, reps, sets, time under
  tension, reduced rest, or increased training frequency.

### Progression

Overload must be applied gradually and systematically over time.

- Too fast = injury, overtraining, burnout
- Too slow = stagnation, frustration
- Just right = consistent, measurable improvement

### Reversibility (Use It or Lose It)

Training adaptations are reversible when the training stimulus is removed.

- Strength declines after approximately 2-3 weeks of inactivity
- Cardiovascular fitness declines faster (within 1-2 weeks)
- Muscle memory allows faster re-adaptation after detraining

### Individuality

Every person responds differently to the same training stimulus based on:

- Genetics, age, sex, training history
- Recovery capacity, sleep quality, stress levels
- Nutrition, supplementation, hydration
- Injury history and mobility limitations

**Application:** Programs should be tailored to the individual, not copy-pasted
from generic templates.

### Recovery

Adaptation occurs during rest, not during training. The training session provides
the stimulus; recovery provides the growth.

## 2. The FITT Principle

The FITT principle provides four variables that can be manipulated to design
and progress any exercise program:

### Frequency

*How often do you train?*

| Goal | Recommended Frequency |
|------|----------------------|
| General fitness | 3-4 days/week |
| Muscle building (hypertrophy) | 4-6 days/week |
| Strength | 3-5 days/week |
| Weight/fat loss | 3-5 days/week (resistance) + 2-3 days (cardio) |
| Endurance | 3-6 days/week |

**Key considerations:**
- Each muscle group should be trained 2x per week for optimal hypertrophy
- Allow at least 48 hours between training the same muscle group with high intensity
- More is not always better — recovery capacity is finite

### Intensity

*How hard do you train?*

**For resistance training — measured by load relative to 1RM:**

| Rep Range | % of 1RM | Primary Adaptation |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| 1-5 reps | 85-100% | Maximal strength |
| 6-12 reps | 67-85% | Hypertrophy (muscle growth) |
| 12-20 reps | 50-67% | Muscular endurance |
| 20+ reps | <50% | Endurance / recovery |

**For cardiovascular training — measured by heart rate zones:**

| Zone | % Max HR | Perceived Effort | Purpose |
|------|----------|-------------------|---------|
| Zone 1 | 50-60% | Very light | Recovery, warm-up |
| Zone 2 | 60-70% | Light | Fat burning, aerobic base |
| Zone 3 | 70-80% | Moderate | Aerobic fitness |
| Zone 4 | 80-90% | Hard | Anaerobic threshold |
| Zone 5 | 90-100% | Maximum | Peak performance, sprints |

**RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) Scale:**

| RPE | Description | Reps in Reserve (RIR) |
|-----|-------------|----------------------|
| 10 | Maximum effort, could not do another rep | 0 |
| 9 | Very hard, maybe 1 more rep possible | 1 |
| 8 | Hard, could do 2 more reps | 2 |
| 7 | Challenging, could do 3 more reps | 3 |
| 6 | Moderate, could do 4+ more reps | 4+ |
| 5 | Easy, warm-up weight | 5+ |

### Time

*How long is each session?*

| Component | Duration |
|-----------|----------|
| Warm-up | 5-10 minutes |
| Main workout | 30-60 minutes (resistance) or 20-60 minutes (cardio) |
| Cool-down | 5-10 minutes |
| Total session | 45-75 minutes |

**Key considerations:**
- Sessions longer than 75-90 minutes may elevate cortisol excessively
- Quality (intensity and focus) matters more than duration
- Time includes rest periods between sets

### Type

*What kind of exercise?*

| Category | Examples | Best For |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Compound lifts | Squat, deadlift, bench press, rows, overhead press | Strength, muscle building, efficiency |
| Isolation exercises | Bicep curls, lateral raises, leg extensions | Targeting weak points, aesthetics |
| Plyometrics | Box jumps, jump squats, clap push-ups | Power, athleticism |
| Steady-state cardio | Running, cycling, swimming | Aerobic base, fat loss |
| HIIT | Intervals, sprints, circuit training | Conditioning, time efficiency |
| Flexibility/Mobility | Stretching, yoga, foam rolling | Injury prevention, range of motion |

## 3. Progressive Overload

### Methods of Overload

Progressive overload is not just "add more weight." There are multiple
mechanisms:

| Method | How | Example |
|--------|-----|---------|
| **Increase load** | Add weight to the bar/machine | Bench press 60kg to 62.5kg |
| **Increase reps** | Do more repetitions at the same weight | 3x8 to 3x10 |
| **Increase sets** | Add more working sets | 3x8 to 4x8 |
| **Increase frequency** | Train the muscle more often per week | 1x/week to 2x/week |
| **Increase time under tension** | Slow down the eccentric (lowering) phase | 2-second lower to 4-second lower |
| **Decrease rest periods** | Reduce time between sets | 90 seconds to 60 seconds |
| **Increase range of motion** | Use fuller ROM | Half squat to full depth squat |
| **Increase complexity** | Progress to harder exercise variants | Push-up to decline push-up |

### Rate of Progression

**The 10% Rule:**
Increase total training volume (weight x reps x sets) by no more than 10% per
week to minimize injury risk.

**ACSM Guidelines:**
Changes in total training volume should be made in increments of 2.5% to 5.0%
per week to avoid overtraining.

**Practical progression models:**

| Level | Weekly Load Increase | Example |
|-------|---------------------|---------|
| Beginner (0-12 months) | 2.5-5 kg per session (upper) / 5-10 kg (lower) | Rapid "newbie gains" |
| Intermediate (1-3 years) | 1-2.5 kg per week | Slower but steady |
| Advanced (3+ years) | 0.5-1 kg per month | Micro-progression, periodization essential |

### Double Progression Model

A practical approach for most trainees:

1. Set a rep range target (e.g., 3 sets of 8-12 reps)
2. Start at the bottom of the range with a given weight (3x8)
3. Each session, try to add 1 rep to at least one set
4. When you hit the top of the range on all sets (3x12), increase the weight
5. Drop back to the bottom of the range with the new weight (3x8)
6. Repeat

## 4. Periodization Models

Periodization is the systematic planning of training into cycles to optimize
adaptation, prevent plateaus, and manage fatigue.

### Periodization Hierarchy

| Cycle | Duration | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Macrocycle** | 6-12 months | Overall training goal (annual plan) |
| **Mesocycle** | 3-6 weeks | Training block with specific focus |
| **Microcycle** | 1 week | Day-to-day training plan |

### Linear Periodization

The simplest model — systematically increase intensity while decreasing volume
over time.

```
Mesocycle 1 (weeks 1-4):  4x12 @ 60% 1RM  — Hypertrophy / base building
Mesocycle 2 (weeks 5-8):  4x8  @ 70% 1RM  — Strength-hypertrophy
Mesocycle 3 (weeks 9-12): 5x5  @ 80% 1RM  — Strength
Mesocycle 4 (weeks 13-16):5x3  @ 90% 1RM  — Peaking / max strength
```

**Best for:** Beginners, athletes peaking for competition, simple and effective.

**Limitation:** Only one quality is trained per phase; others may detrain.

### Undulating Periodization (DUP — Daily Undulating)

Vary intensity and volume within each week.

```
Monday:    4x5  @ 85% 1RM  — Heavy / Strength
Wednesday: 3x12 @ 65% 1RM  — Moderate / Hypertrophy
Friday:    4x8  @ 75% 1RM  — Medium / Power
```

**Best for:** Intermediate to advanced, maintaining multiple qualities simultaneously.

**Research support:** Studies show DUP can produce equal or greater strength
and hypertrophy gains compared to linear periodization.

### Block Periodization

Concentrate training into focused blocks, each emphasizing one primary quality.

```
Block 1 (3-4 weeks): Accumulation — High volume, moderate intensity
Block 2 (3-4 weeks): Transmutation — Moderate volume, high intensity
Block 3 (2-3 weeks): Realization — Low volume, peak intensity
```

**Best for:** Advanced athletes, sport-specific peaking.

### Autoregulated Periodization

Adjust training variables based on daily readiness rather than fixed percentages.

- Use RPE/RIR to determine working weights
- If RPE is higher than expected, reduce volume or intensity
- If RPE is lower than expected, push slightly harder
- Track trends over weeks, not individual sessions

**Best for:** Anyone with variable schedules, stress levels, or recovery capacity.

## 5. Training Splits

### Full Body (3 days/week)

```
Day 1 (Mon): Full Body A
Day 2 (Wed): Full Body B
Day 3 (Fri): Full Body C
```

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| High frequency per muscle (3x/week) | Sessions can be long |
| Ideal for beginners | Limited exercise variety per session |
| Works well with 3 days available | Fatigue accumulates within session |
| Maximum calorie burn per session | May need to prioritize compounds |

**Best for:** Beginners (0-12 months), anyone with 3 days/week available,
general fitness goals.

**Template:**
- 1 squat/leg press pattern
- 1 hip hinge/deadlift pattern
- 1 horizontal push (bench/push-up)
- 1 horizontal pull (row)
- 1 vertical push (overhead press)
- 1 vertical pull (pull-up/lat pulldown)
- 1-2 accessories (arms, core, calves)

### Upper/Lower (4 days/week)

```
Day 1 (Mon): Upper Body
Day 2 (Tue): Lower Body
Day 3 (Thu): Upper Body
Day 4 (Fri): Lower Body
```

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Each muscle hit 2x/week | 4 gym days required |
| Good balance of volume and recovery | Upper days can be long |
| Natural progression from full body | Less specialized than PPL |
| Manageable session length | |

**Best for:** Intermediate trainees, balanced strength and hypertrophy goals,
4 days/week available.

### Push/Pull/Legs — PPL (5-6 days/week)

```
Day 1 (Mon): Push (chest, shoulders, triceps)
Day 2 (Tue): Pull (back, biceps, rear delts)
Day 3 (Wed): Legs (quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves)
Day 4 (Thu): Push
Day 5 (Fri): Pull
Day 6 (Sat): Legs
```

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| High volume per muscle group | Requires 5-6 days/week |
| Synergistic muscle groupings | High time commitment |
| Great for hypertrophy | May be excessive for beginners |
| Lots of exercise variety | Recovery can be challenging |

**Best for:** Intermediate to advanced, hypertrophy-focused, 5-6 days available.

**Muscle group mapping:**
- **Push:** Bench press, overhead press, incline press, dips, lateral raises, tricep extensions
- **Pull:** Deadlift/rows, pull-ups, lat pulldown, face pulls, bicep curls, rear delt flyes
- **Legs:** Squat, leg press, Romanian deadlift, lunges, leg curl, leg extension, calf raises

### Choosing a Split — Decision Matrix

| Factor | Full Body | Upper/Lower | PPL |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Days available | 3 | 4 | 5-6 |
| Experience level | Beginner | Intermediate | Intermediate-Advanced |
| Primary goal | General fitness | Balanced | Hypertrophy |
| Session duration | 60-75 min | 45-60 min | 45-75 min |
| Volume per muscle/week | Moderate | Moderate-High | High |
| Frequency per muscle/week | 3x | 2x | 2x |
| Recovery demands | Low-Moderate | Moderate | High |

## 6. Workout Structure

### Session Template

Every training session should follow this structure:

#### 1. Warm-Up (5-10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Increase blood flow, raise core temperature, activate target muscles,
prepare joints, reduce injury risk.

**Components:**
- **General warm-up (3-5 min):** Light cardio — rowing, cycling, brisk walking, jump rope
- **Dynamic stretching (2-3 min):** Leg swings, arm circles, hip circles, torso rotations
- **Activation (2-3 min):** Band work, bodyweight movements targeting the day's muscles
- **Ramp-up sets:** 2-3 progressively heavier sets before working weight

**Example ramp-up for a 100kg squat working weight:**
- Set 1: Bar only (20kg) x 10
- Set 2: 60kg x 5
- Set 3: 80kg x 3
- Working sets: 100kg x target reps

#### 2. Main Compound Lifts (20-30 minutes)

- Perform the heaviest, most demanding exercises first
- Compound movements that use the most muscle mass
- 3-5 working sets per exercise
- Rest 2-3 minutes between sets for strength; 60-90 seconds for hypertrophy

#### 3. Accessory / Isolation Work (15-25 minutes)

- Target weak points and lagging muscle groups
- Higher rep ranges (8-15 reps)
- Shorter rest periods (60-90 seconds)
- 2-4 sets per exercise
- Supersets and circuits are appropriate here for time efficiency

#### 4. Cool-Down (5-10 minutes)

- Light cardio (3-5 min) to bring heart rate down gradually
- Static stretching of muscles trained (hold 20-30 seconds per stretch)
- Foam rolling on tight areas
- Deep breathing for parasympathetic activation

### Exercise Order Principles

1. **Compound before isolation** — Squat before leg extension
2. **Multi-joint before single-joint** — Bench press before tricep pushdown
3. **Weakest/priority muscles early** — When fresh and strongest
4. **Neurologically demanding before simple** — Olympic lifts before curls
5. **Bilateral before unilateral** — Barbell squat before Bulgarian split squat
6. **Free weights before machines** — Require more stabilization when fresh

### Set and Rep Schemes

| Goal | Sets | Reps | Rest | Tempo |
|------|------|------|------|-------|
| Maximal strength | 4-6 | 1-5 | 3-5 min | Controlled |
| Strength-hypertrophy | 3-5 | 6-8 | 2-3 min | Moderate |
| Hypertrophy | 3-4 | 8-12 | 60-90 sec | Controlled eccentric |
| Muscular endurance | 2-3 | 15-20+ | 30-60 sec | Rhythmic |
| Power | 3-5 | 1-5 | 2-3 min | Explosive |

### Volume Guidelines

**Weekly set volume per muscle group:**

| Level | Sets per Muscle/Week | Notes |
|-------|---------------------|-------|
| Minimum effective volume | 6-8 sets | Maintenance or beginners |
| Moderate volume | 10-14 sets | General hypertrophy |
| High volume | 15-20 sets | Advanced, recovery must support |
| Maximum recoverable volume | 20-25 sets | Risk of overtraining if sustained |

## 7. Rest and Recovery

### Sleep

Sleep is the single most important recovery factor.

| Factor | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Duration | 7-9 hours per night |
| Consistency | Same sleep/wake times (within 30 min) |
| Quality indicators | Fall asleep within 15-20 min, minimal waking |
| Pre-sleep | No screens 30-60 min before bed, cool dark room |

**Impact of poor sleep on training:**
- Reduced strength and power output
- Impaired muscle protein synthesis
- Elevated cortisol (catabolic hormone)
- Reduced growth hormone release (peaks during deep sleep)
- Impaired decision-making and coordination (injury risk)

### Active Recovery

**Between training days:**
- Light walking (20-30 minutes)
- Foam rolling and mobility work
- Light swimming or cycling
- Yoga or stretching
- Low-intensity movement that promotes blood flow without creating fatigue

### Recovery Factors Checklist

```
RECOVERY ASSESSMENT:
[ ] Sleep: 7-9 hours, consistent schedule
[ ] Nutrition: Adequate calories, protein (1.6-2.2g/kg/day)
[ ] Hydration: 2-3+ liters of water daily
[ ] Stress: Mental/emotional stress within manageable levels
[ ] Soreness: DOMS resolved before training same muscle intensely
[ ] Energy: Feeling motivated and energized for training
[ ] Performance: Maintaining or improving in the gym
[ ] Mood: Stable, no unusual irritability or apathy
```

## 8. Deload Protocols

### When to Deload

**Scheduled deloads:**
- Every 4-6 weeks for intermediates
- Every 6-8 weeks for advanced trainees
- Every 3-4 weeks during very high-intensity phases

**Reactive deloads (based on signals):**

| Signal | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Performance declining for 2+ sessions | Deload immediately |
| Persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep | Deload within the week |
| Joint pain or nagging injuries | Deload and address the issue |
| Motivation consistently low | Deload or change program |
| Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above baseline | Deload |
| Sleep quality deteriorating | Deload and prioritize sleep |

### Deload Methods

| Method | How | Best For |
|--------|-----|----------|
| **Volume deload** | Keep weight the same, reduce sets by 40-50% | Maintaining strength while recovering |
| **Intensity deload** | Keep sets/reps the same, reduce weight by 40-50% | Practicing movement patterns at lighter loads |
| **Frequency deload** | Reduce training days (e.g., 5 to 3) | When life stress is high |
| **Full rest** | Take 5-7 days completely off | After competition or extended training block |

**Recommended default:** Volume deload — reduce total sets to 50-60% of normal
while keeping intensity (weight on bar) the same or slightly reduced. This
maintains neural adaptations while allowing recovery.

### Post-Deload Expectations

- Do NOT jump back to pre-deload volume immediately
- First week back: 80-85% of pre-deload volume
- Second week: 90-95%
- Third week: 100% and resume progression

## 9. Programming by Goal

### Strength

| Variable | Guideline |
|----------|-----------|
| Frequency | 3-5 days/week |
| Intensity | 80-95% 1RM |
| Rep range | 1-5 reps |
| Sets | 4-6 per exercise |
| Rest | 3-5 minutes |
| Exercise focus | Big compounds: squat, bench, deadlift, overhead press |
| Periodization | Linear or block |
| Deload frequency | Every 4-6 weeks |

### Hypertrophy (Muscle Building)

| Variable | Guideline |
|----------|-----------|
| Frequency | 4-6 days/week |
| Intensity | 60-80% 1RM |
| Rep range | 6-12 reps (primary), 12-20 (accessory) |
| Sets | 10-20 per muscle group per week |
| Rest | 60-120 seconds |
| Exercise focus | Mix of compounds and isolations |
| Periodization | Undulating (DUP) or block |
| Deload frequency | Every 4-6 weeks |

### Fat Loss

| Variable | Guideline |
|----------|-----------|
| Frequency | 3-5 days/week (resistance) + 2-3 (cardio) |
| Intensity | 70-85% 1RM (maintain strength during deficit) |
| Rep range | 6-12 reps |
| Sets | Maintain pre-diet volume or reduce slightly |
| Rest | 60-90 seconds |
| Exercise focus | Compounds to preserve muscle mass |
| Periodization | Maintain current program, do not add volume in a deficit |
| Cardio | LISS (walking) preferred; HIIT 1-2x/week max |

**Critical rule:** The primary driver of fat loss is caloric deficit through
nutrition. Training during fat loss is about preserving muscle, not burning calories.

### General Fitness / Health

| Variable | Guideline |
|----------|-----------|
| Frequency | 3-4 days/week |
| Intensity | Moderate (RPE 6-8) |
| Rep range | 8-15 reps |
| Sets | 2-3 per exercise |
| Rest | 60-90 seconds |
| Exercise focus | Full body, balanced movement patterns |
| Cardio | 150 min/week moderate or 75 min/week vigorous (WHO guidelines) |
| Flexibility | 2-3 sessions/week, 10-15 minutes |

## 10. Programming by Experience Level

### Beginner (0-12 months consistent training)

- **Split:** Full body, 3x/week
- **Progression:** Linear (add weight every session)
- **Volume:** 2-3 sets per exercise, 6-8 exercises
- **Program duration:** Run for 8-12 weeks before reassessing
- **Focus:** Master compound movement patterns with proper form
- **Common mistake:** Too much volume, too many exercises, program hopping

### Intermediate (1-3 years consistent training)

- **Split:** Upper/Lower or PPL
- **Progression:** Weekly or bi-weekly (double progression model)
- **Volume:** 3-4 sets per exercise, higher weekly volume
- **Program duration:** 8-16 week blocks
- **Focus:** Progressive overload, periodization introduction, weak point work
- **Common mistake:** Not periodizing, avoiding deloads, neglecting weak points

### Advanced (3+ years consistent training)

- **Split:** PPL or specialized splits
- **Progression:** Monthly, micro-loading, autoregulated
- **Volume:** Higher (15-25 sets/muscle/week), carefully managed
- **Program duration:** Block periodization (3-4 week blocks)
- **Focus:** Specificity, peak performance, recovery management
- **Common mistake:** Overtraining, ignoring recovery, too much variation

## 11. Common Programming Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| No progression plan | No stimulus for adaptation | Use double progression or percentage-based |
| Too much volume too soon | Exceeds recovery capacity | Start low, add 1-2 sets per muscle/week |
| Skipping warm-ups | Increased injury risk | Budget 10 min minimum |
| Ignoring compound lifts | Lower overall muscle stimulus | Build program around squat/bench/deadlift/row/press |
| Training to failure every set | CNS fatigue, joint stress | Keep most sets at RPE 7-8 (2-3 RIR) |
| No deloads | Accumulated fatigue, staleness | Schedule deloads every 4-8 weeks |
| Program hopping | Never adapts to any stimulus | Commit to a program for at least 8 weeks |
| Imbalanced push:pull ratio | Shoulder injuries, postural issues | Aim for 1:1 or even 2:3 push:pull ratio |
| Neglecting unilateral work | Strength imbalances | Include at least 1 unilateral exercise per session |
| Ego lifting | Poor form, injury | Use RPE, leave ego at the door |

### Plan Checklist

Before delivering any workout plan:

```
PLAN QUALITY CHECK:
[ ] Goal is clearly defined (strength, hypertrophy, fat loss, general)
[ ] Schedule matches available days and time
[ ] Split is appropriate for experience level
[ ] Progressive overload method is specified
[ ] Warm-up and cool-down included for every session
[ ] Rest periods specified for each exercise type
[ ] Deload week is scheduled
[ ] Volume is within recommended ranges
[ ] Push:pull ratio is balanced (1:1 or better)
[ ] Plan accounts for available equipment
[ ] Progression criteria are clear ("increase weight when...")
[ ] Plan has a defined duration and reassessment date
```
