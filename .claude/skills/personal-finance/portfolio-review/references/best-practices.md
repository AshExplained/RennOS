## 1. Asset Allocation by Risk Profile

Asset allocation is the single largest driver of portfolio outcomes. Match allocation to risk profile, time horizon, and life stage.

### Risk Profile Definitions

| Profile | Description | Volatility Tolerance | Time Horizon |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|--------------|
| Conservative | Capital preservation, income focus | Low | 0–5 years |
| Moderately Conservative | Stability with modest growth | Low–Medium | 3–7 years |
| Moderate | Balanced growth and stability | Medium | 5–15 years |
| Moderately Aggressive | Growth-oriented, accepts drawdowns | Medium–High | 10–20 years |
| Aggressive | Maximum growth, high volatility | High | 15+ years |

### Target Allocations by Risk Profile

**Conservative**
- Stocks: 20–30%
- Bonds: 50–60%
- Cash/Short-term: 10–20%
- Alternatives (REITs, commodities): 0–5%

**Moderately Conservative**
- Stocks: 35–45%
- Bonds: 40–50%
- Cash/Short-term: 5–15%
- Alternatives: 0–5%

**Moderate**
- Stocks: 55–65%
- Bonds: 30–35%
- Cash/Short-term: 0–5%
- Alternatives: 5–10%

**Moderately Aggressive**
- Stocks: 70–80%
- Bonds: 15–25%
- Cash/Short-term: 0–5%
- Alternatives: 5–10%

**Aggressive**
- Stocks: 85–100%
- Bonds: 0–10%
- Cash/Short-term: 0–5%
- Alternatives: 5–15%

### Age-Based Allocation Guidelines

Three widely used formulas for determining equity allocation:

| Rule | Formula | Profile | Rationale |
|------|---------|---------|-----------|
| Traditional | 100 − Age = % in stocks | Conservative | Classic rule, appropriate for shorter retirements |
| Modern | 110 − Age = % in stocks | Moderate | Accounts for longer life expectancy |
| Aggressive Modern | 120 − Age = % in stocks | Aggressive | For investors needing maximum long-term growth |

**Allocation by Life Stage (using Modern 110 rule)**

| Age Range | Stocks | Bonds | Cash/Alt | Risk Profile |
|-----------|--------|-------|----------|--------------|
| 20s–30s | 80–90% | 5–15% | 0–5% | Aggressive |
| 40s | 65–70% | 25–30% | 0–5% | Moderate–Aggressive |
| 50s | 55–60% | 35–40% | 5% | Moderate |
| 60s | 40–50% | 40–50% | 5–15% | Moderately Conservative |
| 70s | 30–40% | 45–55% | 10–20% | Conservative |
| 80+ | 15–25% | 50–60% | 25–35% | Very Conservative |

### Equity Sub-Allocation (within stock portion)

A well-diversified equity sleeve typically includes:
- U.S. Large-Cap: 40–50%
- U.S. Small/Mid-Cap: 15–20%
- International Developed: 20–30%
- Emerging Markets: 5–10%

### Fixed Income Sub-Allocation (within bond portion)

- Investment-Grade Corporate: 30–40%
- U.S. Treasuries / Government: 25–35%
- TIPS (Inflation-Protected): 10–15%
- International Bonds: 10–15%
- High-Yield (if risk-appropriate): 0–10%

## 2. Rebalancing Rules

Rebalancing maintains the portfolio's intended risk level and enforces disciplined buy-low, sell-high behavior.

### Two Primary Approaches

#### Calendar-Based Rebalancing

Rebalance on a fixed schedule regardless of drift magnitude.

| Frequency | Best For | Trade-off |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Quarterly | Active investors, volatile markets | Higher transaction costs |
| Semi-annual | Most investors | Good balance of cost vs. drift |
| Annual | Tax-sensitive, low-volatility portfolios | Risk of excessive drift |

**Guideline:** Semi-annual or annual rebalancing is sufficient for most individual investors. Avoid monthly or weekly rebalancing — the added transaction costs and tax events typically outweigh any benefit.

#### Threshold-Based Rebalancing

Rebalance only when allocations drift beyond tolerance bands.

**The 5/25 Rule (industry standard):**
- **Absolute trigger:** Rebalance when any major asset class drifts 5 percentage points from target
- **Relative trigger:** Rebalance when any sub-asset class drifts 25% from its target weight
- **Use whichever trigger is smaller**

Example applications:
- Target 60% stocks → Rebalance if stocks reach 65% or drop to 55%
- Target 20% international → Rebalance if it reaches 25% (absolute) or 15% (relative 25% of 20% = 5 points)
- Target 5% REITs → Rebalance if it reaches 6.25% or 3.75% (relative trigger = 1.25 points, smaller than 5-point absolute)

#### Hybrid Approach (Recommended)

Combine both methods: review on a calendar schedule (semi-annual) but only execute trades when threshold triggers are breached. This minimizes unnecessary transactions while preventing excessive drift.

### Rebalancing Execution Priority

1. **Tax-advantaged accounts first** — Trades in IRAs, 401(k)s, and Roth accounts incur no capital gains tax
2. **Cash flow rebalancing** — Direct new contributions and dividends toward underweight positions
3. **Taxable accounts last** — Only rebalance here when drift is significant, and pair with tax-loss harvesting

### Tax-Loss Harvesting Integration

Tax-loss harvesting can offset gains elsewhere in the portfolio when rebalancing triggers sales at a loss.

**Process:**
1. Identify overweight positions currently trading at a loss relative to purchase price
2. Sell the position to realize the loss for tax purposes
3. Immediately replace with a similar (not "substantially identical") fund to maintain exposure
4. Use the harvested loss to offset realized capital gains, or up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year
5. Carry forward unused losses to future tax years

**Critical Rules:**
- **Wash-sale rule:** Cannot repurchase the same or substantially identical security within 30 days before or after the sale
- **Replacement examples:** Sell Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) → Buy Schwab U.S. Broad Market ETF (SCHB)
- **2026 0% capital gains threshold:** $49,450 (single filers), $98,900 (married filing jointly)

**When to harvest:**
- During regular rebalancing when overweight positions are at a loss
- Year-end review (before December 31)
- After significant market downturns
- When realizing gains elsewhere that need offsetting

## 3. Diversification Principles

Diversification reduces unsystematic risk by spreading investments across assets with low or negative correlation.

### Core Principles

1. **Asset class diversification** — Spread across stocks, bonds, real estate, commodities, and alternatives
2. **Geographic diversification** — Include domestic and international markets (developed and emerging)
3. **Sector diversification** — Avoid over-concentration in any single industry
4. **Factor diversification** — Blend growth, value, size, and quality factors
5. **Time diversification** — Use dollar-cost averaging for new investments

### Correlation Awareness

| Asset Pair | Typical Correlation | Diversification Benefit |
|------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| U.S. Large-Cap ↔ U.S. Small-Cap | 0.7–0.9 | Low |
| U.S. Stocks ↔ International Developed | 0.6–0.8 | Moderate |
| U.S. Stocks ↔ Emerging Markets | 0.5–0.7 | Moderate |
| Stocks ↔ Investment-Grade Bonds | −0.2 to 0.3 | High |
| Stocks ↔ Treasuries | −0.3 to 0.1 | High |
| Stocks ↔ Gold | −0.1 to 0.3 | High |
| Stocks ↔ REITs | 0.5–0.7 | Moderate |
| Bonds ↔ Commodities | −0.2 to 0.2 | High |

**Key insight:** True diversification requires assets that behave differently under stress, not just assets with different names. During market crises, correlations between risky assets tend to increase — this is when bond and gold allocations provide their greatest benefit.

### Common Diversification Pitfalls

- **Overdiversification:** Holding 50+ positions adds complexity without meaningful risk reduction. Most diversification benefit is captured with 15–30 uncorrelated positions
- **False diversification:** Owning 10 U.S. large-cap growth funds provides no meaningful diversification
- **Ignoring correlation shifts:** Correlations change over time — review annually
- **Home bias:** Overweighting domestic markets (common for U.S. investors) — international stocks represent ~40% of global market cap

## 4. Fee Analysis Methodology

Investment fees compound against you. A 1% annual fee difference can reduce terminal wealth by ~28% over 25 years.

### Fee Taxonomy

#### Visible Fees

| Fee Type | Description | Benchmark Range |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Expense Ratio (Index ETFs) | Annual fund operating cost | 0.03%–0.10% |
| Expense Ratio (Target-Date) | Blended fund operating cost | 0.08%–0.20% |
| Expense Ratio (Active Funds) | Manager + operating cost | 0.50%–0.85% (competitive) |
| Advisory Fee (Robo-advisor) | Automated portfolio management | 0.15%–0.50% |
| Advisory Fee (Traditional) | Human financial advisor | 0.75%–1.50% AUM |
| Trading Commissions | Per-trade charges | $0 (most brokers) – $6.95 |

#### Hidden & Indirect Fees

| Fee Type | Description | Typical Impact |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| 12b-1 Fees | Marketing/distribution fees embedded in mutual funds | Up to 1.0% annually |
| Bid-Ask Spread | Cost of executing trades (especially in less liquid securities) | $0.01–$0.10+ per share |
| Cash Drag | Uninvested cash in brokerage accounts earning below-market rates | 0.1%–0.5% drag |
| Soft Dollar Costs | Research services paid via trading commissions | 0.1%–0.3% estimated |
| Turnover Costs | Transaction costs from frequent fund trading (high-turnover funds) | Up to 1.5% annually |
| Account Fees | Maintenance, inactivity, transfer, or closing fees | $0–$75/year |
| Wrap Fees | All-inclusive fee covering trading, advice, and custody | 1.0%–3.0% AUM |

### Fee Analysis Process

**Step 1: Inventory all holdings**
List every fund, ETF, stock, and account with its associated fee structure.

**Step 2: Calculate Weighted Average Expense Ratio (WAER)**
```
WAER = Σ (Fund Weight × Fund Expense Ratio)
```
Example: 60% in a 0.03% ETF + 40% in a 0.65% mutual fund = 0.268% WAER

**Step 3: Identify all-in cost**
```
All-In Cost = WAER + Advisory Fees + Hidden Costs (estimated)
```

**Step 4: Benchmark against targets**
| Investor Type | Target All-In Cost |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Self-directed (index) | < 0.10% |
| Robo-advised | 0.25%–0.50% |
| Advisor-managed (simple) | 0.75%–1.25% |
| Advisor-managed (complex) | 1.00%–1.50% |

**Step 5: Quantify long-term fee impact**
```
Fee Impact = Initial Investment × ((1 + Return)^Years − (1 + Return − Fee)^Years)
```
Example: $100,000 at 7% over 25 years
- With 0.10% fees → $527,180
- With 1.10% fees → $412,430
- Fee cost: $114,750 (21.8% of potential wealth)

**Step 6: Flag high-cost holdings**
- Any fund with expense ratio > 0.50% for passive exposure
- Any fund with expense ratio > 1.00% for active management
- Advisory fees > 1.0% without clear value-add (tax management, financial planning, behavioral coaching)
- Any 12b-1 fees (institutional share classes typically eliminate these)

### Fee Analysis Tools

- **FINRA Fund Analyzer** — Compare 18,000+ funds by cost impact over time
- **Morningstar** — Fund expense ratio data and percentile rankings
- **Brokerage statements** — Annual fee summaries (required by regulation)

## 5. Portfolio Health Indicators

Use these metrics to assess overall portfolio quality during each review.

### Performance Metrics

| Metric | What It Measures | Good Target | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|----------|
| Total Return | Absolute portfolio growth | Beats inflation + fees | Below inflation |
| Benchmark-Relative Return | Performance vs. appropriate index | Within ±2% of benchmark | Persistent underperformance > 3% |
| Time-Weighted Return (TWR) | Return excluding cash flow effects | Comparable to benchmark | N/A (diagnostic only) |

### Risk-Adjusted Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Good Target | Red Flag |
|--------|---------|-------------|----------|
| Sharpe Ratio | (Return − Risk-Free Rate) / StdDev | > 0.5 (good), > 1.0 (excellent) | < 0 (losing money vs. risk-free) |
| Sortino Ratio | (Return − Risk-Free Rate) / Downside StdDev | > 1.0 | < 0.5 |
| Calmar Ratio | Annualized Return / Max Drawdown (3yr) | > 0.5 | < 0.2 |

### Drawdown Metrics

| Metric | What It Measures | Acceptable Range | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|----------|
| Maximum Drawdown | Largest peak-to-trough decline | Aligned with risk profile | Exceeds risk tolerance |
| Recovery Time | Months to recover from max drawdown | < 24 months | > 36 months |
| Recovery Factor | Cumulative Return / Max Drawdown | > 2.0 | < 1.0 |

**Maximum Drawdown Expectations by Profile:**
- Conservative: < 10%
- Moderate: < 20%
- Aggressive: < 35%

### Concentration & Diversification Metrics

| Metric | What It Measures | Target | Red Flag |
|--------|-----------------|--------|----------|
| Top 10 Holdings Weight | Single-stock concentration | < 40% of equity sleeve | Any single stock > 10% |
| Sector Concentration | Overweight in one sector | No sector > 25% | Any sector > 35% |
| Geographic Concentration | Home bias assessment | International ≥ 20% of equities | International < 10% |
| Number of Holdings | Breadth of diversification | 15–30 positions (or funds providing broad exposure) | < 5 or > 50 direct holdings |
| Correlation to Benchmark | How closely portfolio tracks market | Depends on strategy | Unexpected high/low correlation |

### Income & Yield Metrics (for income-oriented portfolios)

| Metric | What It Measures | Context |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| Portfolio Yield | Weighted average dividend/coupon yield | Compare to income needs |
| Yield-to-Worst | Minimum yield if bonds are called early | Bond portfolio stress metric |
| Dividend Growth Rate | Annual dividend increase trend | > inflation rate preferred |

### Portfolio Health Checklist

Use this checklist during every review:

- [ ] **Allocation drift** — Is any asset class > 5 points from target?
- [ ] **Performance** — Is the portfolio tracking its benchmark within tolerance?
- [ ] **Risk metrics** — Are Sharpe, Sortino, and drawdown within expected ranges?
- [ ] **Concentration** — Any single holding > 10%? Any sector > 25%?
- [ ] **Fee audit** — Is all-in cost within target range? Any high-cost outliers?
- [ ] **Tax efficiency** — Are high-turnover or income-heavy assets in tax-advantaged accounts?
- [ ] **Income adequacy** — Is yield meeting income needs (if applicable)?
- [ ] **Rebalancing needed** — Does drift exceed 5/25 thresholds?
- [ ] **Life changes** — Has risk profile changed due to age, income, or goals?
- [ ] **Holdings review** — Any underperformers to evaluate (> 2 years lagging)?
