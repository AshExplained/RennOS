# Press Release Best Practices Reference

> Compiled from AP Stylebook guidelines, PR Newswire resources, and industry standards.
> Last updated: 2026-03-24

---

## 1. AP-Style Conventions

### General Principles

AP style prioritizes brevity, clarity, and accuracy. The Associated Press Stylebook is the
standard reference for press release formatting across the industry. 89% of journalists prefer
press releases written in AP format (PR Newswire). Releases that follow AP style require less
editing, signal professionalism, and dramatically improve media pickup rates.

### Capitalization

- **Titles before names:** Capitalize formal titles when they precede a name
  - Correct: "President Jane Smith announced..."
  - Correct: "Jane Smith, president of XYZ Corp, announced..."
- **Titles after names or standalone:** Lowercase
- **Company/product names:** Respect the entity's own capitalization (iPhone, PayPal, LinkedIn)
- **Headlines:** Use title case — capitalize first and last words plus all major words
  - Do NOT capitalize articles (a, an, the), conjunctions (and, but, or), or prepositions
    of three letters or fewer, unless they start the headline

### Numbers

- **Spell out** one through nine
- **Use numerals** for 10 and above
- **Always use numerals** for: ages, percentages, temperatures, times, dates, monetary amounts,
  measurements, dimensions, speeds, sports scores, and addresses
- **Spell out** numbers that begin a sentence (exception: years)
- **Ordinals:** Spell out first through ninth; use numerals for 10th and above
- **Large numbers:** Use a combination — "1 million" not "1,000,000"
- **Percentages:** Use numerals with "percent" spelled out (AP style): "5 percent"

### Dates and Times

- **Abbreviate months** when used with a specific date: Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec.
- **Do not abbreviate:** March, April, May, June, July
- **Date format:** Month Day, Year — "March 24, 2026"
- **No ordinal suffixes:** Write "March 24" not "March 24th"
- **Time format:** Use figures with a.m./p.m. — "2 p.m." not "2:00 PM"
- **Noon and midnight:** Use the words, not "12 p.m." or "12 a.m."
- **Comma usage:** Use commas around the year when month and day are present
  - "On March 24, 2026, the company..." but "In March 2026 the company..."

### Punctuation

- **Serial comma (Oxford comma):** NOT used in AP style
  - Correct: "red, white and blue" — not "red, white, and blue"
  - Exception: Use the serial comma when omitting it creates ambiguity
- **Single space** after periods (not double)
- **Quotation marks:** Periods and commas go inside quotation marks
- **Em dashes:** Use with spaces on both sides — like this
- **Hyphens:** Use to link compound modifiers before nouns ("well-known author")

### Abbreviations

- **State names:** Abbreviate in datelines and body text using AP abbreviations (not postal codes)
  - Use: Calif., Conn., Mass. — NOT: CA, CT, MA
  - **Never abbreviate:** Alaska, Hawaii, Idaho, Iowa, Maine, Ohio, Texas, Utah
- **U.S.:** Use periods when used as an adjective; spell out "United States" as a noun
- **Technology terms (2026 updates):**
  - "AI" acceptable on first reference (no need to spell out)
  - "email" — one word, no hyphen
  - "internet" — lowercase
  - "website" — one word, lowercase
  - "ecommerce" — one word, no hyphen

### Spacing and Typography

- Use standard, readable fonts: Times New Roman, Arial, or Calibri
- Body text: 10-12 point font size
- Headlines: 14-16 point font size
- Single-spaced body text with a blank line between paragraphs
- Left-aligned text (no justified alignment)

## 2. Inverted Pyramid Structure

### Concept

The inverted pyramid places the most fundamental information at the top and arranges remaining
details in descending order of importance. The "base" of the pyramid — the broadest, most
essential facts — appears at the top of the story. This structure originated in telegraph-era
journalism and remains the standard for news writing and press releases.

### Why It Matters

- Readers can grasp the essential story from the first paragraph alone
- Editors can cut from the bottom without losing critical information
- Journalists can quickly assess newsworthiness
- Readership drop-off is steep: 100% read the headline, ~70% the first paragraph, ~50% the
  second, ~30% the third, ~10% the fourth (eReleases)

### Layer 1: The Lead (Paragraph 1)

The lead must answer the Five Ws:
- **Who** — The person, company, or organization involved
- **What** — The announcement, action, or event
- **When** — The date, timeframe, or timing
- **Where** — The location or market
- **Why** — The significance or reason

Write the lead as a single paragraph of 2-3 sentences. A reader who stops after this paragraph
should still understand the complete story at a basic level.

### Layer 2: Supporting Details (Paragraphs 2-3)

Expand on the Five Ws with:
- Key data points, statistics, or metrics
- Context that explains why this matters now
- A quote from a primary spokesperson (see Section 4)
- Specific details about the product, event, or initiative

### Layer 3: Background and Context (Paragraphs 4-5)

Provide secondary information:
- Industry context or market trends
- Historical background or previous milestones
- A second quote from a partner, customer, or expert (optional)
- Technical details or specifications

### Layer 4: Boilerplate and Contact (Final Section)

The least news-critical but still essential section:
- Company boilerplate (see Section 6)
- Media contact information

### Practical Tips

- Aim for 3-5 body paragraphs before the boilerplate
- Keep paragraphs short: 2-3 sentences each for readability
- Total word count: 300-500 words (one page)
- Every paragraph should be independently deletable from the bottom up without
  breaking the story's coherence

## 3. Headline and Subheadline Best Practices

### Headlines

- **Length:** 51-100 characters performs best; never exceed 100 characters
- **Voice:** Use strong, active verbs — never passive voice
- **Tense:** Present tense for current events ("launches" not "launched")
- **Content:** Include the company name and the core news
- **Clarity:** A reader should understand the story from the headline alone
- **Case:** Title case per AP style (capitalize first/last word + all major words)
- **Avoid:** Clickbait, questions, exclamation marks, jargon, abbreviations (except
  universally recognized ones)

**Strong examples:**
- "Acme Corp Launches AI-Powered Supply Chain Platform"
- "XYZ Foundation Awards $5 Million in Climate Research Grants"

**Weak examples:**
- "Exciting New Product Launch!" (vague, exclamation mark)
- "Company Makes Announcement" (no specificity)

### Subheadlines

- **Purpose:** Provide additional context the headline cannot fit
- **Length:** One line, typically 120-160 characters
- **Content:** Elaborate on the "why" or "how" — add a detail that deepens the headline
- **Case:** Sentence case (capitalize only first word and proper nouns)
- **Tone:** Slightly more descriptive than the headline; can include a data point

**Example pairing:**
- Headline: "Acme Corp Launches AI-Powered Supply Chain Platform"
- Subheadline: "New platform reduces logistics costs by 30% for mid-market manufacturers"

## 4. Quote Writing Best Practices

### Purpose of Quotes

Quotes humanize press releases and provide a voice behind the announcement. They should offer
insight, opinion, or forward-looking vision — never repeat facts already stated in the body.
Journalists look for quotable soundbites they can pull directly into their stories.

### How Many Quotes

- **Standard:** 1-2 quotes per press release
- **One quote** is sufficient for routine announcements
- **Two quotes** for major announcements — one from the company spokesperson, one from a
  partner, customer, or external expert
- **Never** include more than two quoted sources — it clutters the release

### Attribution Format (AP Style)

- **First reference:** Full name, title, and organization
  - `"Statement here," said Jane Smith, CEO of Acme Corp.`
- **Subsequent references:** Last name only
  - `"Follow-up statement," Smith said.`
- **Attribution verb:** Always use **"said"** — past tense, neutral
  - Do NOT use: "stated," "announced," "declared," "expressed," "noted," "shared,"
    "commented," "remarked," "believes"
  - Exception: "according to" for citing written/published sources
- **Attribution placement:**
  - One-sentence quote: Attribution at the end after a comma
  - Multi-sentence quote: Attribution after the first sentence; do NOT repeat at the end
  - Multiple speakers in one section: Attribution before the quote with a comma (single
    sentence) or colon (multi-sentence)

### Attribution Placement Examples

**One-sentence quote:**
```
"This partnership will transform how we serve our customers," said Jane Smith, CEO of Acme Corp.
```

**Multi-sentence quote:**
```
"This partnership will transform how we serve our customers," said Jane Smith, CEO of Acme Corp.
"We've spent two years developing this capability, and today marks a major milestone for our team."
```

**Introducing source before the quote:**
```
Jane Smith, CEO of Acme Corp, described the significance of the partnership.

"This will transform how we serve our customers. We've spent two years building toward
this moment," Smith said.
```

### What Makes a Good Quote

- **Conversational tone:** Should sound like a real person speaking, not a corporate document
- **Forward-looking:** Signal future direction, growth, or impact
- **Insightful:** Provide perspective that facts alone cannot convey
- **Concise:** 1-2 sentences maximum; 3 sentences is the hard ceiling
- **Unique voice:** Should not be interchangeable with any other company's spokesperson

### What Makes a Bad Quote

- Restating facts already in the press release body
- Corporate jargon ("synergy," "leverage," "ecosystem," "paradigm shift")
- Overly polished language that sounds written, not spoken
- Generic statements ("We are excited to announce...")
- Attributing a single quote to multiple people

### Quote Placement in the Release

- **Opening (rare):** Only for major milestones or bold statements — grabs attention immediately
- **Middle (most common):** After presenting the key facts in paragraphs 1-2; the quote then
  reinforces and adds a human dimension
- **Closing (optional second quote):** A forward-looking statement that leaves a lasting
  impression

### Formatting Rules

- Enclose in double quotation marks
- Do NOT italicize quotes
- Periods and commas go inside quotation marks
- Each new speaker gets a new paragraph
- Never attribute one quote to multiple people

## 5. Press Release Header and Structure Format

### Header Block

```
FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE                    Contact: [Name]
                                         Phone: [Number]
                                         Email: [Address]
                                         Website: [URL]
```

- "FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE" in ALL CAPS, left-aligned
- OR "EMBARGOED FOR RELEASE UNTIL [DATE AND TIME]" if embargo applies
- Contact information right-aligned on same lines

### Structural Template

1. **Header** — Release timing + contact information
2. **Headline** — Title case, active voice, 51-100 characters
3. **Subheadline** — Sentence case, expands on headline (optional but recommended)
4. **Dateline + Lead** — "CITY, State, Month Day, Year — " followed by the lead paragraph
5. **Body paragraphs** — Supporting details in inverted pyramid order
6. **Quote(s)** — Placed after key facts, attributed per AP style
7. **Additional details** — Background, context, secondary information
8. **Boilerplate** — "About [Company]" section (see Section 6)
9. **Contact block** — Repeated at bottom if not in header
10. **End notation** — "###" centered, or "-30-" (traditional)

### End Notation

- Center "###" on its own line after the boilerplate/contact section
- This signals to editors that the release is complete
- Alternative: "-30-" (traditional journalism notation)

## 6. Boilerplate Format

### Purpose

The boilerplate is a standardized "About [Company]" paragraph that appears at the end of every
press release. It gives journalists quick background on the company without requiring
additional research.

### Structure

- **Length:** 3-5 sentences (100-150 words)
- **Heading:** "About [Company Name]" in bold
- **Content should include:**
  - What the company does (core mission/product)
  - Who it serves (target market/audience)
  - Key differentiator or achievement
  - Scale indicators (founding year, employee count, customer count, revenue — as appropriate)
  - Website URL
- **Tone:** Factual and concise — not promotional or salesy
- **Keywords:** Include relevant industry terms for SEO and AI discoverability

### Example

```
About Acme Corp

Acme Corp is a technology company specializing in AI-powered supply chain solutions for
mid-market manufacturers. Founded in 2018, the company serves more than 500 customers
across 30 countries and has been recognized by Gartner as a Cool Vendor in Supply Chain
Technology. Acme Corp is headquartered in Austin, Texas. For more information, visit
www.acmecorp.com.
```

### Best Practices

- Keep the boilerplate consistent across all releases (update quarterly)
- Do NOT use marketing superlatives ("world-class," "leading," "best-in-class")
- Include the website URL as the last sentence
- Update when major milestones occur (funding rounds, acquisitions, customer milestones)

## 7. Distribution Considerations

### Timing

- **Best days:** Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
- **Best time:** 8:00-10:00 a.m. in the target audience's primary time zone
- **Avoid:** Fridays, weekends, holidays, and days with major competing news events
- **Embargoes:** Only use when you have established journalist relationships;
  always confirm embargo terms in writing

### Distribution Channels

- **Wire services:** PR Newswire, Business Wire, GlobeNewsWire — for broad distribution
  and placement into Google News streams
- **Direct media outreach:** Targeted pitches to relevant journalists with the release attached
- **Company newsroom:** Publish on your own website's press/news page
- **Social media:** Share headline and link across owned channels
- **Email distribution:** Targeted media lists segmented by beat and outlet type

### Targeting

- Identify journalists who cover your industry, beat, or geography
- Personalize the pitch email — reference their recent coverage
- Send the release as body text in the email, not just an attachment
- Follow up once (and only once) 2-3 days after sending

## 8. Digital Press Release Optimization

### SEO Best Practices

- **Headline:** Include the primary keyword naturally in the headline
- **First paragraph:** Include the primary keyword within the first 100 words
- **Body:** Use related keywords and natural variations throughout — avoid keyword stuffing
- **Links:** Include 2-3 relevant hyperlinks (company website, product page, relevant resource)
  - Use descriptive anchor text, not "click here"
- **Meta description:** Write a 150-160 character summary for online distribution platforms
- **URL slug:** Keep it short, descriptive, and keyword-relevant

### Multimedia Integration

- Include at least one high-quality image (product photo, headshot, infographic)
- **Image requirements:**
  - Descriptive file names (not "IMG_4532.jpg")
  - Alt text that describes the image and includes relevant keywords
  - Minimum 300 DPI for print; 72 DPI for web
  - Provide images as separate downloadable files, not embedded only
- Video links (YouTube, Vimeo) increase engagement and pickup rates
- Infographics are effective for data-heavy announcements

### AI Search Optimization (AEO)

- Structure content with clear, factual statements that AI systems can parse
- Use schema markup when publishing to your own newsroom
- Include entity names (company, people, products) consistently
- Write in a way that directly answers likely queries about the announcement
- Boilerplate keywords help large language models associate the company with its industry

### Mobile Readability

- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences)
- Bullet points for lists of features or data points
- Bold key phrases sparingly for scannability
- Avoid large blocks of unbroken text

## 9. Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Burying the lead** — Putting the most important information in paragraph 3 or later
2. **Exceeding 500 words** — Journalists will not read long releases; aim for 300-500
3. **Using passive voice** — "The product was launched by Acme" vs. "Acme launched the product"
4. **Generic quotes** — "We are thrilled to announce..." adds nothing
5. **Missing dateline** — Every release needs a city, state, and date
6. **Inconsistent style** — Mixing AP and non-AP conventions in the same release
7. **No boilerplate** — Journalists need background on your company
8. **No contact information** — A release without contact info is useless to media
9. **Jargon and buzzwords** — Write for a general audience, not your internal team
10. **Multiple calls to action** — A press release is not a marketing email
11. **Skipping the "###"** — Always include end notation so editors know the release is complete
12. **Not proofreading** — Typos and grammatical errors destroy credibility

## 10. Pre-Send Checklist

- [ ] Headline is under 100 characters, active voice, title case
- [ ] Subheadline expands the headline with additional context
- [ ] "FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE" or embargo notice is present
- [ ] Dateline follows AP format (CITY, State, Date —)
- [ ] Lead paragraph answers Who, What, When, Where, Why
- [ ] Inverted pyramid structure: most important info first
- [ ] 1-2 quotes with proper AP-style attribution using "said"
- [ ] Quotes sound conversational, not corporate
- [ ] Total word count is 300-500 words
- [ ] Numbers follow AP conventions (spell out 1-9, numerals for 10+)
- [ ] No serial comma (unless needed for clarity)
- [ ] Boilerplate is included and current
- [ ] Contact information is complete (name, phone, email, website)
- [ ] End notation ("###") is present
- [ ] All proper nouns and titles are correctly capitalized
- [ ] Links are working and use descriptive anchor text
- [ ] At least one multimedia element is included or available
- [ ] Release has been proofread for typos and grammatical errors
- [ ] Brand DNA alignment has been verified
