## 1. Interview Preparation Methodology

### 1.1 The Pre-Interview Research Process

Before any media interview, complete this research checklist:

**About the interviewer:**
- Review their recent articles/interviews (last 5-10 pieces)
- Understand their interviewing style (conversational vs. confrontational)
- Note recurring themes or angles they pursue
- Check their social media for recent takes and interests
- Identify their audience demographics and what they care about

**About the outlet:**
- Understand the audience (age, interests, expertise level)
- Review the outlet's editorial stance and recent coverage of your topic
- Note the format (live vs. pre-recorded, video vs. audio vs. text, length)
- Identify what gets the most engagement on their platform

**About the context:**
- What is the news hook or reason for the interview?
- What has been covered about this topic recently?
- Are there any controversies or sensitive aspects to be aware of?
- What are competitors or peers saying about this topic?

### 1.2 Message Development Framework

**Step 1: Define 3-5 Key Messages**

Every interview should have 3-5 key messages you intend to deliver, regardless of what questions are asked.

For each key message, prepare:

```
KEY MESSAGE: [One clear sentence]
HEADLINE: [How you want this quoted]
EVIDENCE: [Data point, case study, or example that supports it]
SOUNDBITE: [Memorable, quotable version — under 15 words]
BRIDGE PHRASE: [How to transition to this message from any question]
```

**Step 2: Prioritize Messages**

- **Primary message:** The one thing you MUST convey, no matter what
- **Secondary messages:** Important but not critical — deliver if opportunity arises
- **Defensive messages:** Prepared responses for anticipated tough questions

**Step 3: Build the Message Map**

```
              [PRIMARY MESSAGE]
             /        |        \
    [Support 1]  [Support 2]  [Support 3]
        |            |            |
    [Example]    [Data point]  [Story/Anecdote]
```

## 2. Message Bridging Techniques

### 2.1 What Is Bridging?

Bridging is the art of answering a tough or off-target question, then smoothly transitioning to your key messages. It involves:

1. **Acknowledge** the question (briefly, honestly)
2. **Answer** with a concise response (do not dodge)
3. **Bridge** to your prepared key message using a transition phrase
4. **Deliver** your key message with evidence

### 2.2 Bridge Phrase Library

**Redirecting to your message:**
- "That's a great question, and what I'd really like our audience to know is..."
- "That's an important point, and it connects to something bigger..."
- "I appreciate you raising that. The core issue here is..."
- "That's one way to look at it. What the data actually shows is..."
- "I understand why people think that. The reality is..."

**Expanding the frame:**
- "There's actually a bigger picture here..."
- "Let me put that in context..."
- "What's really interesting about that is..."
- "That's part of a larger trend we're seeing..."
- "To really understand that, you need to know..."

**Pivoting from negatives:**
- "What I can tell you is..."
- "Here's what we're focused on..."
- "The more important question is..."
- "While I can't speak to that specifically, what I know is..."
- "Rather than speculate, let me share what we've actually seen..."

**Returning to your point:**
- "Going back to the key point here..."
- "And that's exactly why..."
- "Which brings me back to..."
- "This is precisely the reason we..."

### 2.3 Bridging Rules

- **Never dodge without acknowledging.** Ignoring the question makes you look evasive.
- **Keep your answer brief** before bridging — one sentence max.
- **Practice the bridge aloud** — it must sound natural, not rehearsed.
- **Do not use the same bridge phrase twice** in one interview.
- **Bridge to your message, not away from the question** — the audience should feel you're adding value, not deflecting.

## 3. Tough Question Handling

### 3.1 Types of Tough Questions

| Type | Example | Strategy |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Hostile / Confrontational** | "Isn't your product just a rip-off of X?" | Stay calm, acknowledge the comparison, bridge to differentiation |
| **Hypothetical** | "What would you do if X happened?" | Redirect to what IS happening, avoid speculation |
| **Leading** | "So you agree that the industry is failing?" | Reframe the premise without accepting it |
| **Multiple-part** | "What about X, and also Y, and how does Z factor in?" | Pick the strongest question, answer it, bridge to your message |
| **Silence / Pause** | [Interviewer waits for you to fill silence] | State your point, stop talking. Comfortable silence is power. |
| **Off-the-record bait** | "Just between us..." | Nothing is ever off the record. Answer as if it will be published. |
| **Gotcha / Fact-check** | "But your competitor says the data shows..." | If true, acknowledge. If false, correct calmly with evidence. |
| **Personal** | "How much do you personally earn from this?" | Set a boundary politely, bridge to business impact |

### 3.2 The ABCD Framework for Tough Questions

- **A — Acknowledge:** "That's a fair question..."
- **B — Bridge:** "And what's important to understand is..."
- **C — Communicate:** Deliver your key message with evidence
- **D — Dangle:** Leave a hook for a follow-up question you WANT to answer

### 3.3 Handling Repeated Tough Questions

Journalists may ask the same difficult question multiple ways. Strategy:

1. Give the same answer consistently (rephrased slightly each time)
2. Do not expand or add new information under pressure
3. Use a bridge to move the conversation forward
4. If pushed three times, it is acceptable to say: "I've addressed that. Let me share something else that's important..."
5. Stay on message — repetition is a feature, not a bug

### 3.4 What to Never Do

- **Never lie** — It will be discovered and the fallout is worse than any truth
- **Never say "no comment"** — It implies guilt. Instead: "I'm not in a position to discuss that, but I can tell you..."
- **Never badmouth competitors** — It damages your reputation while giving them free press
- **Never assume the interview is over** — Until you are physically separated from the interviewer, everything is on the record
- **Never speculate** — "I don't know, but I'll find out" is always better than guessing
- **Never get emotional or defensive** — Calm confidence wins every time

## 4. Soundbite Crafting

### 4.1 What Makes a Great Soundbite

A soundbite is a concise, quotable statement that captures your key message in a memorable way. Journalists need them because they make stories easier to write and more engaging to read.

**Characteristics of effective soundbites:**
- Under 15 words (10 is ideal)
- Self-contained — makes sense without surrounding context
- Memorable — uses vivid language, contrast, or rhythm
- Quotable — sounds natural when attributed to a speaker
- On-message — directly supports one of your key messages

### 4.2 Soundbite Formulas

**Formula 1 — Contrast:**
"It's not about [old way], it's about [new way]."
Example: "It's not about working harder, it's about working on the right things."

**Formula 2 — Rule of Three:**
"We need [X], [Y], and [Z]."
Example: "Every business needs clarity, consistency, and courage."

**Formula 3 — Analogy:**
"[Complex concept] is like [simple, relatable thing]."
Example: "Building a brand is like building a house — you need the foundation before the furniture."

**Formula 4 — Bold Claim:**
"[Surprising/contrarian statement]. Here's why."
Example: "Most marketing advice is wrong. Here's why."

**Formula 5 — The Flip:**
"Everyone thinks [common belief]. The truth is [surprising reality]."
Example: "Everyone thinks you need more content. The truth is you need better distribution."

**Formula 6 — Question Echo:**
Partially repeat the question in your answer — this creates a natural-sounding, self-contained quote.
Interviewer: "Why did you start this company?"
Answer: "I started this company because no one was solving [problem] the right way."

### 4.3 Soundbite Preparation Process

For each key message:
1. Write the full explanation (3-4 sentences)
2. Distill to the single most important sentence
3. Cut it to under 15 words
4. Test it: Would a journalist use this as a pull quote?
5. Practice saying it aloud — does it sound natural?

## 5. Opening and Closing Statements

### 5.1 Opening Statement

Prepare a 15-30 second opening statement in case the interviewer starts with "Tell us about yourself" or "Give us some background":

**Formula:**
"[Who I am] + [What I do/what the brand does] + [Why it matters right now]"

**Rules:**
- Keep it under 30 seconds
- End with a hook that invites the interviewer to go deeper
- Include your primary key message
- Practice until it sounds conversational, not rehearsed

### 5.2 Closing Statement

Prepare a closing statement for when the interviewer asks "Any final thoughts?":

**Formula:**
"[Restate primary message] + [One actionable takeaway for the audience] + [Where to find more / CTA]"

**Rules:**
- This is your last chance to land your primary message — use it
- Keep it under 20 seconds
- End with something the audience can act on
- Include a URL, handle, or next step if appropriate

## 6. Interview Format-Specific Tips

### 6.1 Live TV / Video

- Look at the interviewer (or camera, as directed), not at notes
- Use hand gestures naturally — they convey confidence
- Dress one level up from the audience's expectation
- Energy should be 20% higher than normal conversation
- Short answers — 30-60 seconds max per response

### 6.2 Podcast / Audio

- More conversational, longer-form — okay to go deeper
- Tell stories — podcasts reward narrative
- Avoid filler words ("um," "uh," "like") — they are amplified in audio
- Speak clearly and at a moderate pace
- It is okay to pause and think before answering

### 6.3 Print / Written Interview

- More precise — your exact words may be printed
- Ask the journalist to read back quotes if possible
- Follow up with additional context or data via email
- Assume everything you say will be attributed
- Shorter, more quotable answers work better

### 6.4 Phone Interview

- Stand up — it improves vocal energy
- Have notes in front of you (advantage of phone)
- Eliminate background noise
- Smile while speaking — it comes through in your voice
- Avoid speakerphone — it sounds unprofessional

- [ ] Reviewed outlet's audience and editorial stance
- [ ] 3-5 key messages defined with evidence and soundbites
- [ ] Bridge phrases practiced for each key message
- [ ] 5-7 expected questions with recommended responses drafted
- [ ] 3-5 tough questions with deflection/reframe strategies prepared
- [ ] Topics to avoid identified with redirect strategies
- [ ] Opening statement (15-30 seconds) prepared
- [ ] Closing statement (15-20 seconds) prepared
- [ ] Format-specific preparation complete (TV, podcast, print, etc.)
- [ ] Brand DNA reviewed for voice and messaging alignment
