# Crisis Communication Best Practices

A domain-expert reference for the crisis-response skill. Grounded in Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT), cross-referenced with practitioner frameworks and contemporary social media guidelines.

---

## 1. Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT)

Developed by Dr. W. Timothy Coombs (2007), SCCT is the dominant evidence-based framework for matching crisis response strategies to crisis types. The core premise: the degree of responsibility stakeholders attribute to an organization determines the reputational threat and dictates the appropriate response strategy.

### 1.1 Reputational Threat Model

Reputational threat is determined by three factors:

1. **Initial crisis responsibility** — How much stakeholders blame the organization for the crisis event itself.
2. **Crisis history** — Whether the organization has faced similar crises before. Repeat incidents intensify skepticism and elevate reputational threat regardless of actual fault.
3. **Prior relational reputation** — How stakeholders viewed the organization before the crisis. A strong prior reputation provides a buffer; a weak one amplifies damage.

**Key rule:** If crisis history is negative OR prior reputation is unfavorable, escalate the response strategy by one level (e.g., from diminish to rebuild).

---

## 2. Crisis Typology

SCCT classifies crises into three clusters based on attributed responsibility. Each cluster carries a different level of reputational threat and demands a different response posture.

### 2.1 Victim Cluster (Minimal Attribution)

The organization is perceived as a victim of the crisis, not its cause.

| Crisis Type | Example |
|:---|:---|
| Natural disaster | Office destroyed by earthquake; operations disrupted |
| Rumors / false information | Fabricated story about product safety goes viral |
| Workplace violence | Employee targeted by external threat |
| Product tampering (external) | Third party contaminates product after distribution |
| Cyberattack (sophisticated) | State-sponsored hack breaches customer data despite strong security |

**Reputational threat:** Low
**Default posture:** Instructing information + adjusting information (see Section 3)

### 2.2 Accidental Cluster (Low-to-Moderate Attribution)

The crisis was unintentional. Stakeholders assign some blame but recognize the lack of deliberate wrongdoing.

| Crisis Type | Example |
|:---|:---|
| Technical-error accident | Software bug causes service outage |
| Technical-error product harm | Manufacturing defect leads to product recall |
| Equipment failure | Hardware failure causes data loss |
| Stakeholder challenge | Activist group targets organization over policy |

**Reputational threat:** Moderate
**Default posture:** Diminish strategies + bolstering

### 2.3 Preventable Cluster (Strong Attribution)

The organization knowingly placed people at risk or violated norms. Stakeholders assign maximum blame.

| Crisis Type | Example |
|:---|:---|
| Human-error accident | Employee negligence causes injury |
| Human-error product harm | Known defect shipped to save cost |
| Organizational misdeed (no injuries) | Deceptive marketing exposed |
| Organizational misdeed (injuries) | Safety violations lead to harm |
| Organizational misdeed (management) | Executive fraud, cover-up, or misconduct |

**Reputational threat:** Severe
**Default posture:** Rebuild strategies (full accountability)

## 3. Response Strategies

SCCT defines four primary strategy families, each containing specific tactics. Organizations should layer strategies — primary + secondary — based on crisis type and intensifying factors.

### 3.1 Deny Strategies

**When to use:** Only when the organization is genuinely not responsible and can prove it. Misapplied denial destroys credibility.

| Tactic | Description | Risk |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Attack the accuser** | Confront the party making the claim | High — appears aggressive; backfires if claim has merit |
| **Denial** | Assert the crisis does not exist or the organization is not involved | Medium — effective only with proof |
| **Scapegoating** | Blame an external party | High — appears evasive unless clearly warranted |

### 3.2 Diminish Strategies

**When to use:** Accidental cluster crises. Reduce perceived severity or organizational control over the event.

| Tactic | Description | Risk |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Excuse** | Minimize organizational responsibility; emphasize lack of intent or control | Low — if credible |
| **Justification** | Acknowledge crisis but minimize perceived damage ("no one was harmed") | Medium — can appear dismissive |

### 3.3 Rebuild Strategies

**When to use:** Preventable cluster crises, or any crisis escalated by negative history or poor prior reputation.

| Tactic | Description | Risk |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Compensation** | Offer tangible remediation to victims (refunds, services, support) | Low — demonstrates accountability |
| **Full apology** | Accept responsibility, express regret, request forgiveness, commit to change | Low — most effective for preventable crises |

### 3.4 Bolster Strategies (Secondary — Always Paired)

**When to use:** As a supplement to any primary strategy. Never use alone.

| Tactic | Description |
|:---|:---|
| **Reminding** | Highlight past good works and positive track record |
| **Ingratiation** | Praise and thank stakeholders |
| **Victimage** | Remind stakeholders the organization is also suffering |

### 3.5 Strategy Matching Quick Reference

| Crisis Cluster | Primary Strategy | Secondary Strategy | Escalation Trigger |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Victim | Deny (if false) or Instructing/Adjusting info | Bolster (victimage) | History of similar crises |
| Accidental | Diminish (excuse/justification) | Bolster (reminding) | Poor prior reputation |
| Preventable | Rebuild (apology + compensation) | Bolster (reminding, ingratiation) | Always treat as high-threat |

**Escalation rule:** When intensifying factors are present, move one level up. Accidental with bad history becomes Rebuild. Victim with bad history becomes Diminish.

## 4. Stakeholder Mapping Methodology

Effective crisis response requires segmented communication. Not every audience receives the same message, channel, or timing.

### 4.1 Stakeholder Categories

| Stakeholder | Priority | Channel | Timing | Key Concern |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Directly affected parties | Highest | Direct contact (email, phone, in-person) | First | Safety, remediation, support |
| Employees / team | Very high | Internal channels, town halls | Before public statement | Transparency, job security, talking points |
| Customers / audience | High | Email, social media, website | Within first hour | Impact on them, next steps |
| Media / press | High | Press release, spokesperson | Coordinated with public statement | Facts, accountability, quotes |
| Partners / collaborators | Medium | Direct outreach | Within first 2 hours | Impact on shared work, alignment |
| Industry / peers | Medium | Public channels | As part of public response | Precedent, standards |
| Regulators / legal | Varies | Formal communication | Per legal counsel | Compliance, cooperation |
| General public | Standard | Social media, blog, press | Public statement | Narrative, values alignment |

### 4.2 Mapping Process

1. **List** all groups who are or could be affected.
2. **Rank** by urgency of impact (who is harmed first?).
3. **Assess** each group's information needs (what do they need to know?).
4. **Assign** channel and spokesperson for each group.
5. **Sequence** communications so internal stakeholders hear before external ones.

## 5. When Silence Is the Right Move

Silence is rarely the correct default. 53% of consumers assume brands are hiding something if unresponsive during a crisis. However, strategic silence has specific valid applications.

### 5.1 Decision Criteria for Non-Response

Choose silence (or minimal response) ONLY when ALL of the following are true:

- [ ] The issue has minimal reach (under 100 mentions, no media pickup)
- [ ] Engagement would amplify the crisis to a larger audience (Streisand Effect risk)
- [ ] The accusation is clearly frivolous and will die naturally
- [ ] No stakeholders are directly harmed or at risk
- [ ] The source lacks credibility and is not gaining traction
- [ ] Legal counsel recommends non-engagement

### 5.2 When Silence Is Dangerous

Never stay silent when:

- People are harmed or at risk
- Media has picked up the story
- The issue is trending on any platform
- Employees or partners are asking questions
- Misinformation is spreading unchallenged
- There is a factual claim that can be corrected

### 5.3 The Middle Path: Monitoring Hold

When uncertain, adopt a "monitoring hold" — do not publish a response, but:

1. Prepare a holding statement ready for instant deployment
2. Monitor mentions every 30 minutes for trajectory
3. Set a trigger threshold (e.g., 500 mentions, media inquiry, trending hashtag)
4. If threshold is hit, deploy the holding statement immediately

## 6. Common Mistakes by Crisis Type

### 6.1 Victim Cluster Mistakes

- Over-apologizing when the organization bears no fault (signals guilt)
- Failing to correct misinformation quickly enough
- Not providing instructing information (what should affected parties do?)
- Letting the narrative be written by others due to slow response

### 6.2 Accidental Cluster Mistakes

- Denying involvement when evidence suggests otherwise
- Using corporate jargon instead of plain human language
- Providing justification that minimizes real harm people experienced
- Inconsistent messaging across channels and spokespeople
- Failing to show corrective action — stakeholders need to see change, not just words

### 6.3 Preventable Cluster Mistakes

- Initial denial followed by forced admission (worst possible pattern)
- "Non-apology apologies" ("We're sorry you feel that way")
- Deflecting blame onto employees while protecting leadership
- Announcing reforms without follow-through or timeline
- Treating it as a PR problem rather than an operational one
- Engaging in social media arguments with critics
- Releasing information in drips rather than full transparency at once

### 6.4 Universal Mistakes (All Types)

- Delayed response (every hour of silence costs exponentially more credibility)
- Internal teams learning about the crisis from external sources
- Posting scheduled/promotional content during an active crisis
- Inconsistent statements across spokespeople
- Failing to update stakeholders as the situation evolves
- No post-crisis review or structural changes
- Generic, template-feeling responses that lack specificity

## 7. Timeline Templates

### 7.1 The First 24 Hours (Golden Window)

| Time | Action |
|:---|:---|
| **0-30 min** | Activate crisis team. Verify facts. Pull all scheduled content. |
| **30-60 min** | Draft holding statement. Brief internal team. Assess severity and trajectory. |
| **1-2 hours** | Deploy holding statement publicly. Begin stakeholder-specific outreach. Establish monitoring cadence. |
| **2-4 hours** | Issue detailed public response (if facts are confirmed). Designate single spokesperson. Begin media engagement if needed. |
| **4-8 hours** | Publish FAQ or detailed update. Monitor sentiment and adjust messaging. Respond to direct inquiries. |
| **8-12 hours** | Second public update (if situation is evolving). Internal team check-in. Assess whether trajectory is improving. |
| **12-24 hours** | Comprehensive update with actions taken. Review and adjust strategy based on sentiment data. Plan next 48 hours. |

### 7.2 Hours 24-48 (Sustained Response)

| Time | Action |
|:---|:---|
| **24-30 hours** | Morning update (if warranted). Review overnight sentiment and media coverage. |
| **30-36 hours** | Proactive positive content if appropriate. Continue direct stakeholder outreach. |
| **36-48 hours** | Assess whether crisis is stabilizing, growing, or shifting. Adjust response strategy if needed. Begin planning post-crisis actions. |

### 7.3 Hours 48-72 (Transition to Recovery)

| Time | Action |
|:---|:---|
| **48-60 hours** | Shift from reactive to proactive messaging. Announce concrete corrective actions with timelines. |
| **60-72 hours** | Begin post-crisis narrative: lessons learned, structural changes, commitments. Reduce monitoring frequency to 2-4 times daily. Plan long-term reputation rebuilding. |

### 7.4 Post-Crisis (Week 1-4)

| Period | Action |
|:---|:---|
| **Week 1** | Conduct internal post-mortem. Document what worked and failed. |
| **Week 2** | Implement announced corrective actions. Publish progress update if public commitments were made. |
| **Week 3-4** | Resume normal content cadence. Monitor for resurgence. Begin proactive positive content. Update crisis playbook based on lessons learned. |

## 8. Social Media Crisis-Specific Guidelines

Social media crises move faster, spread wider, and require platform-specific handling. These guidelines supplement the general framework above.

### 8.1 Speed Expectations

- **First 30 minutes** are the confusion window — clarity and coordination win trust here.
- Brands responding within 2 hours see 61% better sentiment recovery than delayed responders.
- 72% of CCOs/CMOs report stakeholders expect immediate action during a crisis.

### 8.2 Platform-Specific Guidance

| Platform | Best For | Avoid |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **X (Twitter)** | Quick acknowledgments, thread updates, real-time corrections | Long defensive threads, arguing with users |
| **LinkedIn** | Leadership statements, professional context, industry framing | Casual tone, ignoring comments |
| **Instagram** | Visual statements, Stories for updates | Business-as-usual posts during crisis |
| **TikTok** | Authentic, human responses (if audience is there) | Overly corporate language, forced video responses |
| **Facebook** | Detailed statements, community engagement | Disabling comments (intensifies backlash) |

### 8.3 Social Media Do's

- Pause all scheduled posts immediately
- Acknowledge the issue even before you have full information
- Use a human, empathetic tone — not corporate boilerplate
- Provide specific timelines for updates ("We will share more by 3 PM ET")
- Move hostile conversations to private channels when appropriate
- Keep responses consistent across all platforms
- Monitor reshare velocity — unusual spikes warrant escalation

### 8.4 Social Media Don'ts

- Never disable or lock comments (signals hiding)
- Never argue with individuals publicly
- Never post promotional content during an active crisis
- Never use AI-generated responses without human review and personalization
- Never assume a crisis will "blow over" without response
- Never delete posts about the crisis (screenshots exist forever)
- Never use humor or deflection in a serious crisis

### 8.5 Holding Statement Template (Social Media)

> We've just learned of [brief description of event]. We take this seriously and are reviewing all aspects thoroughly. We will share a detailed update within [specific timeframe]. If you are directly affected, please [specific instruction — e.g., "DM us" or "contact support at..."]. Thank you for your patience.

### 8.6 Escalation Triggers (Move from Social to Full Crisis Protocol)

- Sudden spike in negative mentions or reshares
- Mainstream media picks up the story
- Hashtag related to the crisis begins trending
- Public figure or influencer amplifies the issue
- Coordinated calls for boycott or cancellation
- Misinformation spreading faster than corrections
- Employees posting internally or externally about the issue

## 9. The 10Cs of Crisis Communication

A complementary practitioner framework developed by Edward Segal that provides operational principles for executing crisis communications.

1. **Control** — Control the narrative. Respond first; do not let others define the story.
2. **Consult** — Engage legal, HR, leadership, and relevant advisors before communicating.
3. **Concern** — Express genuine empathy for those affected. Focus on them, not the organization.
4. **Clear** — Use plain language. No jargon, no legalese, no AI-sounding boilerplate.
5. **Concise** — Limit key messages to 3-4 major points. Keep statements under 40 seconds when spoken.
6. **Current** — Share the most recent verified information. Cite sources and timestamps.
7. **Correct** — Double-check every fact before publishing. Issue corrections immediately if errors surface.
8. **Conditions** — Frame information with appropriate caveats when the situation remains unclear.
9. **Consistency** — Deliver identical core messages across all audiences and channels.
10. **Confidence** — Communicate with authority and clarity that demonstrates organizational control.

## 10. Personal Brand Considerations

For RennOS and individual/personal brands, crisis response carries additional nuances:

- **Authenticity over polish** — Audiences of personal brands expect the person, not a PR team. Overly polished responses feel inauthentic.
- **Speed matters even more** — Personal brand audiences expect direct, fast communication from the individual.
- **The person IS the brand** — You cannot separate the crisis from the person. Every response is personal.
- **Smaller audience, deeper relationships** — Each stakeholder matters more. Direct outreach has outsized impact.
- **Recovery is faster but scars last** — Personal brands can recover quickly with genuine accountability, but the internet's memory is long.
- **Brand DNA alignment is critical** — Every crisis response must reflect the core values in `data/brand/brand-dna.md`. Deviation during crisis destroys brand coherence.
