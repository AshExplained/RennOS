# Media Pitch Best Practices Reference

> Compiled from industry research, journalist surveys, and PR practitioner data.
> Last updated: 2026-03-24

---

## 1. Pitch Anatomy

A media pitch has six core components. Each must earn its place — journalists decide within seconds whether to keep reading or delete.

### 1.1 Subject Line

The gatekeeper. If it fails, nothing else matters.

**Benchmarks:**
- **Length:** 36-60 characters / 6-10 words optimal
- Subject lines under 50 characters see the highest open rates; open rates drop steeply beyond 50 characters
- Mobile screens display only 33-43 characters, so front-load the value
- Median word count among top-performing pitches (90%+ open rate): 10 words

**What works:**
- Direct, specific language referencing the pitch's value proposition
- Data-driven hooks: "[Study]", "Survey:", "New research:" as prefixes
- Multimedia references ("Video shows...", "Watch...") drive significantly higher engagement
- Trend connections that signal relevance to the journalist's beat
- The journalist's area of coverage or a recent article topic woven in

**What fails:**
- Witty or tabloid-style headlines (triggers clickbait radar)
- Excessive capitals, punctuation, or salesy language (activates spam filters)
- Vague subject lines lacking clear purpose
- Generic personalization without substantive customization
- Including "PR" — slight open rate bump does not translate to higher click rates

**Examples of strong subject lines:**
- "New MIT data on remote work productivity — expert available"
- "Exclusive: [Company] launches [product] addressing [trend]"
- "[Journalist's beat topic]: unreported angle from [credible source]"

### 1.2 Opening Hook

You have 1-2 sentences to justify the journalist's attention. The hook must answer: "Why should I care, and why now?"

**Best practices:**
- Lead with the newsworthy element, not your client or brand
- Reference a recent article the journalist wrote and connect it to your story
- Open with a striking data point, trend, or counterintuitive insight
- Avoid "I hope this email finds you well" and other filler openers
- Never start with "I'm reaching out on behalf of..." — that buries the story

**Hook formulas that work:**
- **Data hook:** "A new study of 5,000 [audience] found that [surprising stat]..."
- **Trend hook:** "Following your recent piece on [topic], there's an unreported angle..."
- **Timeliness hook:** "With [event/deadline] approaching, [expert] has new findings on..."
- **Contrast hook:** "While most coverage of [topic] focuses on X, [source] discovered Y..."

### 1.3 Story Angle

The body of the pitch. This is where you make the case for why this is a story, not just an announcement.

**Structure:**
- What is the story? (1-2 sentences)
- Why is it newsworthy? (timeliness, impact, novelty, conflict, human interest)
- What evidence supports it? (data, case studies, expert perspective)
- Why does it matter to this journalist's audience specifically?

**Angle development tips:**
- Transform a single piece of news into multiple angles for different outlets
- Think like an editor: "Would I assign a reporter to cover this?"
- Ground the angle in what's happening in the broader industry/culture
- Provide context the journalist can't easily Google themselves
- Offer original data, exclusive access, or a fresh expert perspective

### 1.4 Why This Person

The personalization section that proves this is not a mass blast.

**Must include:**
- Why you chose this specific journalist (reference their beat, recent work, or stated interests)
- Why this story fits their outlet's audience
- What makes your source uniquely qualified to speak on this topic

**Credibility signals:**
- Relevant credentials, experience, or track record
- Previous media appearances or publications
- Unique access, data, or perspective others lack
- Social proof (awards, recognition, follower counts only if relevant)

### 1.5 The Ask

Be explicit about what you want. Journalists appreciate directness.

**Types of asks:**
- Interview with [named expert] — specify availability windows
- Exclusive access to [data/event/launch]
- Guest byline or contributed article on [specific topic]
- Expert quote for an upcoming story on [topic]
- Product review or demo — offer to ship/schedule
- Speaking opportunity or panel inclusion

**Ask best practices:**
- One clear ask per pitch (don't give a menu of options)
- Make it easy to say yes — include availability, timezone, format preferences
- Frame the ask in terms of value to the journalist, not to you
- "Available for a 15-minute call this week" is better than "let me know if interested"

### 1.6 Logistics

The practical details that remove friction from saying yes.

**Include:**
- Spokesperson availability (specific dates/times with timezone)
- Assets available: headshots, product images, data visualizations, b-roll
- Embargo or exclusivity terms (if applicable)
- Timeline or deadline (launch date, event date, publication date)
- Contact information (direct email, phone, preferred channel)
- Links to a digital press kit or media page if available

**Note:** 87% of reporters use multimedia assets included with pitches (Cision 2024 State of the Media Report). Having ready assets is a competitive advantage.

## 2. Journalist Preferences

### 2.1 What Editors Want

Based on Cision's 2024 State of the Media Report (3,000+ global media professionals surveyed):

- **Relevance is king:** Nearly 3 in 4 journalists say the #1 reason they reject pitches is lack of relevance to their beat or audience
- **72%** prefer receiving press releases alongside pitches
- **87%** use multimedia assets (images, data visualizations, videos) provided with pitches
- **64%** want PR professionals to follow up only once
- **Just 2%** wish to receive telephone pitches
- Journalists most value: direct pitches, story exclusives, original research, and access to industry experts

**What makes a pitch stand out:**
- Genuine personalization showing you read their recent work
- A clear, unique story angle (not a thinly veiled product announcement)
- Original data or research they can cite
- Expert sources available for interview on tight timelines
- Multimedia assets ready to use

### 2.2 What Annoys Journalists

- **Mass blasts** with no personalization — the single biggest complaint
- **Irrelevant pitches** outside their beat (86% immediately reject these)
- **AI-generated pitches** — seasoned journalists spot them in the first paragraph and reject immediately
- **Multiple rapid follow-ups** resembling spam
- **Phone calls** the day after sending an email (feels like stalking)
- **Pitches disguised as stories** — transparent promotional content without news value
- **No research on the outlet** — pitching a print feature to a podcast-only outlet
- **Overly long pitches** that bury the news under background fluff
- **Misleading subject lines** that promise more than the pitch delivers
- **"Just checking in"** follow-ups with no new information

### 2.3 The 2026 Landscape

- Journalist inboxes receive 500+ emails weekly; yours must cut through noise
- AI-generated pitch volume has increased, making authentic personalization more valuable than ever
- Staffing shortages mean journalists cover broader beats — research their current scope, not their title
- 42% of journalists cite maintaining credibility and combating fake news as their top challenge
- Fewer than half of journalists regularly use AI despite industry hype — don't assume tech-forward

## 3. Length Benchmarks

### 3.1 Subject Line

| Metric | Benchmark | Source |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Character count | 36-60 characters optimal | Prowly, Campaign Monitor |
| Word count | 6-10 words | Prowly, Muck Rack |
| Mobile display limit | 33-43 characters | EmailToolTester |
| Top-performing median | 10 words | Muck Rack (5M pitch analysis) |

### 3.2 Pitch Body

| Metric | Benchmark | Source |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Ideal word count | 50-200 words | Influize, PRLab, Cision |
| Highest response rate | 50-150 words | Influize |
| Acceptable range | Up to 250 words for newsjacking | Muck Rack |
| Paragraph count | 3-5 short paragraphs | PRLab |
| Sentence count | 5-8 sentences total | UPRAISE PR |

### 3.3 Rules of Thumb

- **Under 200 words** is the most commonly cited ideal length
- **50-150 words** see the highest response rates in studies
- If you need more than 200 words, the story angle likely isn't tight enough
- How well you've targeted the journalist matters more than word count — a perfectly targeted 250-word pitch outperforms a generic 100-word pitch
- Follow-up emails should be even shorter than the initial pitch
- Every sentence must earn its place; if it doesn't advance the story or the ask, cut it

## 4. Follow-Up Etiquette

### 4.1 Timing

| Follow-Up | Timing | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| First follow-up | 3-5 business days after initial pitch | Standard for routine pitches |
| Time-sensitive stories | 24 hours | Only when genuine urgency exists |
| Second follow-up | Day 8-10 | Only if first follow-up got no response |
| Beyond second follow-up | Don't | Silence is the answer; move on |

**Best time of day:** Tuesday through Thursday, 8:00 AM - 12:00 PM in the journalist's timezone. This aligns with typical newsroom evaluation windows.

### 4.2 Frequency

- **62% of reporters** advise following up only once
- **8%** encourage multiple follow-ups
- Cap at **2 follow-ups maximum** (3 total contacts including the original pitch)
- A third message should explicitly state it's your final outreach while leaving the door open
- Additional attempts beyond this become counterproductive and damage the relationship

### 4.3 Channels

**Channel hierarchy (in order of preference):**

1. **Email** — Always the first and primary channel. Journalists prefer it because they can respond on their schedule and reference the original pitch
2. **Social media (Twitter/X, LinkedIn)** — For relationship-building only, not active pitch follow-ups. Engage with their content separately from pitching
3. **Phone** — Only for established contacts or genuine breaking news. Never call the day after sending an email
4. **In-person** — Conferences and events only, and keep it brief

### 4.4 Follow-Up Content Rules

**Do:**
- Add new information or a fresh angle not in the original pitch
- Reference a recent development that increases the story's timeliness
- Keep it shorter than the original pitch
- Provide specific resources: stats, interview availability, background materials
- Include a clear, low-friction call to action
- Explicitly say "Available to talk at any time that works for you"

**Don't:**
- Simply resend the original pitch with "just checking in"
- Copy-paste the original and add "did you see my last email?"
- Add pressure ("this opportunity won't last")
- Follow up on multiple channels simultaneously
- Take a non-response personally — most journalists simply can't respond to every pitch

## 5. Pitch Types

### 5.1 Exclusive Pitch

**What it is:** Offering the story to one journalist only. They alone will break the news.

**When to use:**
- Major announcements you want covered in-depth by a specific top-tier outlet
- Stories that need narrative treatment (profiles, features, investigations)
- When you want to build a deeper relationship with a key journalist
- High-impact news where quality of coverage matters more than quantity

**How it works:**
- Approach one journalist with a clear "exclusive" label
- Agree on a publication date and time
- Do not pitch anyone else until after publication (or if the exclusive is declined)
- 76% of journalists are more likely to cover a story if offered an exclusive (Muck Rack 2023 State of Journalism)

**Risks:**
- If the journalist passes, you've lost time
- If the story doesn't run prominently, you get only one placement
- Breaking an exclusive agreement will permanently damage that relationship

### 5.2 Embargo Pitch

**What it is:** Sharing news with multiple journalists before public announcement, under agreement that they won't publish until a specified date and time.

**When to use:**
- Product launches, funding rounds, partnerships, or major announcements
- When you want broad, simultaneous coverage on announcement day
- When journalists need time to prepare in-depth pieces (technical products, complex stories)
- When timing of coverage matters (coordinating with events, earnings, campaigns)

**How it works:**
- Pitch to multiple journalists with a clearly stated embargo date/time
- Each journalist independently decides whether to cover the story
- All coverage goes live simultaneously at the embargo lift
- Journalists who accept the embargo are honor-bound not to publish early

**Risks:**
- The more journalists contacted, the higher the risk of an embargo break
- Not legally binding — relies entirely on trust and professional norms
- An embargo break can sour relationships with all other journalists who honored it
- Some journalists refuse embargoes on principle

**Best practices:**
- Only offer embargoes to journalists you trust or who have a track record of honoring them
- State the embargo clearly in the subject line and first line of the pitch
- Include the exact date, time, and timezone for the embargo lift
- Provide enough lead time for journalists to prepare (3-7 days minimum)

### 5.3 General Pitch

**What it is:** A standard pitch sent to multiple journalists without exclusivity or timing restrictions.

**When to use:**
- Evergreen stories, trend pieces, thought leadership
- When maximizing impressions matters more than depth
- Stories with broad appeal across multiple outlets and beats
- Expert commentary offers, seasonal angles, reactive newsjacking

**How it works:**
- Pitch individually (never BCC blast) to targeted journalists
- No embargo or exclusivity commitments
- Journalists can publish whenever they choose
- Multiple outlets may cover the same angle

**Best practices:**
- Personalize each email even though the core pitch is the same
- Tailor the angle slightly for each outlet's audience
- Don't send to competing journalists at the same outlet
- Track who you've pitched to avoid duplicates

### 5.4 Choosing the Right Type

| Factor | Exclusive | Embargo | General |
|--------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Coverage depth | Deep, narrative | Moderate, news-style | Variable |
| Coverage breadth | Single outlet | Multiple, simultaneous | Multiple, staggered |
| Risk level | Medium (single point of failure) | Medium (embargo breaks) | Low |
| Relationship impact | High (builds trust) | Moderate | Low |
| Best for | Features, profiles | Launches, announcements | Thought leadership, trends |
| Timeline control | Negotiated | Strict | None |
