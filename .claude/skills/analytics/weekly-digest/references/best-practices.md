## 1. Digest Structure

### Recommended Section Order

A well-structured weekly digest follows a top-down information hierarchy — the reader should
get the most important information first, with details available for those who want to dig deeper.

```
1. Executive Summary (2-3 sentences)
2. Key Metrics / KPI Snapshot (5-7 metrics max)
3. Wins & Highlights
4. Concerns & Risks
5. Content Performance (top 3-5 pieces)
6. Growth Snapshot (platform-by-platform)
7. Active Projects / Status Updates
8. Action Items for Next Week
```

### Structural Principles

- **Consistent format every week** — Same sections, same order, every time. This allows
  week-over-week comparison and builds reader habits.
- **Progressive disclosure** — Summary first, then metrics, then details, then actions.
  If the reader stops after the summary, they still got value.
- **Logical flow** — Move from "what happened" (metrics, wins, concerns) to "what's next"
  (projects, action items).
- **Clear section headers** — Use bold, consistent headings so the reader can jump to
  any section instantly.

### Section Length Guidelines

| Section | Target Length | Notes |
|---------|--------------|-------|
| Executive Summary | 2-3 sentences | The "if you read nothing else" section |
| Key Metrics | 5-7 metrics | Table format with comparisons |
| Wins | 3-5 bullet points | Specific, quantified achievements |
| Concerns | 2-4 bullet points | Honest, solution-oriented |
| Content Performance | Top 3 pieces | With specific metrics per piece |
| Growth Snapshot | 1 line per platform | Net change + total |
| Active Projects | 1-2 lines per project | Status + next milestone |
| Action Items | 3-7 items | Specific, assignable, time-bound |

**Total digest should be readable in under 3 minutes.**

## 2. Executive Summary Writing

### Purpose

The executive summary is the single most important section. If the reader only reads
this paragraph, they should walk away with a clear picture of the week.

### Formula

```
[Overall week assessment] + [Biggest win with number] + [Biggest concern or focus area]
```

### Good Examples

- "Strong week driven by a 23% spike in LinkedIn engagement from the AI trends post.
  Newsletter subscribers crossed 5,000. Email open rates dipped to 18% — investigating
  subject line performance."

- "Steady week with consistent content output. Website traffic flat at 12K visits.
  The security audit revealed 3 medium-severity issues that need attention before Q2."

- "Below-target week: two planned posts were delayed due to design bottlenecks.
  However, the podcast episode hit 2,400 downloads (best ever). Prioritizing content
  pipeline fix for next week."

### Bad Examples (and Why)

- "This week was good." — No specifics, no value.
- "We posted 3 times on LinkedIn, 2 times on Twitter, sent 1 newsletter..." — Activity
  list, not a summary. Nobody cares about activity without outcomes.
- "Metrics are attached below." — Defeats the purpose of a summary.

### Writing Tips

1. **Lead with impact, not activity** — "Revenue increased 12%" not "We ran 3 campaigns"
2. **Quantify everything** — Numbers make summaries credible and scannable
3. **Use plain language** — No jargon, no acronyms without context
4. **Be honest about bad weeks** — Credibility comes from balanced reporting
5. **Keep it to 2-3 sentences** — Force yourself to prioritize
6. **Tell a story** — Frame data as narrative showing progress toward goals

## 3. Metric Selection

### Principles for Choosing Weekly Metrics

1. **Align with goals** — Every metric should connect to a business objective.
   If it doesn't ladder up to a goal, it's noise.
2. **Actionable over vanity** — Choose metrics you can actually influence.
   Follower count is vanity; engagement rate is actionable.
3. **Fewer is better** — 5-7 core KPIs maximum. Too many metrics dilute focus.
4. **Always show comparison** — A number without context is meaningless. Show
   week-over-week change, target comparison, or trend direction.
5. **Leading + lagging mix** — Include both predictive indicators (pipeline,
   engagement) and outcome indicators (revenue, conversions).

### Recommended KPI Categories for Personal Brand

#### Audience Growth
| Metric | Why It Matters | Comparison |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| Total followers (per platform) | Overall reach | vs. last week (net change) |
| Newsletter subscribers | Owned audience size | vs. last week + growth rate |
| Website unique visitors | Content discovery | vs. last week + vs. monthly avg |

#### Engagement Quality
| Metric | Why It Matters | Comparison |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| Engagement rate | Content resonance | vs. last week + vs. platform avg |
| Comments / replies | Conversation depth | vs. last week |
| Email open rate | Subject line + audience health | vs. last week + vs. industry avg |
| Email click rate | Content value delivery | vs. last week |

#### Content Performance
| Metric | Why It Matters | Comparison |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| Top post impressions | Reach of best content | Identify patterns |
| Average post performance | Baseline quality | vs. last week |
| Content pieces published | Output consistency | vs. target |

#### Business Impact
| Metric | Why It Matters | Comparison |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| Inbound leads / inquiries | Business development | vs. last week |
| Website conversions | Funnel effectiveness | vs. last week |
| Revenue (if applicable) | Bottom line | vs. target |

### Weekly vs Monthly Metric Depth

- **Weekly**: Focus on 5-7 top-line KPIs that move fast enough to track weekly.
  Don't report on metrics that barely change week to week (e.g., domain authority,
  SEO rankings for stable keywords).
- **Monthly**: Go deeper — add trend analysis, cohort breakdowns, funnel analysis,
  competitive benchmarks, and strategic commentary.

### Metrics to Avoid in Weekly Reports

- **Metrics that don't change weekly** — Domain authority, long-tail SEO rankings,
  brand sentiment scores (report these monthly or quarterly)
- **Raw totals without context** — "500 impressions" means nothing without comparison
- **Platform-internal vanity metrics** — "Profile views" on most platforms are noise
- **Metrics you can't act on** — If you can't do anything about it, don't track it weekly

## 4. Weekly vs Monthly Reports

### Key Differences

| Dimension | Weekly Report | Monthly Report |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| **Purpose** | Pulse check, course correction | Trend analysis, strategic review |
| **Length** | 1-2 pages, 3-minute read | 3-5 pages, 10-minute read |
| **Metrics** | 5-7 core KPIs | 15-20 metrics with deep dives |
| **Comparison** | Week-over-week | Month-over-month + quarterly trend |
| **Tone** | Operational, tactical | Strategic, analytical |
| **Action items** | Immediate (this week) | Strategic (this month/quarter) |
| **Audience** | Day-to-day decision makers | Strategic leadership |
| **Visuals** | Simple tables, sparklines | Charts, trend graphs, funnels |
| **Content depth** | Top 3 performers | Full content audit, theme analysis |
| **Best for** | Fast-moving campaigns, paid media, social | SEO, brand building, long-term growth |

### When Weekly is Essential

- During campaign launches or promotions
- When testing new content strategies
- During rapid growth or decline phases
- When managing active paid media budgets
- When onboarding to a new platform

### When Monthly is Sufficient

- Stable, mature channels with predictable performance
- SEO-focused strategies (rankings move slowly)
- Brand awareness campaigns with long attribution windows
- When the audience prefers less frequent updates

### Hybrid Approach (Recommended)

- **Weekly**: Short pulse digest focusing on KPIs and immediate actions
- **Monthly**: Deep-dive report with trend analysis, strategic insights, and
  recommendations for the upcoming month
- **Quarterly**: Full strategic review with goal assessment and planning

## 5. What to Include vs Exclude

### Always Include

- **Context for every number** — "Email open rate: 22% (up from 19% last week)"
  not just "Email open rate: 22%"
- **Both wins and concerns** — Balanced reporting builds trust and enables
  better decision-making
- **Specific evidence** — Link to the actual post, screenshot the metric,
  name the specific content piece
- **Action items with owners** — "Investigate email subject line performance"
  is better than "fix email"
- **Comparison to targets** — How does actual compare to what was planned?
- **Cross-channel insights** — When a LinkedIn post drove newsletter signups,
  note the connection
- **Anomalies and explanations** — Spikes or drops with hypotheses for why

### Never Include

- **Raw data dumps** — Unprocessed data belongs in dashboards, not digests
- **Internal process details** — "We had 3 meetings about the content calendar"
  is not useful
- **Unexplained metrics** — If you can't explain why it matters, leave it out
- **Redundant information** — Don't repeat the same data point in multiple sections
- **Speculation without basis** — "I think engagement will increase" without evidence
- **Competitor data without action** — Competitor intel is only useful if it informs
  a specific decision
- **Metrics from inactive channels** — If a platform hasn't been active, skip it

### Conditional Include

- **Industry benchmarks** — Include when the audience needs context for whether
  performance is good or bad (especially in early months)
- **Qualitative feedback** — Include notable comments, DMs, or testimonials when
  they reveal audience sentiment
- **Platform algorithm changes** — Include when they explain metric shifts
- **Seasonal factors** — Note holidays, events, or trends that affect performance

## 6. Formatting and Scannability

### Design Principles

1. **Scannable in 30 seconds** — A reader skimming should get the key takeaways
   from headers, bold text, and the summary alone
2. **Tables for comparisons** — KPIs belong in tables, not paragraphs
3. **Bullet points for lists** — Never bury action items or wins in paragraphs
4. **Bold key numbers** — Draw the eye to the metrics that matter
5. **Consistent formatting** — Same structure, same order, every single week
6. **White space** — Dense text walls kill readability

### Markdown Formatting Guide

```markdown
## Executive Summary
One strong paragraph here.

## Key Metrics
| Metric | This Week | Last Week | Change |
|--------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Metric 1 | **500** | 450 | +11% |

## Wins
- **Win 1**: Description with specific number
- **Win 2**: Description with specific number

## Concerns
- **Concern 1**: Description + proposed action

## Action Items
- [ ] Action 1 — Owner — Due date
- [ ] Action 2 — Owner — Due date
```

### Comparison Formatting

Always show direction with metrics:
- **Up**: +15% or arrow up indicator
- **Down**: -8% or arrow down indicator
- **Flat**: ~ or "unchanged"
- **New**: "NEW" for first-time metrics

## 7. Actionable Insights

### The "So What?" Test

Every metric, win, or concern should pass the "so what?" test:
- **Metric**: "Engagement rate dropped 15%"
- **So what?**: "This suggests our content topics this week didn't resonate.
  The two posts about X underperformed while the post about Y overperformed."
- **Now what?**: "Next week, publish 2 more posts in the Y category and A/B test
  X-category headlines."

### Writing Actionable Insights

```
[Observation] + [Hypothesis for why] + [Recommended action]
```

**Good**: "Newsletter open rate dropped to 18% from 24%. The subject lines this
week were longer (avg 62 chars vs usual 45). Next week, test shorter subject
lines and track correlation."

**Bad**: "Newsletter open rate was 18%." (No context, no action)

### Action Item Format

Each action item should be:
- **Specific** — What exactly needs to happen?
- **Assignable** — Who is responsible?
- **Time-bound** — When should it be done?
- **Measurable** — How will we know it's done?

Example: "Test 3 subject line variations under 50 characters for Wednesday's
newsletter — Content team — by Tuesday EOD"

## 8. Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Activity Reports Disguised as Digests
Listing everything that was done ("posted 5 times, sent 2 emails") without
connecting activities to outcomes. Always answer: "What did this activity achieve?"

### Mistake 2: Data Without Narrative
Presenting a wall of numbers without interpretation. The reader shouldn't have
to figure out what the numbers mean — that's the digest compiler's job.

### Mistake 3: Inconsistent Format
Changing the structure every week makes comparison impossible and frustrates
the reader. Lock in a format and stick with it.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Bad News
Skipping concerns or underperformance destroys trust. A digest that only reports
wins is not a digest — it's marketing.

### Mistake 5: Too Many Metrics
Reporting on 20+ metrics weekly creates noise. If you can't explain why each
metric is in the digest, remove it.

### Mistake 6: No Comparison Period
"We got 1,000 visitors" means nothing. "We got 1,000 visitors, up 15% from
last week and 5% above our weekly target" tells a story.

### Mistake 7: Vague Action Items
"Improve engagement" is not an action item. "Test carousel format on LinkedIn
with 3 posts next week" is.

### Mistake 8: Including Metrics That Don't Change Weekly
Domain authority, search rankings for stable terms, and other slow-moving
metrics don't belong in a weekly report. Save them for monthly reviews.

## 9. Templates and Patterns

### Minimal Weekly Digest Template

```markdown
# Weekly Digest — Week of [DATE]

## Executive Summary
[2-3 sentence overview: overall assessment + biggest win + biggest concern]

## Key Metrics

| Metric | This Week | Last Week | Change | Target |
|--------|-----------|-----------|--------|--------|
| [KPI 1] | X | Y | +/-% | Z |
| [KPI 2] | X | Y | +/-% | Z |
| [KPI 3] | X | Y | +/-% | Z |
| [KPI 4] | X | Y | +/-% | Z |
| [KPI 5] | X | Y | +/-% | Z |

## Wins
- **[Win 1]**: [Specific achievement with number]
- **[Win 2]**: [Specific achievement with number]
- **[Win 3]**: [Specific achievement with number]

## Concerns
- **[Concern 1]**: [What happened] — [Why it matters] — [Proposed action]
- **[Concern 2]**: [What happened] — [Why it matters] — [Proposed action]

## Top Content
1. **[Title]** — [Platform] — [Key metric: value]
2. **[Title]** — [Platform] — [Key metric: value]
3. **[Title]** — [Platform] — [Key metric: value]

## Growth Snapshot
| Platform | Followers | Net Change | Notes |
|----------|-----------|------------|-------|
| LinkedIn | X | +Y | |
| Twitter | X | +Y | |
| Newsletter | X | +Y | |
| YouTube | X | +Y | |

## Active Projects
- **[Project 1]**: [Status] — Next: [milestone]
- **[Project 2]**: [Status] — Next: [milestone]

## Action Items
- [ ] [Specific action] — [Owner] — [Due date]
- [ ] [Specific action] — [Owner] — [Due date]
- [ ] [Specific action] — [Owner] — [Due date]
```

### Trend Tracking Pattern

For week-over-week trend analysis, maintain a rolling 4-week view in your
agent memory:

```
Week 1: [metric value]
Week 2: [metric value]
Week 3: [metric value]
Week 4 (current): [metric value]
4-week trend: [up/down/flat] [percentage]
```

This pattern helps surface trends that single-week comparisons miss.

### Anomaly Flagging Pattern

When a metric deviates more than 20% from its 4-week average, flag it:

```
ANOMALY: [Metric] at [value] — [X%] above/below 4-week average of [avg].
Possible cause: [hypothesis].
Recommended action: [action].
```
