## 1. Research Types: Desk vs Primary

### Desk Research (Secondary Research)

Desk research involves analyzing existing published materials rather than collecting
original data. This is the primary mode for the Deep Research skill.

**Sources include:**
- Published reports, whitepapers, and industry analyses
- News articles and press releases
- Academic papers and peer-reviewed journals
- Government data and public statistics
- Company filings, investor presentations, and earnings calls
- Social media discussions and community forums
- Podcasts, interviews, and conference talks (transcripts/summaries)

**Strengths:**
- Fast to execute — no need to recruit participants or design instruments
- Broad coverage — can survey many sources quickly
- Cost-effective — most sources are freely available online
- Good for landscape/overview questions

**Weaknesses:**
- Data may be outdated, biased, or incomplete
- Cannot answer highly specific or novel questions
- Subject to publication bias (only published findings are visible)
- Difficult to verify methodology of underlying studies

### Primary Research

Primary research involves collecting original data directly from sources.

**Methods include:**
- Interviews (structured, semi-structured, unstructured)
- Surveys and questionnaires
- Observation and ethnographic studies
- Experiments and A/B tests
- Focus groups

**When to recommend primary research to the user:**
- When desk research yields insufficient or contradictory data
- When the question is highly specific to the user's audience or niche
- When existing data is more than 2 years old in a fast-moving space
- When competitive intelligence requires direct observation

## 2. Structured Research Methodology

### The SALSA Framework

A proven methodology for structured research:

| Phase | Action | Key Questions |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| **S**earch | Systematic information gathering | What sources exist? What search terms cover the topic? |
| **A**ppraisal | Evaluate source quality | Is this source credible? Current? Relevant? |
| **L**iterature mapping | Organize and categorize findings | What themes emerge? Where are the gaps? |
| **S**ynthesis & **A**nalysis | Combine findings into insights | What does the evidence say collectively? |

### Research Process — Step by Step

#### Phase 1: Define the Research Question

Before searching, clearly articulate:

1. **The core question** — What exactly are we trying to answer?
2. **Scope boundaries** — What is in scope and out of scope?
3. **Depth level** — Quick scan (30 min), standard research (1-2 hours), or deep dive (3+ hours)?
4. **Success criteria** — What would a good answer look like?
5. **Decision context** — What decision will this research inform?

**Question quality check:**
- Is it specific enough to be answerable?
- Is it broad enough to be useful?
- Can it be broken into sub-questions?

#### Phase 2: Search Strategy

Use multiple search angles to avoid tunnel vision:

| Angle | Example Query Pattern |
|-------|----------------------|
| Direct | "[topic] overview 2025 2026" |
| Comparative | "[topic] vs [alternative]" |
| Expert opinion | "[topic] expert analysis" |
| Contrarian | "[topic] criticism problems" |
| Data-focused | "[topic] statistics data report" |
| Trend-based | "[topic] trends future predictions" |
| Industry-specific | "[topic] [industry] case study" |
| Academic | "[topic] research study findings" |

**Search best practices:**
- Use at least 3-5 different query formulations per topic
- Search for both supporting and contradicting evidence
- Prioritize recent sources (last 1-2 years) unless historical context matters
- Check specialized databases and industry-specific publications
- Look for primary sources cited in secondary articles

#### Phase 3: Source Collection and Triage

Rapidly assess whether each source merits deep reading:

1. **Skim** — Read title, abstract/summary, headings, and conclusion
2. **Assess** — Apply CRAAP test (see Section 3)
3. **Categorize** — Primary evidence, supporting context, or discard
4. **Extract** — Pull key claims, data points, and quotes
5. **Record** — Note the source URL, author, date, and relevance

**Minimum source targets by depth:**
- Quick scan: 5-8 sources
- Standard research: 10-20 sources
- Deep dive: 25-50+ sources

#### Phase 4: Analysis and Synthesis

See Section 5 for detailed synthesis techniques.

#### Phase 5: Report and Recommend

See Section 6 for structured report format.

## 3. Source Evaluation Criteria

### The CRAAP Test

Developed by librarians at California State University, Chico, the CRAAP test
evaluates five dimensions of source credibility:

#### Currency

*How recent is the information?*

| Factor | Strong Signal | Weak Signal |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Publication date | Within last 1-2 years | More than 5 years old |
| Topic velocity | Matches topic's pace of change | Outdated for fast-moving fields |
| Data freshness | Uses current statistics | Relies on old data sets |
| Updates | Source is regularly updated | Last updated years ago |

**Note:** For historical analysis or foundational concepts, older sources may
be perfectly appropriate. Currency matters most for trends, technology, and
market data.

#### Relevance

*How well does this source address the research question?*

- Does it directly address the question, or is it tangentially related?
- Is the depth appropriate (too shallow or too academic)?
- Is the audience appropriate (consumer vs professional vs academic)?
- Does it provide evidence, opinion, or both?

#### Authority

*Who created this and are they qualified?*

| Indicator | High Authority | Low Authority |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| Author credentials | Named expert, relevant degree/experience | Anonymous, no credentials listed |
| Publication | Peer-reviewed journal, established outlet | Unknown blog, no editorial process |
| Organization | Recognized institution, industry body | Unknown entity, no track record |
| Citations | Widely cited by others | No citations or references |
| Track record | History of accurate reporting | History of errors or bias |

#### Accuracy

*Is the information correct and verifiable?*

- Are claims supported by evidence or citations?
- Can the data be verified through other sources?
- Is the methodology described and sound?
- Are there obvious errors, typos, or broken links?
- Does the source acknowledge limitations?

#### Purpose

*Why was this information created?*

| Purpose | Reliability Impact |
|---------|-------------------|
| Inform/Educate | Generally reliable, check for bias |
| Report research findings | High reliability if methodology is sound |
| Persuade/Advocate | May cherry-pick data; cross-reference |
| Sell/Market | Likely biased toward product/service; use cautiously |
| Entertain | Low reliability for factual claims |
| Satire/Parody | Not a factual source |

### Beyond CRAAP: Lateral Reading

The CRAAP test evaluates a source in isolation. Lateral reading complements it:

1. **Leave the source** — Open new tabs to check what others say about it
2. **Check the author/org** — Search for their reputation independently
3. **Find the original** — If a source cites a study, go read the actual study
4. **Cross-reference claims** — Do other credible sources report the same facts?
5. **Check for corrections** — Has the source been retracted or corrected?

### Source Tier System

For quick categorization during research:

| Tier | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Tier 1** | Primary data, peer-reviewed, official | Academic journals, government data, company filings |
| **Tier 2** | Established media, expert analysis | Major publications, industry analysts, recognized experts |
| **Tier 3** | Secondary reporting, aggregation | News aggregators, blog posts citing Tier 1-2, Wikipedia |
| **Tier 4** | Opinion, anecdotal, unverified | Social media, forums, anonymous blogs, self-published |

**Usage rule:** Always have at least 2-3 Tier 1-2 sources for key claims.
Tier 3-4 sources are useful for finding leads but should not be sole evidence.

## 4. Cognitive Bias in Research

### Critical Biases to Watch For

#### During Search

| Bias | Description | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking information that supports pre-existing beliefs | Actively search for contradicting evidence; use "criticism" and "problems" queries |
| **Availability bias** | Overweighting easily found information | Use multiple search engines and databases; go beyond first-page results |
| **Anchoring bias** | Over-relying on the first piece of information found | Record first finding but deliberately seek alternatives before concluding |
| **Selection bias** | Choosing sources that are convenient rather than representative | Define source criteria before searching; include diverse source types |

#### During Analysis

| Bias | Description | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Framing effect** | Drawing different conclusions based on how information is presented | Re-frame findings in multiple ways; look at absolute vs relative numbers |
| **Survivorship bias** | Focusing on successful examples while ignoring failures | Explicitly ask: "What about cases where this failed?" |
| **Recency bias** | Overweighting recent information | Include historical context and long-term trends |
| **Authority bias** | Accepting claims because of who said them | Evaluate the argument on its merits, not just the source's reputation |
| **Bandwagon effect** | Assuming popular opinion equals correct opinion | Look for minority expert opinions and contrarian views |

#### During Synthesis

| Bias | Description | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Narrative bias** | Forcing findings into a neat story | Present contradictions and uncertainties explicitly |
| **Outcome bias** | Judging quality of analysis by whether it confirms expectations | Evaluate process quality, not just outcome alignment |
| **Hindsight bias** | Believing outcomes were predictable after the fact | Note predictions and uncertainties before outcomes are known |

### Bias Checking Protocol

Before finalizing any research report, run this checklist:

```
BIAS CHECK:
[ ] Did I search for evidence AGAINST my initial hypothesis?
[ ] Did I include sources from multiple perspectives?
[ ] Did I flag where sources disagree rather than picking a side?
[ ] Did I note the limitations of my sources?
[ ] Did I distinguish between facts, expert opinions, and my own interpretations?
[ ] Did I avoid over-relying on any single source?
[ ] Did I consider what data might be MISSING (not just what I found)?
[ ] Did I present numbers in context (not cherry-picked)?
```

## 5. Synthesis Techniques

### Technique 1: Thematic Synthesis

Best for broad topics with diverse sources.

1. **Code** — Tag each finding with themes (e.g., "market size," "competitor moves," "risk")
2. **Cluster** — Group related themes together
3. **Synthesize** — Write a narrative for each theme cluster
4. **Connect** — Identify relationships between themes

### Technique 2: Framework Synthesis

Best when an existing mental model or framework applies.

1. **Choose framework** — Select or create an analytical framework (e.g., SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, Jobs-to-Be-Done)
2. **Map findings** — Place each finding into the framework's categories
3. **Identify gaps** — Where does the framework have empty cells?
4. **Conclude** — What does the filled framework tell us?

### Technique 3: Narrative Synthesis

Best for telling a coherent story from research.

1. **Chronological ordering** — Arrange findings by timeline
2. **Causal chain** — Connect cause and effect across sources
3. **Stakeholder mapping** — Organize by who is affected and how
4. **Scenario building** — Construct possible futures based on evidence

### Technique 4: Evidence Triangulation

Best for high-stakes or contested topics.

1. **Source triangulation** — Same finding from 3+ independent sources
2. **Method triangulation** — Finding supported by different research methods
3. **Perspective triangulation** — Multiple stakeholders confirm the finding
4. **Time triangulation** — Finding is consistent across time periods

**Triangulation strength levels:**
- Strong: 3+ independent, credible sources agree
- Moderate: 2 credible sources agree, no contradictions
- Weak: Single source or contradictory evidence exists

### Technique 5: Gap Analysis

Always identify what the research did NOT find:

- What questions remain unanswered?
- Where was data insufficient or contradictory?
- What assumptions are untested?
- What would change the conclusion if it turned out differently?

## 6. Structured Report Format

### Standard Report Template

Every research report should follow this structure:

```markdown
# Research Report: [Topic]
> Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
> Requested by: [User]
> Depth: [Quick Scan / Standard / Deep Dive]
> Confidence: [HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW]

## Executive Summary
[2-3 sentence answer to the research question. Lead with the conclusion.]

## Research Question
[The specific question(s) this research addresses.]

## Key Findings
[5-7 findings, each with evidence and source attribution]

### Finding 1: [Title]
- **Evidence:** [What the data says]
- **Source:** [Where it comes from, with link]
- **Confidence:** [High/Medium/Low for this specific finding]

### Finding 2: [Title]
...

## Data Points
[Relevant numbers, statistics, benchmarks — presented in tables where possible]

| Metric | Value | Source | Date |
|--------|-------|--------|------|
| ... | ... | ... | ... |

## Perspectives and Disagreements
[Where do sources disagree? What are the different viewpoints?]

### View A: [Position]
[Evidence and proponents]

### View B: [Position]
[Evidence and proponents]

### Assessment
[Which view is better supported and why]

## Implications for the user
[How does this affect the brand, business, or personal goals?]

## Recommended Actions
[3-5 specific, actionable recommendations]

1. **[Action]** — [Why and how]
2. **[Action]** — [Why and how]
3. **[Action]** — [Why and how]

## Gaps and Limitations
[What this research did NOT answer or could not verify]

## Sources
[Numbered list of all sources consulted, with URLs]

1. [Author/Org]. "[Title]." *Publication*, Date. URL
2. ...
```

### Report Quality Checklist

Before delivering any report:

```
REPORT QUALITY CHECK:
[ ] Executive summary answers the question in 2-3 sentences
[ ] Every key finding has a cited source
[ ] Data points include source and date
[ ] Contradictory evidence is presented, not hidden
[ ] Implications are specific to the user (not generic)
[ ] Recommendations are actionable (not vague)
[ ] Gaps and limitations are honestly stated
[ ] Confidence level is justified
[ ] All sources are listed with URLs
[ ] Bias check has been completed
```

## 7. Citation Standards

### In-Text Citation Format

Use inline attribution for key claims:

- **Direct quote:** "Exact words" (Source Name, Date)
- **Paraphrased finding:** According to [Source], the market grew by 15% (link)
- **Data point:** Revenue reached $2.3B in 2025 ([Company Filing](url))
- **Multiple sources:** Several reports confirm this trend ([Source A](url); [Source B](url))

### Source List Format

In the Sources section at the end of reports:

```
1. [Author or Organization]. "[Article/Report Title]." *Publication Name*, Publication Date. [Link](URL)
2. [Author]. "[Title]." *Publication*, Date. [Link](URL)
```

**If author is unknown:**
```
3. "[Title]." *Publication*, Date. [Link](URL)
```

### Citation Rules

- Every factual claim must have a source
- Opinions must be attributed to who holds them
- Statistics must include the date they were collected/published
- If a secondary source cites a primary study, cite the primary study when possible
- Never fabricate or assume source details — if unsure, note it

## 8. Confidence Assessment Framework

### Overall Confidence Levels

| Level | Criteria | Typical Scenario |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **HIGH** | 5+ credible sources agree; data is recent and verifiable; no significant contradictions; methodology is sound | Well-studied topic with abundant data |
| **MEDIUM** | 2-4 credible sources; some data gaps; minor contradictions exist; some assumptions required | Emerging topic or limited data availability |
| **LOW** | Few sources available; significant contradictions; data is old or unverifiable; heavy reliance on opinion | Novel topic, limited research, or highly contested area |

### Per-Finding Confidence

Each key finding should also carry its own confidence indicator:

- **Verified** — Cross-referenced across 3+ independent, credible sources
- **Supported** — 1-2 credible sources with no contradicting evidence
- **Preliminary** — Single source or limited evidence; needs more investigation
- **Contested** — Sources actively disagree; present both sides

### When to Flag Uncertainty

Always explicitly flag when:
- The best available data is more than 2 years old
- Key claims come from a single source
- The source has a potential conflict of interest
- The finding contradicts conventional wisdom (could be right, but flag it)
- Sample sizes are small or methodologies are unclear

## 9. Quick-Reference Checklists

### Pre-Research Checklist

```
[ ] Research question is clearly defined
[ ] Scope and depth level are set
[ ] Brand DNA and relevant strategy docs are loaded for context
[ ] Search strategy includes multiple angles (direct, contrarian, data, expert)
[ ] Time allocation is decided
```

### During-Research Checklist

```
[ ] Using 3+ different search query formulations
[ ] Evaluating each source with CRAAP test
[ ] Recording source URLs, dates, and key extracts
[ ] Searching for contradicting evidence (not just confirming)
[ ] Tracking which sub-questions are answered and which remain open
[ ] Noting data gaps and limitations as I go
```

### Post-Research Checklist

```
[ ] Bias check completed (Section 4)
[ ] Report follows standard structure (Section 6)
[ ] Every finding has a cited source
[ ] Confidence level is assessed and justified
[ ] Implications are specific to the user's context
[ ] Recommendations are concrete and actionable
[ ] Gaps and limitations are honestly reported
[ ] All sources listed with working URLs
```

## Key Principles — Summary

1. **Start with the question, not the search.** Define what you need to know before looking for it.
2. **Search broadly, evaluate ruthlessly.** Cast a wide net, then apply strict quality filters.
3. **Seek disconfirming evidence.** The most valuable finding is often the one that challenges your assumption.
4. **Triangulate everything important.** No single source should carry a key conclusion alone.
5. **Be transparent about uncertainty.** Honest "I don't know" beats false confidence.
6. **Connect to the user's context.** Research without implications is just trivia.
7. **Cite everything.** If you can't cite it, you can't claim it.
