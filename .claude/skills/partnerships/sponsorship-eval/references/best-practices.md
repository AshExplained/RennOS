# Sponsorship Evaluation Best Practices

Reference material for the Sponsorship Evaluation skill. Covers evaluation methodology,
red flags, pricing benchmarks, and FTC compliance requirements.

---

## 1. Evaluation Scorecard Rubric

Use this weighted scorecard to assess every inbound sponsorship opportunity. Score each
dimension 1-10, multiply by its weight, and sum for a total score out of 100.

### Scoring Dimensions

| # | Criterion | Weight | What to Assess | Score Guide |
|---|-----------|--------|----------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Brand Fit** | 20% | Values alignment, tone match, reputation reinforcement. Does this sponsor align with the user's brand DNA? Would the user authentically endorse this product? | 9-10: Perfect alignment. 7-8: Strong fit, minor gaps. 5-6: Acceptable but not ideal. 3-4: Notable misalignment. 1-2: Contradicts brand values. |
| 2 | **Audience Overlap** | 25% | ICP (ideal customer profile) match. Does the sponsor's target customer overlap with the user's audience by at least 60%? Geographic, demographic, and psychographic alignment. | 9-10: 80%+ overlap. 7-8: 60-80% overlap. 5-6: 40-60% overlap. 3-4: 20-40% overlap. 1-2: Minimal overlap. |
| 3 | **Compensation** | 20% | Is the offered rate fair relative to deliverables, platform, and audience size? Compare against rate card and market benchmarks (see Section 3). | 9-10: Above market rate. 7-8: At market rate. 5-6: 10-20% below market. 3-4: 20-40% below market. 1-2: Significantly undervalued. |
| 4 | **Scope & Deliverables** | 15% | Clarity of deliverables, timeline reasonableness, revision limits, usage rights, and content approval process. Are expectations well-defined? | 9-10: Crystal clear, reasonable scope. 7-8: Clear with minor ambiguity. 5-6: Some vague terms needing clarification. 3-4: Significant ambiguity. 1-2: Undefined or unreasonable scope. |
| 5 | **Exclusivity Terms** | 10% | Duration, category breadth, and geographic scope of exclusivity. Does exclusivity come with appropriate premium compensation (30-50% above non-exclusive)? | 9-10: No exclusivity or well-compensated. 7-8: Narrow exclusivity, fair premium. 5-6: Moderate exclusivity, some premium. 3-4: Broad exclusivity, underpaid. 1-2: Unrestricted exclusivity, no premium. |
| 6 | **Creative Control** | 10% | How much freedom does the user retain over messaging, tone, and format? Are revision rounds capped (standard: 2-3)? Can the user decline if final product feels inauthentic? | 9-10: Full creative control. 7-8: Guidelines with flexibility. 5-6: Moderate brand restrictions. 3-4: Heavy scripting required. 1-2: Zero creative freedom. |

### Score Interpretation

| Total Score | Recommendation | Action |
|-------------|---------------|--------|
| 80-100 | **ACCEPT** | Strong opportunity. Proceed to contract review. |
| 65-79 | **NEGOTIATE** | Good potential but terms need improvement. Identify specific areas to push on. |
| 50-64 | **CONDITIONAL** | Only pursue if key terms can be significantly improved. |
| Below 50 | **DECLINE** | Does not meet minimum threshold. Draft polite decline. |

### Hard Filters (Pre-Scorecard)

Before scoring, apply these pass/fail filters. If any fail, skip scoring and decline:

- Audience alignment below 40% overlap with the user's target demographic
- Sponsor is in a category that directly conflicts with existing exclusive partnerships
- Sponsor has active public controversy or ethical violations
- Timeline is unreasonable (less than 7 days for complex deliverables)
- Payment terms exceed Net-60 without justification
- Sponsor requests perpetual/unlimited usage rights without appropriate compensation

## 2. Red Flags to Watch For

### Contract Red Flags

- **Unrestricted usage rights** -- Allows permanent reuse of your content across any channel without additional compensation. Always negotiate time-limited usage windows.
- **No payment deadline specified** -- Contract should state specific payment terms (Net-30 is standard). Vague terms like "upon completion" invite delays.
- **Overly broad exclusivity** -- Exclusivity that covers an entire product category, long timeframes (6+ months), or multiple geographies without premium compensation (30-50% above non-exclusive rates).
- **Unilateral termination** -- Brand can exit at any time, but creator is locked in. Termination should be mutual with defined notice periods and kill fees.
- **Vague scope of work** -- Phrases like "promote across your content" or "ongoing support" without specific deliverables, format, platforms, or posting schedule.
- **Unlimited revision rounds** -- Standard is 2-3 rounds. Unlimited revisions signal a brand that will micromanage.
- **No content approval process** -- Should define clear approval deadlines (3-5 business days typical) so content is not held indefinitely.

### Deal Structure Red Flags

- **Rushed timelines** -- "We need an answer today" or "Offer expires in 2 hours" suggests the brand does not respect your business. Legitimate partners allow time for counsel review.
- **Upfront payment requests** -- Legitimate brands never ask creators to pay to participate. If they ask for money, it is a scam.
- **Product-only compensation** -- Free product exchanges are only acceptable for new creators building portfolios. Established creators should always receive monetary compensation.
- **Accepting underpriced deals** -- Sets a precedent. Brands that lowball once will continue to do so.
- **Performance-only compensation** -- Affiliate-only or commission-only deals shift all risk to the creator. Require a base fee plus performance bonuses.

### Brand & Reputation Red Flags

- **Poor customer reviews or recent scandals** -- Research the brand thoroughly before associating.
- **Unethical business practices** -- Environmental, labor, or data privacy violations.
- **Products potentially harmful to audience** -- Health, financial, or safety concerns.
- **Unprofessional communication** -- Gmail or free email accounts instead of @brandname.com domains.
- **No verifiable web presence** -- Hastily created social accounts, no real website.
- **History of non-payment to other creators** -- Check creator communities and forums.

### Audience Quality Red Flags (When Evaluating the Sponsor's Claims)

- **Sudden follower spikes** -- Indicates bot growth or purchased followers.
- **Flat engagement with high follower count** -- Fake audience signals.
- **Bot-pattern comments** -- Generic emoji-only or irrelevant comments.
- **Audience geo-mismatch** -- Their claimed audience location does not match reality.
- **Refusal to share analytics** -- Legitimate partners are transparent about their metrics.

### Rate Modifiers

Apply these adjustments to base rates:

| Factor | Modifier |
|--------|----------|
| Exclusivity (category) | +30-50% |
| Whitelisting / paid amplification rights | +20-40% |
| Usage rights beyond 30 days | +15-30% per additional 30-day period |
| Rush delivery (under 7 days) | +25-50% |
| Multi-platform bundle | -10-20% discount |
| Long-term partnership (3+ months) | -10-15% per deliverable |
| Performance bonus (on top of base) | +10-20% of base if KPIs hit |

### Pricing Calculation Methods

1. **CPM Method**: (Expected Reach / 1,000) x $3-$5 CPM = base rate
2. **Engagement Method**: Total Engagements x $0.10-$0.50 = engagement-based rate
3. **Conversion Method**: Track conversions generated; request 10-20% of attributed sales value

**Key benchmark**: Creators with 10K-50K followers achieve 60% higher engagement rates
than those with 1M+ followers, making mid-tier creators often the best value.

## 4. FTC Disclosure Requirements for Sponsorships

Non-compliance exposes both the user and the sponsor to legal liability. Every sponsorship
evaluation must confirm the brand's willingness to support proper FTC disclosure.

### What Requires Disclosure

Any **material connection** between a creator and a brand must be disclosed. This includes:

- Monetary payments (flat fee, commission, performance bonus)
- Free products or services
- Discounts, gifts, or trips
- Family or employment relationships
- Any benefit received in exchange for coverage or endorsement

**Rule of thumb**: If any reasonable viewer might not expect the connection exists,
disclose it.

### Disclosure Language Requirements

- Must use **clear, unambiguous language** -- not vague shorthand
- Acceptable: #ad, #sponsored, #advertisement, "Paid partnership with [Brand]"
- **Not acceptable**: #sp, #spon, #collab (too vague), #ambassador (unclear)
- Built-in platform partnership tools (e.g., Instagram's "Paid partnership" label)
  should be used but are **not sufficient alone** -- additional clear disclosure is required

### Placement Rules by Platform

| Platform | Requirements |
|----------|-------------|
| **Instagram/Facebook Feed** | Disclosure above the "View More" fold. Clear hashtag (#ad) at the beginning of the caption, not buried among other hashtags. |
| **Instagram/Facebook Stories** | Superimposed text clearly visible on the image/video. Must remain on screen long enough to read. Use both the platform's paid partnership label AND text overlay. |
| **Instagram Reels / TikTok** | Verbal disclosure at the start ("This video is sponsored by [Brand]") PLUS written text overlay visible throughout. |
| **YouTube** | Verbal disclosure within the first 30 seconds. Written disclosure in the video description above the fold. Use YouTube's paid promotion checkbox. |
| **Live Streams** | Disclosure must be repeated verbally AND displayed on-screen periodically, not just at the start. |
| **Podcasts** | Verbal disclosure at the beginning AND during the sponsored segment. Written disclosure in the episode description. |
| **Twitter/X** | #ad or "Sponsored by [Brand]" within the tweet itself, not in a reply or follow-up tweet. |
| **Blog Posts** | Clear disclosure at the top of the post, before any sponsored content. |

### Key Compliance Rules

1. **Proximity**: Disclosure must be immediately adjacent to the endorsement, not separated by scrolling, clicking, or navigating.
2. **Visibility**: Must stand out from surrounding content. Same font size and color, not hidden or obscured.
3. **Permanence**: Must remain visible for the life of the content. Cannot be removed after a campaign ends.
4. **Repetition**: In long-form or live content, disclosure must be repeated -- not just stated once at the beginning.
5. **AI-generated content**: If AI tools, avatars, or generated voices are used in sponsored content, both the sponsorship AND the AI involvement must be disclosed (2025 update).

### Shared Liability

As of 2025, the FTC holds **all parties** equally responsible:
- The **creator** who posts the content
- The **brand** that paid for it
- Any **agency or intermediary** that facilitated the deal

A brand that does not require or support proper disclosure is a red flag.

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

- **Civil penalties**: Up to **$53,088 per violation** (2025 rate, adjusted annually for inflation). Each individual post can constitute a separate violation.
- **Enforcement actions**: FTC warning letters, consent orders, injunctions
- **Financial remedies**: Disgorgement of profits (e.g., Teami LLC returned $930,000+)
- **Reputational damage**: Loss of audience trust, future brand contract opportunities
- **Brand liability**: Brands can face separate penalties for failing to ensure creator compliance

### What to Confirm with Every Sponsor

Before accepting any deal, verify:

- [ ] Brand acknowledges FTC disclosure requirements in the contract
- [ ] Contract does not prohibit or discourage proper disclosure
- [ ] Brand will not request removal of disclosure after campaign ends
- [ ] Content approval process does not penalize inclusion of disclosure
- [ ] Brand provides guidance or is open to discussion on disclosure format
