# Deal Structure — Best Practices Reference

> Methodology guide for structuring, pricing, and negotiating partnership deals.
> Read this before structuring any deal to ground decisions in proven frameworks.

---

## 1. Deal Term Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Deliverables** | Specific outputs a party agrees to produce (content, mentions, features, access, IP) |
| **Exclusivity** | Clause restricting either party from working with competitors during the deal period |
| **IP Rights** | Intellectual property ownership — who owns content created, repurposing rights, usage licenses |
| **Usage Rights** | The scope of how, where, and for how long a brand can use creator-produced content |
| **Morality Clause** | Provision allowing termination if either party engages in behavior that damages the other's reputation |
| **Indemnification** | Contractual protection where one party agrees to compensate the other for certain losses or damages |
| **Termination Clause** | Conditions under which either party can end the agreement early, including notice periods |
| **Revenue Share** | Percentage split of revenue generated from the partnership |
| **Commission** | Percentage-based payment earned on sales driven through tracked attribution (links, codes) |
| **Retainer** | Recurring fixed payment (monthly/quarterly) for ongoing services or availability |
| **Flat Fee** | One-time fixed payment for defined deliverables regardless of performance |
| **Performance Bonus** | Additional payment triggered when agreed-upon metrics are met or exceeded |
| **Kill Fee** | Payment owed if a project is cancelled after work has begun but before completion |
| **Rate Card** | Standardized pricing document listing fees for different deliverable types |
| **Anchor Price** | The initial price quoted in negotiation, which sets the reference point for all subsequent discussion |
| **Reservation Price** | The least favorable deal you will accept before walking away — your true bottom line |
| **BATNA** | Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — your optimal fallback if the deal fails |
| **ZOPA** | Zone of Possible Agreement — the range between both parties' reservation prices where a deal can happen |
| **Walk-Away Point** | The threshold below which you reject the deal and pursue your BATNA |
| **Concession** | Something you give up during negotiation to move toward agreement |
| **Non-Compete** | Clause preventing work with direct competitors for a specified period |
| **Right of First Refusal** | Partner's right to match any competing offer before you accept it |
| **Net vs. Gross Revenue** | Net = after deductions (platform fees, taxes, refunds); Gross = total before deductions |
| **Content License** | Permission to use content under defined terms (duration, platforms, modifications) vs. full ownership transfer |
| **FTC Disclosure** | Legal requirement to clearly mark sponsored content as advertising (e.g., #ad, #sponsored) |
| **SOW (Statement of Work)** | Detailed document specifying exact deliverables, timelines, and acceptance criteria |

## 2. Common Deal Structures by Type

### 2.1 Sponsorship (Flat Fee)

**What it is:** Brand pays a fixed amount for specific, defined deliverables. Transactional relationship — payment for promotional benefit.

**Typical terms:**
- Fixed fee per deliverable (e.g., one video, one post, one newsletter mention)
- 50% upfront / 50% on delivery (standard), or 100% within 30 days of content going live
- Usage rights limited to campaign duration and specified platforms
- Creator retains underlying IP; brand receives a license to use
- Category exclusivity during campaign window (with premium for broader exclusivity)
- Late payment penalties (1.5% per month is standard)

**Best for:** One-off brand awareness campaigns, product launches, reusable UGC.

**Key protections:**
- Specific deliverables — never accept vague language like "ongoing support"
- Defined usage rights with duration, platforms, and modification limits
- Termination-for-cause provisions on both sides
- Kill fee if brand cancels after work begins

### 2.2 Affiliate

**What it is:** Performance-based deal. Creator earns commission on sales driven through tracked links or discount codes. No upfront payment (pure affiliate) or small base + commission (hybrid).

**Typical terms:**
- Commission rates: 5–30% depending on product margin and creator leverage
- Tracking via unique links, promo codes, or UTM parameters
- Payment on net sales (after returns/refunds), typically monthly
- Cookie window: 7–90 days (longer = more valuable to creator)
- No exclusivity required (or lighter exclusivity)
- Minimum payout thresholds may apply

**Best for:** Testing new partnerships with low risk, performance-driven campaigns, scaling with attribution.

**Key protections:**
- Transparent attribution methodology and real-time tracking access
- Clearly defined commission base (net vs. gross)
- Minimum guaranteed payment or floor to protect creator investment

### 2.3 Licensing

**What it is:** Creator licenses existing content, likeness, or IP to a brand for defined use. Payment for usage rights rather than new content creation.

**Typical terms:**
- License fee (flat or royalty-based)
- Defined scope: platforms, territories, duration, modifications allowed
- Exclusivity level: exclusive vs. non-exclusive license
- Creator retains ownership; brand gets usage rights only
- Renewal terms and rate escalation for extensions
- Approval rights over how content is used or modified

**Best for:** Repurposing existing high-performing content, brand ads using creator likeness, long-term brand campaigns.

**Key protections:**
- Time-limited license (avoid perpetual grants)
- Approval rights over modifications and context of use
- Separate pricing for each additional platform or territory
- Reversion clause if brand fails to use within agreed period

### 2.4 Collaboration / Co-Creation

**What it is:** Both parties invest resources toward a shared outcome. Deeper than sponsorship — involves co-ownership, shared risk, and shared upside.

**Typical terms:**
- Revenue share (define net revenue waterfall: gross - platform fees - taxes - refunds = net)
- Co-ownership of created IP (or clearly defined split)
- Shared decision-making on creative direction
- Longer-term commitment (6–24 months)
- Joint KPIs and reporting cadence
- Exit/dissolution terms including IP division

**Best for:** Product co-creation, joint ventures, long-term strategic partnerships.

**Key protections:**
- Crystal-clear IP ownership split documented before work begins
- Defined exit strategy with asset division terms
- No personal guarantees of either party's assets
- Dispute resolution mechanism (mediation before litigation)
- Regular financial reporting and audit rights

### 2.5 Retainer / Ambassador

**What it is:** Recurring monthly/quarterly payment for ongoing content, promotion, and brand representation. Creator functions as an extension of the brand team.

**Typical terms:**
- Monthly retainer fee with defined deliverable minimums
- Typically 30–50% premium over equivalent one-off deal rates
- Category exclusivity (standard for ambassadors)
- 3–12 month minimum commitment
- Tiered performance bonuses on top of base retainer
- First-look or right-of-first-refusal on new campaigns
- Regular check-ins and reporting cadence

**Best for:** Always-on brand presence, long-term partnerships, established top performers.

**Key protections:**
- Minimum commitment period with mutual opt-out windows
- Defined deliverable scope (prevent scope creep)
- Rate review at renewal — pricing should escalate with growth
- Clear brand guidelines and approval workflow
- Termination provisions with reasonable notice (30–90 days)

### 2.6 Hybrid (Base + Performance)

**What it is:** Combines guaranteed base payment with performance upside. Balances risk between both parties.

**Typical terms:**
- Base fee (flat or retainer) + commission/bonus structure
- Example: $500 base + 20% commission, or $200 base + $500 bonus at $10K GMV
- Performance metrics defined upfront (sales, impressions, clicks, sign-ups)
- Attribution methodology agreed before launch
- Payment schedule: base on delivery, performance monthly or quarterly

**Best for:** Mid-tier partnerships, scaling proven creators, product launches with measurable goals.

**Key protections:**
- Minimum guaranteed compensation regardless of performance
- Transparent, real-time performance tracking
- Cap on deliverable obligations (prevent unlimited work for base fee)
- Clear escalation path if performance significantly exceeds expectations

## 3. BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement

### 3.1 What BATNA Is

Developed by Roger Fisher and William Ury in *Getting to Yes* (1981), BATNA is your optimal fallback if the current negotiation fails. It is the single most important piece of information in any negotiation because it determines your true leverage.

> "A BATNA is only as strong as your understanding of it; misjudging your alternative can lead you to reject a favorable agreement or accept a worse one."
> — Harvard Program on Negotiation

### 3.2 How to Determine Your BATNA

**Four-step process:**

1. **Define your purpose** — What do you actually need from this deal? (Revenue? Exposure? Credibility? Content?)
2. **List all realistic alternatives** — Other potential partners, self-funded alternatives, doing nothing. Be honest — hypothetical alternatives are not a BATNA.
3. **Evaluate each option across multiple dimensions** — Price, timing, reputational risk, opportunity cost, strategic value, relationship potential.
4. **Select the strongest alternative** — This becomes your BATNA and determines your reservation price (walk-away point).

### 3.3 Using BATNA in Negotiation

- **Sets your walk-away point:** Never accept a deal worse than your BATNA
- **Builds confidence:** Knowing your alternative eliminates desperation
- **Creates leverage:** A strong BATNA means you negotiate from strength without aggressive tactics
- **Dynamic reassessment:** Continuously update your BATNA as negotiations progress and new information emerges

### 3.4 BATNA and ZOPA

Your BATNA determines your reservation price (the least favorable deal you will accept). The other party's BATNA determines theirs. The overlap between these two reservation prices is the ZOPA — the Zone of Possible Agreement. If no overlap exists, no deal is possible, and both parties should pursue their BATNAs.

### 3.5 Common BATNA Mistakes

| Mistake | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Overestimating your BATNA | Reject good deals, appear unreasonable |
| Not having a BATNA at all | Negotiate from desperation, accept bad terms |
| Revealing your BATNA too early | Gives the other party your floor — they anchor to it |
| Treating BATNA as static | Miss opportunities as conditions change |
| Surface-level comparison | Overlook hidden differences (coverage, scope, quality) |

## 4. Anchoring Strategy

### 4.1 The Anchoring Effect

The first number mentioned in any negotiation exerts disproportionate influence on the entire discussion. This is a well-documented cognitive bias — the anchor becomes the reference point against which all subsequent numbers are evaluated.

### 4.2 How to Anchor Effectively

**Make the first offer.** Research consistently shows that the party who makes the first offer achieves better outcomes, because they set the reference frame.

**Anchor high, not unreasonable.** Your opening position should be:
- Intentionally above your target price (to create negotiation room)
- Justifiable with data (market rates, rate card, comparable deals)
- Within the range of reason (not so high it kills credibility)

**The three-position model:**
1. **Open Position** — Aspirational. Sets the ceiling and frames the conversation. Start 20–40% above your target depending on deal type.
2. **Target Position** — Your desired outcome. Aligned with your value proposition and market rates.
3. **Walk-Away Position** — The worst acceptable deal. Determined before negotiation begins, not under pressure.

### 4.3 Defending Against Anchoring

When the other party anchors first:
- Do not counter-anchor reactively — pause and reframe
- Present your own independent analysis and data
- Name the tactic: "I see you've set an anchor — let me share what the market data shows"
- Never negotiate against yourself by lowering your price without receiving something in return

## 5. Concession Strategy

### 5.1 Core Principles

Concessions are a negotiation tool, not a sign of weakness. Managed correctly, they build goodwill and move toward agreement without sacrificing core interests.

**Rules of concession:**
1. **Never concede without receiving something in return** — Every concession should be a trade, not a gift
2. **Make concessions progressively smaller** — Signals you are approaching your limit
3. **Concede on low-value items to you, high-value items to them** — Maximize perceived generosity at minimal cost
4. **Never split the difference first** — Let the other party propose compromises
5. **Label your concessions** — Make sure the other party recognizes the value of what you gave up

### 5.2 Concession Planning

Before negotiation, categorize all deal terms into three tiers:

| Tier | Description | Action |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Non-negotiable** | Core terms you will not bend on (minimum rate, IP ownership, brand alignment) | Refuse. Explain why. |
| **Tradeable** | Terms you can flex on if you get something back (timeline, exclusivity scope, usage duration) | Trade strategically. |
| **Giveaways** | Low-cost items that feel valuable to the other party (extra social mention, extended content license, meet-and-greet) | Concede these to build goodwill. |

### 5.3 Concession Sequencing

- Open with your strongest position
- Make your first concession on a giveaway tier item — establishes reciprocity
- Pause between concessions — rapid concessions signal desperation
- Decrease concession size with each round
- Tie significant concessions to significant reciprocal moves

## 6. Negotiation Psychology

### 6.1 Key Psychological Principles

**Reciprocity:** People feel obligated to return favors. When you make a concession, the other party feels pressure to reciprocate. Use this deliberately by conceding on low-cost items first.

**Loss Aversion:** People feel losses more intensely than equivalent gains. Frame your proposals in terms of what the partner gains, and frame alternatives in terms of what they lose by not agreeing.

**Anchoring Bias:** The first number mentioned dominates the frame. Always aim to set the anchor. If they anchor first, explicitly reframe before responding.

**Commitment & Consistency:** Once someone agrees to a small commitment, they are more likely to agree to larger ones. Start with easy agreements to build momentum.

**Social Proof:** Reference comparable deals and market rates to legitimize your position. "This is standard in the industry" is a powerful frame.

**Scarcity:** Limited availability increases perceived value. Communicate genuine constraints — limited calendar slots, exclusive partnerships, competing offers — to create urgency.

**The Contrast Principle:** Present your target offer after a more expensive option. The target looks more reasonable by comparison.

### 6.2 Emotional Discipline

- Never negotiate when emotional — pause, regroup, respond later
- Separate the relationship from the substance (Fisher & Ury's core principle)
- Ask questions rather than making statements when tension rises
- Silence is a negotiation tool — use it after making an offer

### 6.3 Information Asymmetry

The party with more information has more power. Before any negotiation:
- Research the partner's BATNA (what are their alternatives to you?)
- Understand their budget cycle and decision timeline
- Know your market value from multiple data points
- Identify their true decision-maker

## 7. Negotiation Checklist — Pre-Deal

Use this checklist before entering any deal negotiation:

- [ ] Rate card reviewed — know your standard pricing
- [ ] Market rates researched — know what comparable creators/partners charge
- [ ] BATNA identified — know your best alternative if this deal fails
- [ ] Reservation price set — know your absolute minimum
- [ ] Anchor price prepared — know your opening ask (20–40% above target)
- [ ] Non-negotiables listed — know what you will not bend on
- [ ] Concession tiers mapped — know what you can trade and what you can give away
- [ ] Partner research done — understand their alternatives, budget, and timeline
- [ ] Brand alignment verified — deal aligns with Brand DNA
- [ ] Legal review items flagged — IP, exclusivity, termination, indemnification

## 8. Deal Structure Decision Matrix

| Scenario | Recommended Structure | Why |
|----------|----------------------|-----|
| Unknown partner, first deal | Flat fee or affiliate | Low risk, test the relationship |
| Proven partner, scaling up | Retainer or hybrid | Reward consistency, incentivize growth |
| High-value brand, long-term | Retainer + performance bonus | Income stability + upside |
| Product co-creation | Collaboration (revenue share) | Shared risk and reward |
| Content repurposing request | Licensing | Monetize existing assets without new work |
| Tight budget partner, high potential | Hybrid (small base + commission) | Balance risk while maintaining motivation |
| Speaking or event appearance | Flat fee + expenses | Clear scope, defined deliverable |
| Ongoing consulting or advisory | Retainer | Predictable income, defined availability |
